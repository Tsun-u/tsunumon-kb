# A.2: Forces and Momentum

> Theme A: Space, Time and Motion | SL + HL

## Key Concepts

### Newton's Laws of Motion
- **First law (inertia)**: an object keeps its current state of motion—whether stationary or moving at constant velocity—until a net external force acts on it
- **Second law**: $\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$ — the net force on an object equals its mass multiplied by the acceleration it undergoes
- **Third law**: whenever one object exerts a force on another, the second object pushes back with an equal force in the opposite direction; these paired forces always act on different objects

### Types of Forces
- **Weight**: $W = mg$ — the gravitational pull exerted on an object by Earth (or another massive body)
- **Normal force** ($F_N$): a contact force directed perpendicular to the surface supporting the object
- **Friction**: a force that resists relative sliding between surfaces in contact
  - Static friction: $f_s \leq \mu_s F_N$
  - Kinetic friction: $f_k = \mu_k F_N$
- **Tension**: the pulling force that a rope, string, or cable transmits along its length
- **Air resistance / drag**: a force that acts opposite to an object's velocity when moving through a fluid medium
- **Spring force**: $F = -kx$ (Hooke's law)

### Free-Body Diagrams
- Isolate a single object and draw every force acting on it—nothing else
- Each force arrow originates from the object's center of mass
- The vector sum of all forces gives the net force, which determines the object's acceleration

### Circular Motion (SL + HL)
- Centripetal acceleration: $a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$
- Centripetal force: $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$
- Angular velocity: $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f$
- Applications: banking, orbits, vertical circles

### Momentum
- Linear momentum: $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$
- Newton's second law (momentum form): $\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$
- Impulse: $J = F\Delta t = \Delta p$
- **Conservation of momentum**: in a system with no net external force, the total momentum before and after any interaction stays the same
  - $m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = m_1 v_1' + m_2 v_2'$
- Types of collisions:
  - **Elastic**: kinetic energy is conserved alongside momentum
  - **Inelastic**: momentum is conserved but some kinetic energy converts to other forms
  - **Perfectly inelastic**: the colliding objects merge and move together after impact

### Fluid Resistance and Terminal Velocity
- Drag force grows as an object moves faster through a fluid
- Terminal velocity is reached when the drag force exactly balances the object's weight ($F_{drag} = mg$), so the net force—and therefore acceleration—drops to zero
- Qualitative treatment at SL; quantitative aspects at HL

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Centripetal force is a separate type of force"** -- It is the net inward force (gravity, tension, friction, etc.) causing circular motion.
2. **"A moving object must have a force in the direction of motion"** -- Newton's first law: no net force is needed for constant velocity.
3. **"Action and reaction cancel out"** -- They act on different objects and therefore do not cancel.
4. **"Heavier objects are harder to stop because they have more momentum"** -- Correct, but this is because $p = mv$, not because of some inherent resistance beyond mass.
