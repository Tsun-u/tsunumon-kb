# E.1: Structure of the Atom

> Theme E: Nuclear and Quantum Physics | SL + HL

## Key Concepts

### Atomic Models
- **Thomson model** (plum pudding): electrons embedded within a diffuse, uniform positive charge distribution
- **Rutherford model**: a compact, positively charged nucleus at the centre; electrons occupy the surrounding space
  - Evidence: alpha particle scattering experiment (Geiger-Marsden)
  - The vast majority of alpha particles passed straight through → most of the atom's volume is empty
  - A small fraction scattered through large angles → the nucleus is tiny, massive, and positively charged
- **Bohr model**: electrons restricted to fixed circular orbits, each corresponding to a specific energy
  - Only certain orbits are permitted; electrons cannot exist between these allowed levels
  - A photon is emitted or absorbed whenever an electron moves between two energy levels

### Energy Levels
- Atomic electrons are confined to a discrete set of allowed energies — the energy is quantized
- **Ground state**: the configuration in which the electron occupies the lowest available energy level
- **Excited state**: any configuration in which an electron has been promoted to a higher energy level
- Energy of emitted/absorbed photon: $E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$
  - $h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s (Planck's constant)
- **Emission spectra**: photons are released as electrons drop to lower energy levels, producing bright lines at wavelengths unique to each element
- **Absorption spectra**: incident photons of particular wavelengths are absorbed by electrons, leaving dark lines at those wavelengths
- Hydrogen energy levels: $E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2}$ eV

### Nuclear Structure
- **Protons**: carry a single positive charge ($+e$), each with mass ≈ 1 u
- **Neutrons**: electrically neutral particles, each with mass ≈ 1 u
- **Atomic number** ($Z$): the count of protons in the nucleus, which determines the element
- **Mass number** ($A$): the total number of protons and neutrons combined
- **Isotopes**: atoms of the same element that differ in neutron count, and therefore in mass
- Notation: $^A_Z X$ (e.g., $^{12}_6 C$)
- **Unified atomic mass unit**: $1 \text{ u} = 1.661 \times 10^{-27}$ kg = 931.5 MeV/c²

### Nuclear Forces
- **Strong nuclear force**: operates only at very short range (~1 fm), binding nucleons within the nucleus
  - Provides an attractive force large enough to counteract the electrostatic repulsion among protons
  - Affects protons and neutrons equally, regardless of charge
- Nuclear stability arises from the competition between the attractive strong force and the repulsive electrostatic force between protons

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Electrons orbit the nucleus like planets"** -- The Bohr model is a useful historical stepping stone, not a complete picture. Quantum mechanics replaces fixed orbits with probability distributions that describe where an electron is likely to be found.
2. **"Atoms are solid spheres"** -- An atom is overwhelmingly empty space; the nucleus occupies only a tiny fraction of the atom's total volume.
3. **"All atoms of an element are identical"** -- While all atoms of an element share the same atomic number, isotopes differ in neutron count and therefore have distinct masses.
4. **"Continuous spectra and line spectra are the same"** -- Dense, hot objects emit a continuous spectrum spanning all wavelengths, whereas low-pressure excited gases emit only specific discrete wavelengths, producing a characteristic line spectrum.
