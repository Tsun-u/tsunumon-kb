## Key Concepts

### Photoelectric Effect
- When light strikes a metal surface, electrons may be released from the metal
- Experimental results that classical wave theory fails to account for:
  - No electrons are released when the incident light is below a characteristic threshold frequency ($f_0$), no matter how bright the source
  - The maximum kinetic energy of released electrons is determined solely by the light's frequency, not its intensity
  - Electron emission begins without any measurable time delay after illumination
- **Einstein's explanation**: light is quantised into discrete packets called photons, each carrying energy $E = hf$
  - $E_k^{max} = hf - \phi$ (photoelectric equation)
  - $\phi = hf_0$: work function — the minimum energy required to liberate one electron from the metal surface
  - Stopping potential: $eV_s = E_k^{max}$

### Wave-Particle Duality
- Light demonstrates behaviour characteristic of both waves and particles, depending on the experiment
- **de Broglie hypothesis**: wave-like behaviour is not unique to light — every moving particle possesses an associated wavelength
  - de Broglie wavelength: $\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$
- Experimental support: electrons passing through a crystal lattice produce diffraction and interference patterns, just as waves do
- Wave behaviour becomes detectable only when $\lambda$ is of a similar magnitude to the dimensions of the diffracting structure or aperture

### Uncertainty Principle
- **Heisenberg uncertainty principle**:
  - $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$
  - There is an irreducible trade-off between how precisely a particle's position and momentum can be defined at the same time
  - $\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$
- This trade-off is not caused by imperfect instruments; it reflects an intrinsic feature of quantum systems independent of any measurement apparatus

### Quantum Tunneling
- A quantum particle may penetrate and cross a potential energy barrier even when its total energy is less than the barrier height — something that Newtonian mechanics forbids
- The likelihood of tunneling falls off exponentially as the barrier becomes thicker or taller
- Real-world examples include: thermonuclear fusion inside stars, the scanning tunnelling microscope (STM), and tunnel diodes in electronics

### Matter Waves and the Bohr Model
- Standing wave condition: $2\pi r = n\lambda$ (an orbiting electron must fit a whole number of de Broglie wavelengths around its circular path to form a stable standing wave)
- Quantization of angular momentum: $L = n\frac{h}{2\pi}$
- This standing-wave constraint naturally restricts electrons to discrete orbital radii, which is why atoms possess only specific, permitted energy levels

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"The photoelectric effect proves light is a particle"** -- The photoelectric effect demonstrates that light can behave like a particle; however, phenomena such as interference and diffraction demonstrate its wave nature. Light is neither exclusively a wave nor a particle — it exhibits both characters.
2. **"The uncertainty principle is about measurement error"** -- The Heisenberg uncertainty principle is not a statement about the precision of our instruments. It describes an inherent quantum indeterminacy that persists even with perfect, idealised measurements.
3. **"Only small particles have wave properties"** -- Every material object has a de Broglie wavelength associated with its momentum. For everyday macroscopic objects, this wavelength is so extraordinarily tiny that wave behaviour is completely undetectable.
4. **"Quantum tunneling violates conservation of energy"** -- Conservation of energy holds throughout a tunneling event; the particle carries identical energy on both sides of the barrier, with no energy created or destroyed.
