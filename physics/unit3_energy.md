# Unit 3: Work, Energy, and Power

> Exam Weight: 18--23% | ~22--27 Class Periods

## Topics

### 3.1 Translational Kinetic Energy
- Kinetic energy: energy of motion
- $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
- Scalar quantity, always non-negative
- Depends on speed (not velocity); frame-dependent

### 3.2 Work
- Work done by a constant force: $W = Fd\cos\theta$
- $\theta$ is the angle between the force and displacement vectors
- Work is a scalar; can be positive, negative, or zero
- Positive work: force has component in direction of displacement (transfers energy to object)
- Negative work: force has component opposite to displacement (removes energy from object)
- Zero work: force perpendicular to displacement (e.g., normal force on flat surface, centripetal force)
- Work-energy theorem: $W_{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i$
- Net work equals the change in kinetic energy

### 3.3 Potential Energy
- Gravitational potential energy: $U_g = mgy$ (near Earth's surface, with chosen reference level)
- Elastic (spring) potential energy: $U_s = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2$
- Potential energy is associated with a system, not a single object
- Only changes in potential energy are physically meaningful; reference point is arbitrary
- Conservative forces: work done is path-independent (gravity, spring force)
- Non-conservative forces: work done is path-dependent (friction, air resistance)

### 3.4 Conservation of Energy
- Total mechanical energy: $E = K + U$
- In an isolated system with only conservative forces: $K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f$
- With non-conservative forces (e.g., friction): $K_i + U_i + W_{nc} = K_f + U_f$
- Energy is never created or destroyed; it can be transformed or transferred
- Friction converts mechanical energy to thermal energy (internal energy)
- Energy bar charts (LOL diagrams) are useful visual representations

### 3.5 Power
- Average power: $P_{avg} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \frac{W}{\Delta t}$
- Instantaneous power: $P = Fv\cos\theta$
- For constant force in direction of motion: $P = Fv$
- Power is the rate of energy transfer or transformation

## Learning Objectives

- Calculate kinetic energy for translational motion
- Determine work done by constant forces, including at angles
- Apply the work-energy theorem to relate net work to changes in kinetic energy
- Calculate gravitational and elastic potential energy
- Apply conservation of energy to solve problems with and without non-conservative forces
- Use energy bar charts to represent energy transformations
- Calculate power as the rate of energy transfer
- Identify when mechanical energy is or is not conserved

## Essential Knowledge

1. Kinetic energy is associated with the motion of an object and depends on mass and speed
2. Work is the mechanism by which energy is transferred to or from a system via a force acting through a displacement
3. The work-energy theorem states that net work equals the change in kinetic energy
4. Potential energy is stored energy associated with the configuration of a system (position)
5. In a closed system with no non-conservative forces, mechanical energy is conserved
6. When friction or other non-conservative forces act, mechanical energy decreases (converted to thermal energy)
7. Power measures the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred

## Key Equations

### Kinetic Energy

$$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

### Work

$$W = Fd\cos\theta$$

$$W_{net} = \Delta K$$

### Potential Energy

$$U_g = mgy$$

$$U_s = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2$$

### Conservation of Energy

$$K_i + U_i + W_{nc} = K_f + U_f$$

$$\Delta E_{system} = 0 \quad \text{(isolated system)}$$

### Power

$$P_{avg} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \frac{W}{\Delta t}$$

$$P = Fv\cos\theta$$

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Kinetic energy ($K$)** | Energy associated with an object's motion; $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ |
| **Work ($W$)** | Energy transferred to/from a system by a force acting through a displacement |
| **Gravitational PE ($U_g$)** | Energy stored due to an object's height in a gravitational field |
| **Elastic PE ($U_s$)** | Energy stored in a deformed elastic object (spring) |
| **Mechanical energy** | Sum of kinetic and potential energy: $E = K + U$ |
| **Conservative force** | Force whose work is independent of path (only depends on start and end points) |
| **Non-conservative force** | Force whose work depends on the path taken (e.g., friction) |
| **Power ($P$)** | Rate of energy transfer or work done per unit time |
| **Isolated system** | A system with no external forces doing work |
| **Thermal energy** | Internal energy associated with random molecular motion; increased by friction |
| **Work-energy theorem** | Net work on an object equals its change in kinetic energy |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **Work requires motion** -- Correct. If there is no displacement, no work is done (e.g., holding a heavy box stationary does zero work on the box).
2. **Force always does positive work** -- False. Work is negative when force opposes displacement (e.g., friction).
3. **Potential energy is absolute** -- Only changes in PE matter. The reference level for $U_g = 0$ is arbitrary.
4. **Energy is "used up"** -- Energy is never destroyed; it transforms (e.g., mechanical to thermal).
5. **Centripetal force does work** -- No. Centripetal force is always perpendicular to velocity, so $W = Fd\cos 90° = 0$.
6. **KE depends on direction** -- KE depends on speed (scalar), not velocity (vector). $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ is always positive.
7. **Spring PE is $\frac{1}{2}kx$** -- It's $\frac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2$. Don't forget the square.
