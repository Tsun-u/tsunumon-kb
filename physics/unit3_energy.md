# Unit 3: Work, Energy, and Power

> Exam Weight: 18--23% | ~22--27 Class Periods

## Topics

### 3.1 Translational Kinetic Energy
- Kinetic energy: energy an object possesses due to its motion
- $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
- A scalar quantity that is always greater than or equal to zero
- Determined by speed rather than velocity; its value depends on the chosen reference frame

### 3.2 Work
- Work done by a constant force: $W = Fd\cos\theta$
- $\theta$ is the angle between the force and displacement vectors
- Work is a scalar that may be positive, negative, or zero
- Positive work: the force has a component pointing in the same direction as the displacement, adding energy to the object
- Negative work: the force opposes the displacement, removing energy from the object
- Zero work: force acts perpendicular to the displacement (e.g., normal force on a level surface, centripetal force in circular motion)
- Work-energy theorem: $W_{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i$
- The total work done on an object equals how much its kinetic energy changes

### 3.3 Potential Energy
- Gravitational potential energy: $U_g = mgy$ (valid near Earth's surface; requires a chosen zero reference height)
- Elastic (spring) potential energy: $U_s = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2$
- Potential energy belongs to a system, not to any single object within it
- Only differences in potential energy carry physical meaning; the zero reference is chosen for convenience
- Conservative forces: the work they do is independent of the path taken (e.g., gravity, spring force)
- Non-conservative forces: the work they do depends on the specific path followed (e.g., friction, air resistance)

### 3.4 Conservation of Energy
- Total mechanical energy: $E = K + U$
- For an isolated system subject only to conservative forces: $K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f$
- When non-conservative forces act (e.g., friction): $K_i + U_i + W_{nc} = K_f + U_f$
- Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only change form or move between objects
- Friction causes mechanical energy to convert into thermal energy (heat within the materials)
- Energy bar charts (LOL diagrams) provide a helpful way to track energy changes visually

### 3.5 Power
- Average power: $P_{avg} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \frac{W}{\Delta t}$
- Instantaneous power: $P = Fv\cos\theta$
- For a constant force aligned with the motion: $P = Fv$
- Power quantifies how rapidly energy is transferred or converted

## Skills to Master

- Compute the kinetic energy of a moving object given its mass and speed
- Find the work performed by a constant force applied at an angle to the displacement
- Use the work-energy theorem to connect net work to the resulting change in kinetic energy
- Evaluate gravitational and elastic potential energy for a given configuration
- Solve energy conservation problems both with and without the influence of non-conservative forces
- Construct and interpret energy bar charts to track energy transformations across a process
- Determine average and instantaneous power from energy, work, force, and velocity data
- Recognize conditions under which mechanical energy remains constant versus when it decreases

## Core Concepts

1. An object's kinetic energy arises from its motion and scales with both its mass and the square of its speed
2. When a force causes a displacement, it transfers energy into or out of a system — this transfer is called work
3. The total (net) work done on an object precisely equals the object's change in kinetic energy — the work-energy theorem
4. Potential energy represents energy held within a system by virtue of the relative positions or configurations of its parts
5. In an isolated system where only conservative forces operate, the sum of kinetic and potential energy stays constant
6. Non-conservative forces such as friction remove mechanical energy from a system, converting it to thermal energy
7. Power is the rate at which energy moves or work is accomplished — it describes how fast, not just how much

## Key Equations

### Kinetic Energy

$$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

### Work

$$W = Fd\cos\theta$$

$$W_{net} = \Delta K$$

### Potential Energy

$$U_g = mgy$$

$$U_s = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2$$

### Conservation of Energy

$$K_i + U_i + W_{nc} = K_f + U_f$$

$$\Delta E_{system} = 0 \quad \text{(isolated system)}$$

### Power

$$P_{avg} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \frac{W}{\Delta t}$$

$$P = Fv\cos\theta$$

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Kinetic energy ($K$)** | The energy an object has by virtue of moving; given by $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ |
| **Work ($W$)** | The amount of energy moved into or out of a system when a force acts across a displacement |
| **Gravitational PE ($U_g$)** | Stored energy resulting from an object's position within a gravitational field |
| **Elastic PE ($U_s$)** | Energy held in a stretched or compressed elastic object such as a spring |
| **Mechanical energy** | The combined total of kinetic and potential energy in a system: $E = K + U$ |
| **Conservative force** | A force for which the work done between two points is the same regardless of the route taken |
| **Non-conservative force** | A force whose work output varies depending on the path traveled (e.g., friction) |
| **Power ($P$)** | How quickly energy is transferred or work is performed; energy per unit time |
| **Isolated system** | A system on which no external agent performs work |
| **Thermal energy** | Energy linked to the disordered motion of atoms and molecules; generated by friction |
| **Work-energy theorem** | The principle that an object's change in kinetic energy equals the net work done on it |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **Work requires motion** -- True. Without displacement, a force cannot do work, regardless of its magnitude. Holding a heavy object still exerts a force but transfers zero energy to that object.
2. **A force always adds energy to an object** -- Not so. When a force points opposite to the direction of motion, the work is negative — meaning energy is removed from the object (e.g., friction slowing a sliding block).
3. **Potential energy has a fixed, absolute value** -- Only the change in potential energy matters physically. The height chosen as $U_g = 0$ is a free choice that cancels out in any complete calculation.
4. **Energy gets "used up" or destroyed** -- Energy always persists; it simply shifts form. What we call "lost" energy has typically become thermal energy through friction or other dissipative processes.
5. **Centripetal force does work on a circular orbit** -- It does not. Because centripetal force always points toward the center — perpendicular to the velocity — every increment of work it does is $Fd\cos 90° = 0$.
6. **Kinetic energy depends on direction of travel** -- KE depends only on speed, which is the magnitude of velocity. Reversing direction leaves KE unchanged; $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ is always non-negative.
7. **Spring potential energy is $\frac{1}{2}k\Delta x$** -- The correct expression is $\frac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2$. The compression or extension must be squared before multiplying by $\frac{1}{2}k$.
