# Unit 12: Magnetism and Electromagnetism

> Exam Weight: 12--15% | ~10--13 Class Periods

## Topics

### 12.1 Magnetic Fields
- The magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is a vector quantity that describes the region of influence surrounding magnets and moving charges, capable of exerting forces on other moving charges and current-carrying conductors
- Magnetic field lines always form closed, continuous loops: outside the magnet they travel from the north (N) pole to the south (S) pole, and inside the magnet they run from south to north
- Isolated magnetic poles have never been found; magnetism always involves paired north and south poles together. Cutting a bar magnet produces two smaller magnets, each with its own N and S pole — not separated monopoles
- Earth's geographic north pole sits near a magnetic south pole, which is why the north-seeking pole of a compass needle points toward it (opposite poles attract)
- The SI unit of magnetic field is the tesla: $1 \text{ T} = 1 \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{A} \cdot \text{s}^2}$
- A bar magnet qualifies as a magnetic dipole, and its external field pattern closely resembles that generated by a circular current loop
- Where field lines are closely packed, the field is strong; where they spread apart, the field is weaker

### 12.2 Magnetism and Moving Charges
- A charged particle moving through a magnetic field experiences a force whose magnitude is:

$$F = qvB\sin\theta$$

  where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$
- Because this force always acts at right angles to both the velocity and the field, it never does work on the particle — it steers the particle's path without altering its speed or kinetic energy
- Right-hand rule for positive charges: align the fingers with $\vec{v}$ and curl them toward $\vec{B}$; the extended thumb points in the direction of $\vec{F}$. For a negative charge, reverse the result
- When a charged particle's velocity is exactly perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, the constant sideways force causes it to travel in a circle. Equating the magnetic force to the required centripetal force:

$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r} \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$

- The orbital radius grows with the particle's momentum $mv$ and shrinks with larger charge or stronger field
- The orbital period in a uniform $B$ field is $T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}$, which is independent of the particle's speed — a key property exploited in cyclotrons
- Velocity selector: electric and magnetic fields oriented perpendicular to each other exert opposing forces on a moving charge; only particles whose speed satisfies $v = \frac{E}{B}$ experience zero net force and travel straight through

### 12.3 Magnetism and Current-Carrying Wires
- A wire carrying current through an external magnetic field is pushed by a force:

$$F = BIL\sin\theta$$

  where $I$ is the current, $L$ is the length of wire within the field, and $\theta$ is the angle between the current direction and $\vec{B}$
- Right-hand rule for wire forces: orient the fingers along the conventional current direction and curl them toward $\vec{B}$; the thumb indicates the direction of the resulting force $\vec{F}$
- Two parallel wires with currents in the same direction pull each other together; currents flowing in opposite directions push the wires apart
- A flat coil of wire placed in a uniform magnetic field feels a twisting force — a torque — that acts to rotate it until its plane is perpendicular to the field:

$$\tau = NIAB\sin\theta$$

  where $N$ is the number of turns, $A$ is the area of the loop, and $\theta$ is the angle between the area vector (normal to the loop) and $\vec{B}$
- A current-carrying loop behaves as a magnetic dipole; to find which face is the north pole, curl the right-hand fingers in the direction the current circulates — the thumb then points toward the north pole (and in the direction of the magnetic moment $\vec{\mu}$)

### 12.4 Electromagnetic Induction and Faraday's Law
- Magnetic flux quantifies how much of the magnetic field passes through a given surface:

$$\Phi_B = BA\cos\theta$$

  where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{B}$ and the area vector $\hat{n}$ (normal to the surface). Units: weber (Wb), $1 \text{ Wb} = 1 \text{ T} \cdot \text{m}^2$
- Faraday's law: whenever the magnetic flux through a conducting loop changes, an emf is induced in that loop:

$$|\mathcal{E}| = N \left|\frac{\Delta \Phi_B}{\Delta t}\right|$$

  where $N$ is the number of turns in the coil
- Lenz's law: the induced current always sets up its own magnetic field in the direction that resists the flux change responsible for it — a direct expression of energy conservation
- Motional emf: a straight conducting rod of length $L$ swept at velocity $v$ perpendicular to a field $B$ acts as a source of emf:

$$\mathcal{E} = BLv$$

- Any of the following changes in a circuit produces a changing flux and therefore an induced emf:
  - Altering the magnetic field strength $B$
  - Changing the effective area $A$ enclosed by the loop
  - Rotating the loop so that the angle $\theta$ between $\vec{B}$ and the area vector changes
  - Moving the loop in or out of a field region
- Practical devices that rely on electromagnetic induction:
  - **Generators**: mechanical rotation of a coil in a magnetic field produces a continuously changing flux, generating alternating emf
  - **Transformers**: an alternating current in a primary coil creates a time-varying flux that induces a voltage in a secondary coil; $\frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}$ (ideal transformer)
  - **Eddy currents**: circulating induced currents that form in solid conductors exposed to changing flux; harnessed in electromagnetic braking and induction-based heating

## Core Concepts

1. All magnetic fields trace back to moving charges — either free currents in wires or aligned atomic currents in permanent magnets; field lines always close on themselves and no point source (monopole) for magnetism has ever been found
2. The force a magnetic field exerts on a moving charge is always perpendicular to the particle's velocity, which means it continuously redirects the particle without ever increasing or decreasing its kinetic energy
3. A charged particle entering perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field orbits in a circle; the radius of that orbit is set by the particle's momentum divided by the product of its charge and the field strength
4. A magnetic field pushes on any segment of wire carrying current; as a result, two parallel current-carrying wires exert opposing or attracting forces on each other depending on whether their currents run in opposite or the same direction
5. Faraday's law states that any change in the magnetic flux through a conducting loop drives an induced emf around that loop; Lenz's law specifies that the resulting induced current will oppose — though never fully cancel — the flux change that created it
6. The ability to generate emf from changing flux is the physical foundation of AC generators, transformers, induction motors, and numerous other electromagnetic technologies

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Magnetic field ($\vec{B}$)** | A vector field created by moving charges and magnets that exerts forces on other moving charges and magnetic materials; SI unit is tesla (T) |
| **Tesla (T)** | SI unit of magnetic field strength; $1 \text{ T} = 1 \text{ kg/(A} \cdot \text{s}^2\text{)}$ |
| **Magnetic dipole** | A magnetic object — such as a bar magnet or a current-carrying loop — that possesses both a north and a south pole |
| **Magnetic monopole** | A theoretical particle carrying only a single magnetic pole; exhaustive searches have never found one in nature |
| **Right-hand rule** | A hand-gesture technique for finding directions in magnetism: used to determine the magnetic force on a charge, the field encircling a current, or the magnetic moment of a loop |
| **Magnetic flux ($\Phi_B$)** | A measure of the total magnetic field threading through a surface, equal to $BA\cos\theta$; SI unit is weber (Wb) |
| **Weber (Wb)** | SI unit of magnetic flux; $1 \text{ Wb} = 1 \text{ T} \cdot \text{m}^2$ |
| **Electromagnetic induction** | The phenomenon in which a time-varying magnetic flux through a conductor loop drives an emf and, if the circuit is closed, an electric current |
| **Electromotive force (emf, $\mathcal{E}$)** | The energy per unit charge delivered to a circuit by an inductive or chemical source; SI unit is volt (V) |
| **Faraday's law** | A law stating that the induced emf in a coil equals the negative rate of change of the magnetic flux linking it; in magnitude: $|\mathcal{E}| = N|\Delta\Phi_B/\Delta t|$ |
| **Lenz's law** | A rule that the induced current will always flow in the direction that creates a magnetic field opposing — but not eliminating — the change in flux that produced it |
| **Motional emf** | The voltage generated across a conductor as it moves through a magnetic field due to the magnetic force separating positive and negative charges |
| **Velocity selector** | A device in which perpendicular electric and magnetic fields are balanced so that only particles travelling at exactly $v = E/B$ pass straight through without deflection |
| **Transformer** | A device with two inductively coupled coils that uses a changing magnetic flux to step AC voltage up or down between the primary and secondary windings |
| **Eddy currents** | Swirling induced currents that arise inside solid conductors when they are exposed to a changing magnetic flux; responsible for electromagnetic braking and heating effects |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **Magnetic forces can do work on charged particles** -- This is incorrect. Because the magnetic force always acts at a right angle to the velocity ($\vec{F} \perp \vec{v}$), the dot product $\vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}$ is zero and no work is done. A magnetic field can curve a particle's trajectory but cannot speed it up or slow it down.
2. **The north pole of a compass points to Earth's magnetic north pole** -- This is misleading. Earth's geographic north pole is geographically defined and happens to be located near a magnetic south pole. A compass north pole is attracted toward it precisely because opposite magnetic poles attract each other.
3. **Breaking a magnet in half gives an isolated north pole and an isolated south pole** -- Cutting a magnet creates two smaller magnets, each with its own north and south pole. No matter how many times you divide it, you never isolate a single pole — magnetic monopoles have never been observed.
4. **A stronger magnetic field always means a greater force on a charged particle** -- Field strength is just one factor. The force $F = qvB\sin\theta$ also depends on the particle's speed and the orientation of its velocity relative to the field. A stationary charge or a charge moving exactly along a field line feels zero force, no matter how strong the field.
5. **An induced emf requires a changing magnetic field** -- Faraday's law is about changing magnetic flux, not changing magnetic field alone. Flux can change even when $B$ is constant — for instance, by moving the loop, resizing it, or rotating it. Any of these will induce an emf.
6. **Lenz's law means the induced current stops the flux change entirely** -- The induced current resists the change, but does not halt it. If the induced current perfectly canceled the flux change, there would be no flux change to drive an emf, and therefore no current — a logical contradiction. The opposition is real but always partial.
7. **A transformer can work with direct current (DC)** -- Transformers depend on a continuously changing magnetic flux to induce voltage in the secondary coil. A steady DC current creates a static, unchanging flux, which produces no induction and therefore no output from the secondary coil.
