# E.5: Fusion and Stars

> Theme E: Nuclear and Quantum Physics | SL + HL

## Key Concepts

### Nuclear Fusion
- Two light nuclei collide and merge into a single heavier nucleus, releasing energy in the process
- Temperatures on the order of ~$10^7$ K are needed so that nuclei have sufficient kinetic energy to get close enough for the strong nuclear force to take over despite their mutual electrostatic repulsion
- The energy release originates from the fact that the resulting nucleus sits at a higher binding energy per nucleon than the original pair — the mass difference appears as released energy via $E = mc^2$
- Examples:
  - Proton-proton chain (main sequence stars like the Sun):
    - $4^1_1H \rightarrow ^4_2He + 2^0_{+1}e + 2\nu_e + \text{energy}$ (net reaction)
  - Deuterium-tritium fusion (most promising for reactors):
    - $^2_1H + ^3_1H \rightarrow ^4_2He + ^1_0n + 17.6$ MeV

### Stellar Physics
- **Main sequence stars**: energy generation is driven by hydrogen-to-helium fusion occurring in the stellar core
  - Hydrostatic equilibrium: a star maintains a stable size because the inward pull of gravity is exactly offset by outward radiation and thermal pressure from fusion
  - Mass-luminosity relationship: higher-mass stars run fusion reactions faster, producing far greater luminosity and surface temperatures
  - Hertzsprung-Russell (HR) diagram: a graph of stellar luminosity against surface temperature used to classify stars and track their evolution
    - Main sequence: a prominent diagonal band where the majority of stars spend most of their lives
    - Red giants: occupy the upper-right region — large, cool, and highly luminous
    - White dwarfs: found in the lower-left region — compact remnants that are hot but faint

### Stellar Evolution
- **Low-mass stars** (like the Sun):
  - Progress from the main sequence → red giant → planetary nebula → white dwarf
- **High-mass stars**:
  - Progress from the main sequence → red supergiant → supernova → neutron star or black hole
- Elements heavier than iron cannot be synthesised through ordinary stellar fusion; they are forged during the violent conditions of supernova explosions
- **Chandrasekhar limit**: an upper mass threshold of roughly 1.4 solar masses beyond which electron degeneracy pressure can no longer support a white dwarf against gravitational collapse

### Fusion Power (on Earth)
- The central engineering problem is generating and sustaining plasma hot enough for fusion while preventing it from contacting the reactor walls
- **Magnetic confinement**: a tokamak uses powerful toroidal magnetic fields to trap and insulate the high-temperature plasma away from physical surfaces
- **Inertial confinement**: high-powered lasers simultaneously strike a small fuel pellet from multiple directions, compressing and heating it to fusion conditions in an instant
- Compared to fission, fusion offers key advantages: the primary fuel (deuterium) is extractable from seawater, long-lived radioactive byproducts are minimal, and there is no self-sustaining chain reaction that could lead to meltdown
- Currently in the experimental phase — facilities such as ITER and NIF are working toward demonstrating net energy gain, but commercial power generation remains a future goal

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Nuclear fusion** | A nuclear reaction in which two light nuclei merge to produce a heavier nucleus, with the mass difference converted into released energy |
| **Plasma** | A high-energy state of matter in which electrons have been stripped from atoms, leaving a gas of free ions and electrons; the necessary medium for fusion reactions |
| **Main sequence** | The extended period in a star's lifetime during which it steadily converts hydrogen into helium via fusion in its core |
| **Hydrostatic equilibrium** | The condition in which the inward gravitational force on a star's layers is exactly countered by the outward pressure from heat and radiation generated in the core |
| **HR diagram** | A Hertzsprung-Russell diagram — a classification chart that maps stars according to their luminosity on one axis and surface temperature on the other |
| **Supernova** | The catastrophic explosion marking the end of a massive star's life, during which nucleosynthesis produces elements beyond iron |
| **Chandrasekhar limit** | The theoretical maximum mass (~1.4 $M_\odot$) that a white dwarf remnant can possess before gravitational collapse overcomes electron degeneracy pressure |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"The Sun burns fuel like fire"** -- Solar energy production is a nuclear process, not a chemical one; the Sun fuses atomic nuclei together, releasing energy through mass conversion rather than through electron-level chemical bonding.
2. **"Fusion is easy because hydrogen is abundant"** -- Fuel availability is not the bottleneck; the real difficulty lies in reaching and maintaining the enormous temperatures and densities required for nuclei to overcome their electrostatic repulsion and fuse.
3. **"All stars end as black holes"** -- Stellar fate depends on mass; only the most massive stars end this way. Intermediate-mass stars like the Sun eventually become white dwarfs, while slightly more massive stars may collapse into neutron stars.
4. **"Stars are in the same location on the HR diagram throughout their lives"** -- A star's position on the HR diagram shifts as it ages; as internal composition and energy output change, both luminosity and surface temperature change, moving the star to different regions of the diagram.
