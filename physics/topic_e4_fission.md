## Key Concepts

### Mass Defect and Binding Energy
- **Mass defect** ($\Delta m$): the assembled nucleus has less mass than the sum of its free nucleons; this missing mass is the mass defect
  - $\Delta m = [Zm_p + Nm_n] - m_{nucleus}$
- **Binding energy** ($E_b$): the energy equivalent of the mass defect — how much energy would be needed to pull every nucleon completely apart
  - $E_b = \Delta m \cdot c^2$ (from $E = mc^2$)
- **Binding energy per nucleon**: $E_b / A$
  - Reaches a maximum at iron-56 (~8.8 MeV per nucleon)
  - Nuclei heavier than iron can release energy through fission (daughter nuclei sit higher on the curve)
  - Nuclei lighter than iron can release energy through fusion (product nucleus sits higher on the curve)

### Nuclear Fission
- A heavy nucleus breaks apart into two smaller fragment nuclei, releasing fast neutrons and a large quantity of energy
- Induced fission: a neutron is captured by the heavy nucleus, destabilising it and causing it to split
- Example: $^{235}_{92}U + ^1_0n \rightarrow ^{141}_{56}Ba + ^{92}_{36}Kr + 3^1_0n + \text{energy}$
- The fragment nuclei are not unique; a given fissile isotope can produce many different fragment pairs
- Each fission event typically releases around 200 MeV of energy

### Chain Reaction
- The fast neutrons emitted in each fission event are available to trigger additional fission events in nearby nuclei
- **Critical mass**: the smallest quantity of fissile material in which a self-sustaining chain reaction is possible
- **Subcritical**: too many neutrons escape or are absorbed without causing fission; the reaction rate declines and eventually stops
- **Supercritical**: each generation produces more neutrons than the last, driving an exponentially growing, uncontrolled release of energy (the principle behind nuclear weapons)
- **Critical**: on average, exactly one neutron from each fission event goes on to cause another fission, maintaining a steady reaction rate and constant power output

### Nuclear Power
- **Nuclear reactor** components:
  - **Fuel rods**: house the fissile material (typically enriched uranium or plutonium) where the sustained chain reaction takes place
  - **Moderator**: a material (such as water, heavy water, or graphite) that slows fast neutrons down to thermal (low-energy) speeds, making them far more effective at inducing fission in U-235
  - **Control rods**: inserted into or withdrawn from the core to fine-tune the reaction rate by absorbing surplus neutrons; common materials are boron and cadmium
  - **Coolant**: a fluid that circulates through the reactor core, carrying heat away to drive turbines for electricity generation
  - **Shielding**: dense barriers of concrete and steel that absorb emitted radiation and protect workers and the surrounding environment
- **Thermal neutrons**: low-energy neutrons with speeds comparable to thermal motion — these have a much higher probability of being captured by U-235 and triggering fission than fast neutrons do
- Advantages: virtually no CO₂ emissions during operation, extremely high energy output per unit mass of fuel, and capable of supplying steady round-the-clock baseload electricity
- Disadvantages: produces long-lived radioactive waste requiring secure storage for thousands of years, carries the risk of severe accidents if safety systems fail, and raises international concerns about nuclear material being diverted for weapons

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Nuclear fission is the same as an atomic bomb"** -- A reactor is held at precisely critical conditions so the chain reaction remains stable and controllable. A nuclear weapon, by contrast, drives the material rapidly into a supercritical state so that energy is released in an instant rather than steadily over time.
2. **"Fission produces no waste"** -- While fission emits negligible greenhouse gases, the fragment nuclei left behind are intensely radioactive and must be isolated in specialised facilities for timescales that can span tens of thousands of years.
3. **"Any heavy nucleus can be used as fuel"** -- Only specific fissile isotopes — most commonly U-235 and Pu-239 — have the right nuclear properties to capture slow neutrons reliably and maintain a self-sustaining chain reaction.
4. **"Nuclear energy is always dangerous"** -- Like any large-scale energy technology, nuclear power carries risks, but these can be reduced substantially through robust engineering and regulation. When measured by deaths or health impacts per unit of energy produced, nuclear power's safety record is historically comparable to or better than that of most fossil-fuel alternatives.
