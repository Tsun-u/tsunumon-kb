# Unit 2: Force and Translational Dynamics

> Exam Weight: 18--23% | ~22--27 Class Periods

## Topics

### 2.1 Systems and Center of Mass
- A system is any group of objects bundled together and analyzed as one unit
- The center of mass is the single representative point where, for purposes of translational analysis, the entire mass of the system behaves as if it were located
- For a two-object system: $x_{cm} = \frac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2}{m_1 + m_2}$
- Forces that objects within the system exert on one another (internal forces) have no net effect on the center-of-mass motion
- The center of mass accelerates only when a net external force acts on the system

### 2.2 Forces and Free-Body Diagrams
- A force arises from an interaction between two objects; it can be thought of as either a push or a pull
- Contact forces require physical touching: normal force, friction, tension, spring force
- Field (non-contact) forces act at a distance: gravity, electromagnetism
- A free-body diagram (FBD) isolates one object and represents every force acting on it as an arrow originating from a point representing that object
- Each arrow's direction and relative length must faithfully reflect the force's direction and magnitude

### 2.3 Newton's Third Law
- Whenever object A pushes or pulls on object B, object B simultaneously pushes or pulls on object A with equal magnitude and opposite direction
- $\vec{F}_{A \text{ on } B} = -\vec{F}_{B \text{ on } A}$
- These action-reaction partners always act on two DIFFERENT objects, so they never appear together on one object's FBD
- The paired forces are always the same type (e.g., both gravitational, or both contact normal)

### 2.4 Newton's First Law
- Without a net external force, a stationary object remains stationary and a moving object keeps moving at constant velocity
- Inertia is an object's built-in resistance to any change in its velocity
- An inertial reference frame is one that is not accelerating; Newton's first law holds exactly within such a frame
- Equilibrium occurs when $\sum \vec{F} = 0$, meaning zero net force and therefore zero acceleration (covers both static and constant-velocity cases)

### 2.5 Newton's Second Law
- $\vec{a} = \frac{\sum \vec{F}}{m}$ or equivalently $\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$
- An object's acceleration grows in direct proportion to the net force applied and shrinks in inverse proportion to its mass
- The law must be applied separately along each coordinate axis: $\sum F_x = ma_x$, $\sum F_y = ma_y$
- Inertial mass quantifies how strongly an object resists being accelerated
- Gravitational mass quantifies how strongly an object responds to a gravitational field; experiments confirm these two are equal

### 2.6 Gravitational Force
- Close to Earth's surface: $F_g = mg$ where $g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
- Weight is the gravitational pull Earth exerts on an object; it points toward Earth's center
- Newton's law of universal gravitation: $F_g = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2}$
- $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2$
- Gravitational field: $g = \frac{F_g}{m} = \frac{GM}{r^2}$

### 2.7 Kinetic and Static Friction
- Kinetic friction: $f_k = \mu_k F_N$ — acts on a surface that is already sliding, opposing the relative motion
- Static friction: $f_s \leq \mu_s F_N$ — acts to prevent surfaces from starting to slide; its magnitude self-adjusts up to a maximum value
- For nearly all real surfaces $\mu_s > \mu_k$, so more force is needed to start sliding than to maintain it
- Friction acts along the contact surface, always opposing relative motion or tendency to move
- The normal force is perpendicular to the contact surface and does not contribute to friction directly — it determines friction's ceiling

### 2.8 Spring Forces
- Hooke's Law: $F_s = k|\Delta x|$ (magnitude), directed toward equilibrium
- $k$ = spring constant, measuring the spring's stiffness; units: N/m
- The spring force is restorative: it always points back toward the natural (equilibrium) length, opposing the stretch or compression
- This linear relationship holds for ideal springs as long as deformation stays within the elastic limit

### 2.9 Circular Motion
- Uniform circular motion means the object's speed is constant, but its direction — and therefore velocity — changes continuously
- Centripetal acceleration: $a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$, directed toward center
- Centripetal force is not a new or separate kind of force; it is whatever net inward force happens to be keeping the object on its circular path
- Period: $T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$
- Frequency: $f = \frac{1}{T}$
- In orbital scenarios the gravitational attraction between bodies supplies the centripetal force needed to maintain the circular (or elliptical) path

## Skills to Master

- Enumerate every force on an object and construct correct free-body diagrams
- Use Newton's three laws together to predict how objects will move and to explain observed motion
- Contrast inertial mass with gravitational mass and articulate why they are treated as equivalent
- Compute gravitational forces using both the surface approximation $F_g = mg$ and the universal gravitation formula
- Work through friction scenarios involving both static and kinetic friction coefficients
- Use Hooke's Law to find spring forces and related displacements
- Examine uniform circular motion, determine the centripetal acceleration, and trace which physical force provides it
- Break down inclined-plane and multi-object problems using Newton's second law applied component by component
- Articulate how Newton's third law pairs are related and why they cannot cancel within a single free-body diagram

## Core Concepts

1. Every force originates from an interaction between two objects; forces are classified as contact (requiring physical touch) or non-contact (acting through fields across a distance)
2. Newton's first law establishes what an inertial reference frame is: a frame where an object with zero net force neither accelerates nor changes direction, and where equilibrium ($\sum \vec{F} = 0$) is a well-defined concept
3. The relationship $\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$ (Newton's second law) quantitatively connects the net force on an object, its mass, and the resulting acceleration
4. When two objects interact, each exerts a force on the other that is equal in strength, opposite in direction, and of the same physical type; these action-reaction partners always target different objects
5. Near Earth's surface the gravitational pull on any object scales directly with its mass according to $F_g = mg$
6. Both static and kinetic friction forces are proportional to the normal force pressing the surfaces together, scaled by the appropriate coefficient ($\mu_s$ or $\mu_k$)
7. Traveling in a circle at constant speed still requires acceleration — directed inward toward the center — because the velocity direction is continuously changing
8. For ideal springs within their elastic limit, the restoring force grows linearly with the amount the spring is stretched or compressed (Hooke's Law)

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Force** | A vector quantity arising from the interaction of two objects, measured in newtons (N); perceived as either a push or a pull |
| **Net force** | The single vector obtained by adding all individual forces acting on an object |
| **Normal force** | The contact force a surface exerts on an object perpendicular (at right angles) to their shared interface |
| **Tension** | The pulling force carried along a string, rope, or cable connecting two objects |
| **Weight** | The gravitational force Earth exerts on an object, given by $W = mg$ and directed downward |
| **Mass** | A measure of how much matter an object contains; equivalently, a measure of its resistance to acceleration |
| **Inertia** | The inherent property of matter by which it opposes any change in its state of motion |
| **Equilibrium** | A condition in which the net force on an object is zero, meaning its acceleration is also zero |
| **Free-body diagram** | A simplified sketch that represents one object as a dot and depicts every force acting on it as a labeled arrow |
| **Centripetal** | Literally "center-seeking"; describes anything directed toward the center of a circular path |
| **Period ($T$)** | The duration of one complete cycle or revolution |
| **Frequency ($f$)** | How many complete cycles or revolutions occur per second |
| **Spring constant ($k$)** | A numerical indicator of a spring's stiffness; the force required per unit of stretch or compression |
| **Coefficient of friction ($\mu$)** | A dimensionless number expressing the ratio of friction force to normal force for a given pair of surfaces |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Centripetal force" is a separate force** -- It is not a distinct force type. "Centripetal force" simply names the role played by whatever inward-directed force — gravity, tension, friction, normal force, or some combination — keeps an object on a curved path.
2. **Objects in motion require a force to keep moving** -- This contradicts Newton's first law. An object moving at constant velocity needs zero net force to keep going; a force is only needed when you want to change that velocity.
3. **Normal force always equals weight** -- This holds only when an object rests on a flat, horizontal surface. On a slope at angle $\theta$, the normal force becomes $F_N = mg\cos\theta$, which is less than the full weight.
4. **Heavier objects are harder to push because of more friction** -- There is truth here (greater mass raises the normal force, which raises friction), but the deeper reason is inertia: a more massive object requires more net force to achieve the same acceleration, independent of surface friction.
5. **Newton's third law pairs cancel out** -- Cancellation would require both forces to act on the same object. Third-law partners always act on different objects, so they appear on separate free-body diagrams and never cancel each other.
6. **Static friction is always at its maximum** -- Static friction is a reactive force that takes whatever value is needed to prevent slipping, anywhere from zero up to its ceiling of $\mu_s F_N$. It reaches that maximum only at the instant sliding is about to begin.
7. **Objects in circular motion are "pushed outward"** -- In an inertial (non-rotating) reference frame there is no outward centrifugal force; that sensation is a fictitious effect felt in a rotating frame. The real acceleration points inward, toward the center of the circle.
