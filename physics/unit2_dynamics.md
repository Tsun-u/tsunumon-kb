# Unit 2: Force and Translational Dynamics

> Exam Weight: 18--23% | ~22--27 Class Periods

## Topics

### 2.1 Systems and Center of Mass
- A system is a collection of objects treated as a single entity for analysis
- Center of mass: the point where the system can be treated as if all mass were concentrated
- For a two-object system: $x_{cm} = \frac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2}{m_1 + m_2}$
- Internal forces do not change the motion of the center of mass
- Only external forces affect the acceleration of the center of mass

### 2.2 Forces and Free-Body Diagrams
- A force is a push or pull resulting from an interaction between two objects
- Contact forces: normal, friction, tension, spring
- Non-contact (field) forces: gravitational, electromagnetic
- Free-body diagram (FBD): shows all forces acting ON a single object, drawn as arrows from a dot representing the object
- Forces must be drawn proportional to magnitude and in the correct direction

### 2.3 Newton's Third Law
- If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A
- $\vec{F}_{A \text{ on } B} = -\vec{F}_{B \text{ on } A}$
- Third-law pairs act on DIFFERENT objects (never on the same FBD)
- Third-law pairs are always the same type of force

### 2.4 Newton's First Law
- An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in uniform motion, unless acted upon by a net external force
- Inertia: the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion
- Inertial reference frame: a frame in which Newton's first law holds (non-accelerating)
- Equilibrium: $\sum \vec{F} = 0$ (static or dynamic equilibrium)

### 2.5 Newton's Second Law
- $\vec{a} = \frac{\sum \vec{F}}{m}$ or equivalently $\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$
- The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to net force and inversely proportional to mass
- Must be applied component-wise: $\sum F_x = ma_x$, $\sum F_y = ma_y$
- Inertial mass: measure of resistance to acceleration
- Gravitational mass: measure of gravitational interaction (experimentally equal to inertial mass)

### 2.6 Gravitational Force
- Near Earth's surface: $F_g = mg$ where $g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
- Weight is the gravitational force on an object (vector, directed toward center of Earth)
- Newton's law of universal gravitation: $F_g = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2}$
- $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2$
- Gravitational field: $g = \frac{F_g}{m} = \frac{GM}{r^2}$

### 2.7 Kinetic and Static Friction
- Kinetic friction: $f_k = \mu_k F_N$ (opposes relative sliding motion)
- Static friction: $f_s \leq \mu_s F_N$ (opposes tendency to slide; adjusts up to maximum)
- $\mu_s > \mu_k$ for most surfaces
- Friction is always parallel to the contact surface
- Normal force is perpendicular to the contact surface

### 2.8 Spring Forces
- Hooke's Law: $F_s = k|\Delta x|$ (magnitude), directed toward equilibrium
- $k$ = spring constant (stiffness); units: N/m
- Restoring force: always directed opposite to displacement from equilibrium
- Valid for ideal springs within elastic limit

### 2.9 Circular Motion
- Uniform circular motion: constant speed, changing direction
- Centripetal acceleration: $a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$, directed toward center
- Centripetal force is NOT a new force; it's the net inward force causing circular motion
- Period: $T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$
- Frequency: $f = \frac{1}{T}$
- For orbits: gravitational force provides centripetal force

## Learning Objectives

- Identify forces acting on an object and draw accurate free-body diagrams
- Apply Newton's three laws to predict and explain motion
- Distinguish between inertial and gravitational mass
- Calculate gravitational force using both $F_g = mg$ and universal gravitation
- Solve problems involving friction (static and kinetic)
- Apply Hooke's Law to spring force problems
- Analyze uniform circular motion and identify the source of centripetal force
- Use Newton's second law in component form for inclined planes and multi-object systems
- Explain the relationship between Newton's third law pairs

## Essential Knowledge

1. Forces are interactions between objects and can be contact or non-contact
2. Newton's first law defines inertial reference frames and the concept of equilibrium
3. Newton's second law relates net force, mass, and acceleration ($\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$)
4. Newton's third law pairs are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, same type, and act on different objects
5. Gravitational force near Earth is proportional to mass ($F_g = mg$)
6. Friction forces depend on the normal force and coefficient of friction
7. An object in uniform circular motion has acceleration directed toward the center
8. Spring forces obey Hooke's Law for small displacements

## Key Equations

### Newton's Laws

$$\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$

$$\vec{F}_{A \text{ on } B} = -\vec{F}_{B \text{ on } A}$$

### Gravitational Force

$$F_g = mg \quad \text{(near surface)}$$

$$F_g = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2} \quad \text{(universal)}$$

$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2} \quad \text{(gravitational field)}$$

### Friction

$$f_k = \mu_k F_N$$

$$f_s \leq \mu_s F_N$$

### Spring Force (Hooke's Law)

$$|\vec{F}_s| = k|\Delta x|$$

### Circular Motion

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$$

$$T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$$

$$f = \frac{1}{T}$$

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Force** | An interaction between two objects; a push or pull (vector, SI unit: newton, N) |
| **Net force** | Vector sum of all forces acting on an object |
| **Normal force** | Contact force perpendicular to a surface, exerted by the surface on the object |
| **Tension** | Pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable |
| **Weight** | Gravitational force on an object; $W = mg$ |
| **Mass** | Measure of the amount of matter in an object; also measure of inertia |
| **Inertia** | Tendency of an object to resist changes in its velocity |
| **Equilibrium** | State where net force is zero; object has zero acceleration |
| **Free-body diagram** | Diagram showing all forces acting on a single object |
| **Centripetal** | "Center-seeking"; directed toward the center of a circular path |
| **Period ($T$)** | Time for one complete revolution or cycle |
| **Frequency ($f$)** | Number of revolutions or cycles per unit time |
| **Spring constant ($k$)** | Measure of spring stiffness; force per unit deformation |
| **Coefficient of friction ($\mu$)** | Dimensionless ratio of friction force to normal force |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Centripetal force" is a separate force** -- No. Centripetal force is the name for the net inward force. It could be gravity, tension, friction, normal force, or a combination.
2. **Objects in motion require a force to keep moving** -- No. Newton's first law says objects in motion stay in motion without a net force.
3. **Normal force always equals weight** -- Only on flat horizontal surfaces. On inclines, $F_N = mg\cos\theta$.
4. **Heavier objects are harder to push because of more friction** -- Partially true (more $F_N$ means more friction), but the primary reason is greater inertia (more mass).
5. **Newton's third law pairs cancel out** -- They act on different objects, so they never cancel in a single free-body diagram.
6. **Static friction is always at its maximum** -- Static friction adjusts from zero up to $\mu_s F_N$ as needed to prevent sliding.
7. **Objects in circular motion are "pushed outward"** -- There is no outward (centrifugal) force in an inertial frame. The object accelerates inward.
