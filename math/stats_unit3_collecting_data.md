# Unit 3: Collecting Data
> Exam Weight: 12–15% | ~9–10 Class Periods

## Topics

### 3.1 Introducing Statistics: Do the Data We Collected Tell the Truth?
- **Learning Objective (DAT-3.A):** Identify the type of study (observational study, survey, or experiment)
- **Essential Knowledge:**
  - **Observational study:** observes individuals and measures variables without attempting to influence the responses
  - **Experiment:** deliberately imposes a treatment on individuals to observe their responses
  - Only **experiments** with random assignment can establish **cause-and-effect** relationships
  - Observational studies can only show **association**

### 3.2 Introduction to Planning a Study
- **Learning Objective (DAT-3.B):** Identify the population and sample in a study
- **Essential Knowledge:**
  - **Population:** the entire group of individuals about which we want information
  - **Sample:** a subset of the population from which we actually collect data
  - **Census:** an attempt to contact every individual in the population
  - A well-designed study minimizes bias and variability

### 3.3 Random Sampling and Data Collection
- **Learning Objective (DAT-3.C):** Describe appropriate sampling methods
- **Essential Knowledge:**
  - **Simple Random Sample (SRS):** every possible sample of size $n$ has an equal chance of being selected
  - **Stratified Random Sample:** divide the population into strata (homogeneous groups), then take an SRS from each stratum
  - **Cluster Sample:** divide the population into clusters (often geographic), randomly select entire clusters, then survey all individuals in chosen clusters
  - **Systematic Sample:** select every $k$th individual from a list after a random start
  - **Convenience sample:** NOT a probability sampling method — results in bias
  - Random sampling allows us to **generalize** results to the population

### 3.4 Potential Problems with Sampling
- **Learning Objective (DAT-3.D):** Identify sources of bias in sampling methods
- **Essential Knowledge:**
  - **Undercoverage bias:** some groups in the population are left out of the sampling process
  - **Nonresponse bias:** individuals selected for the sample do not respond or cannot be contacted
  - **Voluntary response bias:** individuals choose to participate, leading to over-representation of strong opinions
  - **Response bias:** the wording of questions, interviewer behavior, or other factors influence responses
  - **Selection bias:** the sampling method systematically favors certain outcomes
  - Even large samples cannot fix a biased sampling method

### 3.5 Introduction to Experimental Design
- **Learning Objective (VAR-3.A):** Identify the components of an experiment
- **Essential Knowledge:**
  - **Explanatory variable (factor):** the variable that is manipulated
  - **Response variable:** the outcome being measured
  - **Treatments:** the specific conditions applied to experimental units
  - **Experimental units (subjects):** the individuals on which the experiment is performed
  - **Confounding variable:** a variable associated with both the explanatory and response variables, making it impossible to distinguish their effects
  - **Lurking variable:** a variable not included in the study that may influence the relationship

### 3.6 Experimental Design
- **Learning Objective (VAR-3.B):** Describe the principles of experimental design
- **Essential Knowledge:**
  - **Three principles of experimental design:**
    1. **Control:** keep other variables constant to reduce confounding
    2. **Randomization:** use random assignment to create comparable treatment groups
    3. **Replication:** use enough subjects to reduce chance variation
  - **Completely Randomized Design (CRD):** all experimental units are randomly assigned to treatments
  - **Randomized Block Design:** group experimental units into blocks of similar individuals, then randomly assign treatments within each block
  - **Matched Pairs Design:** a special case of blocking — compare two treatments using pairs of similar subjects or the same subject under both treatments
  - **Control group:** a group that receives no treatment or a placebo for comparison
  - **Blinding:** subjects don't know which treatment they receive (single-blind); neither subjects nor evaluators know (double-blind)
  - **Placebo effect:** subjects respond favorably simply because they believe they are being treated

### 3.7 Inference and Experiments
- **Learning Objective (DAT-3.E):** Determine the scope of inference based on study design
- **Essential Knowledge:**
  - **Scope of inference** depends on two factors:
    - **Random sampling** → results can be **generalized** to the population
    - **Random assignment** → **causal conclusions** can be drawn
  - Four combinations:

    | | Random Assignment | No Random Assignment |
    |---|---|---|
    | **Random Sample** | Causal + Generalizable | Association + Generalizable |
    | **No Random Sample** | Causal (limited pop.) | Association (limited pop.) |

  - **Statistical significance:** an observed effect is too large to be plausibly due to chance alone
  - When results are statistically significant in an experiment with random assignment, we can conclude causation

## Common Misconceptions
- Confusing **random sampling** (for generalization) with **random assignment** (for causation) — this is the #1 error on the AP exam
- Thinking a large sample size eliminates bias — it does not; bias is a systematic error in the method
- Confusing **stratified** sampling with **cluster** sampling — stratified samples from each group; cluster surveys entire groups
- Thinking observational studies can establish causation
- Believing that confounding variables can be eliminated from observational studies by statistical methods alone
