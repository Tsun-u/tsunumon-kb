# Unit 1: Chemistry of Life

> AP Exam Weight: 8-11% | ~5-7 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: SYI, ENE, IST

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 1.1: Structure of Water and Hydrogen Bonding

**Learning Objective SYI-1.A**: Explain how the properties of water that result from its polarity and hydrogen bonding affect its biological function.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- SYI-1.A.1: The subcomponents of biological molecules and their sequence determine the properties of that molecule.
- SYI-1.A.2: Living systems depend on properties of water that result from its polarity and hydrogen bonding.
- SYI-1.A.3: The hydrogen bonds between water molecules result in cohesion, adhesion, and surface tension.

**Key Details:**
- Water is a polar molecule due to the unequal sharing of electrons between oxygen and hydrogen atoms
- Oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, creating a partial negative charge on O and partial positive charges on H atoms
- Hydrogen bonds form between the partially positive H of one water molecule and the partially negative O of another
- Properties of water essential for life: cohesion, adhesion, surface tension, high specific heat capacity, high heat of vaporization, universal solvent, expansion upon freezing

### Topic 1.2: Elements of Life

**Learning Objective ENE-1.A**: Describe the composition of macromolecules required by living organisms.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- ENE-1.A.1: Living systems require a variety of macromolecules, including proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and nucleic acids.
- ENE-1.A.2: The most common elements in biological molecules are carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and sulfur (S) — often abbreviated as CHONPS.

### Topic 1.3: Introduction to Biological Macromolecules

**Learning Objective SYI-1.B**: Describe the properties of the monomers and the type of bonds that connect the monomers in biological macromolecules.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- SYI-1.B.1: Hydrolysis and dehydration synthesis (condensation reaction) are used to cleave and form covalent bonds between monomers, respectively.
- SYI-1.B.2: Monomers are the building blocks of polymers. The monomers of the major biological macromolecules are:
  - Carbohydrates: monosaccharides
  - Proteins: amino acids
  - Nucleic acids: nucleotides
  - Lipids: fatty acids and glycerol (note: lipids are NOT true polymers)

### Topic 1.4: Properties of Biological Macromolecules

**Learning Objective SYI-1.B (continued)**: Describe the properties of the monomers and the type of bonds that connect the monomers in biological macromolecules.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- Carbohydrates: linked by glycosidic bonds; functions include energy storage (starch, glycogen) and structural support (cellulose, chitin)
- Lipids: include fats (triglycerides), phospholipids, and steroids; linked by ester bonds; largely hydrophobic
- Proteins: linked by peptide bonds between amino acids; structure determined by R-group interactions
- Nucleic acids: linked by phosphodiester bonds between nucleotides

### Topic 1.5: Structure and Function of Biological Macromolecules

**Learning Objective SYI-1.C**: Explain how a change in the subunits of a polymer may lead to changes in structure or function of the macromolecule.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- SYI-1.C.1: A change in the subunit composition or sequence of a polymer may result in changes in the structure and function of the molecule.
- Protein structure has four levels:
  - **Primary**: amino acid sequence (peptide bonds)
  - **Secondary**: alpha-helices and beta-pleated sheets (hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms)
  - **Tertiary**: 3D folding of a single polypeptide (R-group interactions: hydrophobic, ionic, disulfide bridges, hydrogen bonds)
  - **Quaternary**: association of multiple polypeptide subunits
- Denaturation: disruption of protein shape (not breaking peptide bonds) due to changes in pH, temperature, or salt concentration

### Topic 1.6: Nucleic Acids

**Learning Objective IST-1.A**: Describe the composition of nucleic acids and their roles in living systems.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.A.1: DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) and RNA (ribonucleic acid) are nucleic acids that carry genetic information.
- DNA nucleotide components: deoxyribose sugar + phosphate group + nitrogenous base (A, T, G, C)
- RNA nucleotide components: ribose sugar + phosphate group + nitrogenous base (A, U, G, C)
- DNA is double-stranded; RNA is typically single-stranded
- Base pairing rules: A-T (DNA) or A-U (RNA), G-C (both)
- Chargaff's rules: in DNA, the amount of adenine equals thymine; the amount of guanine equals cytosine

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Polymer** | A large molecule (macromolecule) composed of many repeating subunits (monomers) linked by covalent bonds |
| **Monomer** | A small molecule that serves as a building block for a larger polymer |
| **Dehydration synthesis** | A chemical reaction that joins two monomers by removing a water molecule (H2O); also called condensation reaction |
| **Hydrolysis** | A chemical reaction that breaks a covalent bond between monomers by adding a water molecule |
| **Hydrogen bond** | A weak attraction between a partially positive hydrogen atom (bonded to an electronegative atom) and a nearby partially negative atom; NOT a covalent or ionic bond |
| **Covalent bond** | A chemical bond formed by the sharing of electrons between two atoms |
| **Ionic bond** | A chemical bond formed by the transfer of electrons from one atom to another, resulting in attraction between oppositely charged ions |
| **Electronegativity** | The tendency of an atom to attract shared electrons toward itself |
| **Polar molecule** | A molecule with an uneven distribution of electrical charge, resulting in partial positive and partial negative regions |
| **Hydrophobic** | "Water-fearing"; describes nonpolar molecules that do not dissolve in water |
| **Hydrophilic** | "Water-loving"; describes polar or charged molecules that dissolve readily in water |
| **Amphipathic** | Having both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions (e.g., phospholipids) |
| **Peptide bond** | A covalent bond between the amino group (-NH2) of one amino acid and the carboxyl group (-COOH) of another, formed by dehydration synthesis |
| **Glycosidic bond** | A covalent bond between two monosaccharides, formed by dehydration synthesis |
| **Phosphodiester bond** | A covalent bond linking nucleotides in nucleic acids, connecting the 3' carbon of one sugar to the 5' carbon of the next via a phosphate group |
| **Denaturation** | The loss of a protein's three-dimensional shape due to disruption of non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions); primary structure (peptide bonds) remains intact |
| **R-group (side chain)** | The variable group attached to the central carbon of an amino acid that determines its chemical properties |
| **Monosaccharide** | A simple sugar (e.g., glucose, fructose, galactose) that is the monomer of carbohydrates |
| **Disaccharide** | A sugar composed of two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond (e.g., sucrose, lactose, maltose) |
| **Polysaccharide** | A polymer of many monosaccharides (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin) |
| **Triglyceride** | A lipid composed of one glycerol molecule bonded to three fatty acid chains via ester bonds |
| **Saturated fatty acid** | A fatty acid with no double bonds between carbon atoms in the hydrocarbon chain; solid at room temperature |
| **Unsaturated fatty acid** | A fatty acid with one or more double bonds in the hydrocarbon chain; liquid at room temperature |
| **Phospholipid** | A lipid with a glycerol backbone, two fatty acid tails (hydrophobic), and a phosphate-containing head group (hydrophilic) |
| **Nucleotide** | The monomer of nucleic acids, composed of a five-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base |
| **Purine** | A double-ringed nitrogenous base (adenine, guanine) |
| **Pyrimidine** | A single-ringed nitrogenous base (cytosine, thymine in DNA, uracil in RNA) |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Water (H2O) vs. Hydrogen Peroxide (H2O2)**: Water is H2O (two hydrogen, one oxygen). Hydrogen peroxide is H2O2 (two hydrogen, two oxygen). Never confuse these. H2O2 is a reactive molecule used by some organisms as a defense mechanism; it is NOT water.

2. **Hydrogen bonds are NOT covalent bonds**: Hydrogen bonds are weak intermolecular attractions, not strong intramolecular covalent bonds. A single hydrogen bond is weak, but collectively many hydrogen bonds provide significant stability.

3. **Lipids are NOT true polymers**: Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, lipids are not built from repeating monomer subunits. They are grouped as macromolecules due to their large size and biological importance.

4. **Denaturation does NOT break peptide bonds**: Denaturation disrupts secondary, tertiary, and quaternary protein structure but leaves the primary structure (amino acid sequence) intact.

5. **Starch vs. Glycogen vs. Cellulose**: All three are polysaccharides made of glucose, but they differ in bonding:
   - Starch (alpha-glucose, alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds): plant energy storage
   - Glycogen (alpha-glucose, alpha-1,4 with alpha-1,6 branches): animal energy storage
   - Cellulose (beta-glucose, beta-1,4 glycosidic bonds): plant cell wall structural component

6. **DNA vs. RNA sugar**: DNA contains deoxyribose (missing one oxygen at the 2' carbon). RNA contains ribose. The "deoxy-" prefix means "lacking oxygen."

7. **Thymine vs. Uracil**: DNA uses thymine (T); RNA uses uracil (U). Both pair with adenine, but they are different molecules. Thymine has a methyl group that uracil lacks.

8. **Saturated vs. unsaturated fats**: Saturated = no double bonds = straight chains = solid at room temperature. Unsaturated = double bonds = kinked chains = liquid at room temperature. Do NOT reverse this.
