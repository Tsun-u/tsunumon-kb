# Unit 6: Gene Expression and Regulation

> AP Exam Weight: 12-16% | ~18-20 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: 1 Evolution · 3 Information Storage & Transmission · 4 Systems Interactions

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 6.1: DNA and RNA Structure

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to compare the structural features of DNA and RNA and explain how these differences relate to each molecule's function.

**Core Concepts:**
- DNA is a double-stranded helix with antiparallel strands running 5'→3' and 3'→5'.
- The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs: adenine-thymine (A-T, 2 hydrogen bonds) and guanine-cytosine (G-C, 3 hydrogen bonds).
- The backbone of each strand consists of alternating sugar (deoxyribose) and phosphate groups linked by phosphodiester bonds.
- RNA is typically single-stranded and contains ribose sugar and uracil instead of thymine.
- Types of RNA: mRNA (messenger), tRNA (transfer), rRNA (ribosomal), and other regulatory RNAs (e.g., miRNA, siRNA).

### Topic 6.2: DNA Replication

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to trace the steps of DNA replication, name the enzymes involved, and distinguish between leading and lagging strand synthesis.

**Core Concepts:**
- DNA replication is **semiconservative**: each daughter molecule contains one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand. This was demonstrated by the Meselson-Stahl experiment.
- Key enzymes and their functions:
  - **Helicase**: Unwinds and separates the double helix at the replication fork.
  - **Topoisomerase**: Relieves tension ahead of the replication fork by cutting and rejoining DNA strands.
  - **Primase**: Synthesizes a short RNA primer to provide a 3'-OH starting point for DNA polymerase.
  - **DNA polymerase III**: Synthesizes new DNA strand by adding nucleotides in the 5'→3' direction. Requires a primer and a template.
  - **DNA polymerase I**: Removes RNA primers and replaces them with DNA.
  - **DNA ligase**: Joins Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand (and seals nicks in the backbone).
- **Leading strand**: Synthesized continuously in the 5'→3' direction toward the replication fork.
- **Lagging strand**: Synthesized discontinuously in short segments called **Okazaki fragments** in the 5'→3' direction away from the replication fork.
- DNA polymerase has proofreading ability (3'→5' exonuclease activity), which corrects errors and ensures high fidelity.
- Telomeres (repetitive sequences at chromosome ends) shorten with each replication in somatic cells. Telomerase (active in germ cells, stem cells, and cancer cells) extends telomeres.

### Topic 6.3: Transcription and RNA Processing

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to outline the transcription process and describe how eukaryotic pre-mRNA is modified before it can be translated.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Transcription** is the synthesis of mRNA from a DNA template, catalyzed by **RNA polymerase**.
- RNA polymerase reads the **template strand** (3'→5') and synthesizes mRNA in the 5'→3' direction. The mRNA sequence is complementary to the template strand and nearly identical to the **coding strand** (except with U instead of T).
- Transcription stages:
  - **Initiation**: RNA polymerase binds to the **promoter** region (e.g., TATA box in eukaryotes) with the help of transcription factors.
  - **Elongation**: RNA polymerase moves along the template, adding RNA nucleotides.
  - **Termination**: RNA polymerase reaches a terminator sequence and releases the mRNA.
- **RNA processing in eukaryotes** (post-transcriptional modifications):
  - **5' cap**: A modified guanine nucleotide added to the 5' end; protects mRNA from degradation and aids ribosome binding.
  - **3' poly-A tail**: A string of adenine nucleotides added to the 3' end; protects from degradation and aids export from nucleus.
  - **RNA splicing**: Introns (non-coding sequences) are removed by spliceosomes; exons (coding sequences) are joined together.
  - **Alternative splicing**: Different combinations of exons can be joined, producing different mRNA variants (and thus different proteins) from the same gene.
- Prokaryotes do NOT have introns (generally) and do NOT perform RNA processing; transcription and translation occur simultaneously.

### Topic 6.4: Translation

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to describe how ribosomes use the sequence of codons on mRNA to assemble a polypeptide, and explain the roles of tRNA and the genetic code in this process.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Translation** is the synthesis of a polypeptide (protein) from an mRNA template by ribosomes.
- The **genetic code** is read in triplets called **codons** (three consecutive nucleotides on mRNA).
  - The genetic code is nearly universal across all life (evidence of common ancestry).
  - The code is **degenerate** (redundant): most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon.
  - There are 64 possible codons: 61 code for amino acids, 3 are stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA).
  - **Start codon**: AUG (codes for methionine; initiates translation).
  - **Stop codons**: UAA, UAG, UGA (do NOT code for any amino acid).
- **tRNA** (transfer RNA) molecules carry specific amino acids to the ribosome. Each tRNA has an **anticodon** that is complementary to the mRNA codon.
- Translation stages:
  - **Initiation**: Small ribosomal subunit binds to mRNA at the start codon (AUG); initiator tRNA (carrying methionine) binds; large subunit joins.
  - **Elongation**: tRNAs bring amino acids to the ribosome; peptide bonds form between adjacent amino acids; ribosome moves along mRNA one codon at a time (5'→3').
  - **Termination**: A stop codon is reached; a release factor binds; the polypeptide is released.
- Ribosomes have three binding sites: A (aminoacyl), P (peptidyl), and E (exit).

### Topic 6.5: Regulation of Gene Expression

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to compare prokaryotic operon-based regulation with the multilevel regulatory strategies found in eukaryotic cells, and explain how each mechanism controls when and how much a gene is expressed.

**Core Concepts:**
- Gene regulation allows cells to control which genes are expressed, when, and to what extent.
- **Prokaryotic gene regulation — Operons:**
  - **Operon**: A cluster of genes under the control of a single promoter, regulated together.
  - **lac operon** (inducible): Genes for lactose metabolism are OFF by default; when lactose is present, it acts as an inducer, removing the repressor and allowing transcription.
  - **trp operon** (repressible): Genes for tryptophan synthesis are ON by default; when tryptophan is abundant, it acts as a corepressor, binding to the repressor and turning off transcription.
- **Eukaryotic gene regulation** occurs at multiple levels:
  - **Epigenetic / Chromatin level**: DNA methylation (adding methyl groups to DNA, typically silences genes) and histone modification (acetylation loosens chromatin → increased transcription; deacetylation tightens chromatin → decreased transcription).
  - **Transcriptional level**: Transcription factors, enhancers, silencers, and promoter accessibility.
  - **Post-transcriptional level**: Alternative splicing, mRNA stability, miRNA and siRNA (which can degrade mRNA or block translation).
  - **Translational level**: Regulation of translation initiation, mRNA lifespan.
  - **Post-translational level**: Protein folding, modification (phosphorylation, glycosylation), ubiquitin tagging for degradation by proteasomes.

### Topic 6.6: Gene Expression and Cell Specialization

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain why cells with identical genomes can develop into structurally and functionally distinct cell types, citing specific examples of differential gene expression.

**Core Concepts:**
- All cells in a multicellular organism contain the same DNA (genome), but different cells express different genes, leading to cell specialization (differentiation).
- Cell differentiation is determined by which genes are turned on or off, not by loss of genetic material.
- Examples: red blood cells express hemoglobin genes; pancreatic beta cells express insulin genes; muscle cells express actin and myosin genes.
- Stem cells are undifferentiated cells capable of developing into specialized cell types.

### Topic 6.7: Mutations

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to distinguish among types of DNA mutations, predict their likely effects on protein structure and function, and evaluate their significance for evolution and heredity.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Point mutations** (substitutions):
  - **Silent mutation**: Nucleotide change does not alter the amino acid (due to redundancy of genetic code).
  - **Missense mutation**: Nucleotide change results in a different amino acid (may or may not affect protein function; e.g., sickle cell: GAG→GTG → glutamic acid→valine).
  - **Nonsense mutation**: Nucleotide change creates a premature stop codon, producing a truncated (usually nonfunctional) protein.
- **Frameshift mutations**:
  - **Insertion**: Addition of one or more nucleotides shifts the reading frame, altering all downstream codons.
  - **Deletion**: Removal of one or more nucleotides shifts the reading frame.
  - Frameshift mutations are generally more harmful than substitutions because they alter every amino acid downstream of the mutation.
- Mutations can be caused by errors in DNA replication, mutagens (chemicals, radiation, UV light), or transposable elements.
- Mutations in somatic cells affect only the individual and are not heritable. Mutations in germ cells (gametes) can be passed to offspring.
- Mutations are a source of genetic variation and provide the raw material for natural selection.

### Topic 6.8: Biotechnology

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain the principles behind key biotechnology tools and evaluate their applications in medicine, agriculture, and forensics.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Gel electrophoresis**: Separates DNA fragments by size using an electric field; smaller fragments move farther through the gel.
- **Polymerase chain reaction (PCR)**: Amplifies (makes many copies of) specific DNA segments using DNA polymerase, primers, and thermal cycling (denaturation, annealing, extension).
- **Restriction enzymes**: Cut DNA at specific recognition sequences; produce sticky ends or blunt ends.
- **Recombinant DNA**: DNA from different sources combined using restriction enzymes and DNA ligase; can be inserted into vectors (plasmids) and introduced into host organisms.
- **Bacterial transformation**: Introduction of foreign DNA into bacterial cells.
- **CRISPR-Cas9**: A gene editing tool that uses a guide RNA to direct the Cas9 enzyme to cut DNA at a specific location, allowing for precise gene modification.
- Applications: GMOs, gene therapy, forensics (DNA fingerprinting), cloning, disease diagnosis.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Gene** | A segment of DNA that contains the instructions for building a specific protein or functional RNA molecule |
| **Codon** | A sequence of three consecutive nucleotides on mRNA that specifies a particular amino acid or stop signal |
| **Anticodon** | A sequence of three nucleotides on tRNA that is complementary to a specific mRNA codon |
| **Template strand** | The strand of DNA that is read by RNA polymerase during transcription (3'→5' direction); also called the antisense strand |
| **Coding strand** | The strand of DNA that has the same sequence as the mRNA (except with T instead of U); also called the sense strand or non-template strand |
| **Promoter** | A specific DNA sequence where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription |
| **Terminator** | A DNA sequence that signals the end of transcription |
| **Intron** | A non-coding segment of a eukaryotic gene that is transcribed but removed (spliced out) before translation |
| **Exon** | A coding segment of a eukaryotic gene that is retained in the mature mRNA after splicing |
| **mRNA (messenger RNA)** | RNA molecule that carries the genetic code from DNA to the ribosome for translation |
| **tRNA (transfer RNA)** | RNA molecule that carries a specific amino acid to the ribosome and has an anticodon that pairs with the mRNA codon |
| **rRNA (ribosomal RNA)** | RNA component of ribosomes; catalyzes peptide bond formation (acts as a ribozyme) |
| **Operon** | A unit of prokaryotic gene regulation consisting of a promoter, operator, and a cluster of functionally related genes transcribed as a single mRNA |
| **Operator** | A DNA sequence in an operon where the repressor protein binds to block transcription |
| **Repressor** | A protein that binds to the operator of an operon and blocks RNA polymerase, preventing transcription |
| **Inducer** | A molecule (e.g., allolactose in the lac operon) that binds to and inactivates a repressor, allowing transcription |
| **Transcription factor** | A protein that binds to DNA regulatory sequences (promoters, enhancers) to regulate the initiation of transcription in eukaryotes |
| **Enhancer** | A distal DNA regulatory sequence that, when bound by activator proteins, increases transcription of a gene |
| **Silencer** | A DNA regulatory sequence that, when bound by repressor proteins, decreases transcription of a gene |
| **DNA methylation** | The addition of methyl groups (-CH3) to DNA (usually cytosine bases), typically silencing gene expression |
| **Histone acetylation** | The addition of acetyl groups to histone proteins, which loosens chromatin and promotes gene expression |
| **Epigenetics** | Heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve changes to the DNA sequence itself (e.g., methylation, histone modifications) |
| **Alternative splicing** | The process by which different combinations of exons from the same pre-mRNA are joined, producing different mature mRNAs and protein variants |
| **Point mutation** | A change in a single nucleotide in the DNA sequence |
| **Frameshift mutation** | An insertion or deletion of nucleotides (not in multiples of three) that shifts the reading frame of codons downstream |
| **Mutagen** | An agent (chemical, radiation, or biological) that causes mutations in DNA |
| **Restriction enzyme** | A bacterial enzyme that cuts DNA at specific recognition sequences |
| **PCR (polymerase chain reaction)** | A laboratory technique that amplifies specific DNA sequences through repeated cycles of denaturation, primer annealing, and DNA synthesis |
| **Gel electrophoresis** | A technique that separates DNA, RNA, or protein molecules by size using an electric field through a gel matrix |
| **Plasmid** | A small, circular, extrachromosomal DNA molecule in bacteria; used as a vector in recombinant DNA technology |
| **CRISPR-Cas9** | A gene editing system using a guide RNA to direct Cas9 nuclease to a specific DNA sequence for cutting and modification |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **DNA replication is SEMICONSERVATIVE, not conservative or dispersive**: Each daughter DNA molecule has one old strand and one new strand. This was proven by Meselson and Stahl.

2. **RNA polymerase reads the TEMPLATE strand (3'→5'), not the coding strand**: The mRNA produced is complementary to the template strand and has the same sequence as the coding strand (with U instead of T).

3. **Introns are NOT "junk DNA"**: While introns are removed from mRNA, they play regulatory roles and enable alternative splicing, which increases protein diversity.

4. **The genetic code is DEGENERATE, not ambiguous**: Multiple codons can code for the same amino acid (degeneracy), but each codon specifies only ONE amino acid (no ambiguity).

5. **Transcription occurs in the NUCLEUS; translation occurs in the CYTOPLASM (in eukaryotes)**: In prokaryotes, both processes can occur simultaneously since there is no nuclear membrane.

6. **Silent mutations DO change the DNA sequence**: A silent mutation changes a nucleotide but does NOT change the amino acid (due to codon degeneracy). The DNA IS different; the protein is the same.

7. **Not all mutations are harmful**: Mutations can be neutral (silent), beneficial (providing selective advantage), or harmful. They are the source of all genetic variation.

8. **DNA polymerase can only synthesize in the 5'→3' direction**: It CANNOT synthesize in the 3'→5' direction. This is why the lagging strand must be synthesized in Okazaki fragments.

9. **Epigenetic changes do NOT alter the DNA sequence**: Methylation and histone modification change gene expression without changing the nucleotide sequence of DNA. However, some epigenetic marks can be heritable.

10. **The lac operon is INDUCIBLE; the trp operon is REPRESSIBLE**: Do not confuse these. The lac operon is normally OFF and is induced (turned ON) by lactose. The trp operon is normally ON and is repressed (turned OFF) when tryptophan is abundant.
