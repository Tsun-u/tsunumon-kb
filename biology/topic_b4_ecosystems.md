# B4: Form and Function of Ecosystems

> Theme B: Form and Function | Level 4: Ecosystems

## Subtopics

### B4.1 Adaptation to Environment (SL + HL)

- **Adaptation**: inherited characteristic that increases an organism's fitness in a particular environment
- Types of adaptation:
  - **Structural**: physical features (e.g., thick fur, deep roots, camouflage)
  - **Behavioral**: actions that improve survival (e.g., migration, nocturnal activity)
  - **Physiological**: internal body processes (e.g., venom production, hibernation metabolism)
- Adaptations to specific environments:
  - Xerophytes: adaptations to dry conditions (reduced leaves, thick cuticle, sunken stomata, CAM/C4 photosynthesis)
  - Hydrophytes: adaptations to aquatic conditions (aerenchyma, flexible stems, reduced root systems)
  - Halophytes: salt-tolerant plants
- Animal adaptations: thermoregulation (endotherms vs. ectotherms), counter-current heat exchangers

#### HL Extension -- B4.1
- Allen's rule and Bergmann's rule (body size and extremity length vs. climate)
- Detailed analysis of convergent evolution as evidence for adaptation

### B4.2 Ecological Niches (SL + HL)

- **Ecological niche**: the role and position of a species in its ecosystem
  - Includes habitat, diet, activity patterns, interactions with other species
- **Fundamental niche** vs. **realised niche**: potential range vs. actual range (limited by competition)
- **Competitive exclusion principle**: two species cannot occupy the same niche in the same habitat indefinitely
- **Character displacement**: divergence of traits in sympatric species to reduce competition
- **Keystone species**: species with disproportionate impact on ecosystem structure
- Niche partitioning: species divide resources to coexist

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Adaptation** | Inherited characteristic that enhances survival and reproduction in a specific environment |
| **Xerophyte** | Plant adapted to survive in dry conditions |
| **Ecological niche** | The role, position, and set of conditions a species occupies in an ecosystem |
| **Fundamental niche** | Full range of conditions and resources a species could potentially occupy |
| **Realised niche** | Actual range occupied, limited by interspecific competition |
| **Competitive exclusion** | Principle that two species competing for the same niche cannot coexist indefinitely |
| **Keystone species** | Species whose removal would cause disproportionate changes to ecosystem structure |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Organisms adapt during their lifetime"** -- Adaptations are inherited; individuals do not evolve. Acclimation is different from adaptation.
2. **"Every trait is an adaptation"** -- Some traits are neutral or even vestigial; not all features are adaptive.
3. **"Ecological niche = habitat"** -- Niche includes all aspects of how a species interacts with its environment, not just where it lives.
4. **"Competition always leads to extinction"** -- Competition can drive niche partitioning and coexistence.
