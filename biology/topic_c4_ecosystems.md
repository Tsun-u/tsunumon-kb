# C4: Interaction and Interdependence -- Ecosystems

> Theme C: Interaction and Interdependence | Level 4: Ecosystems

## Subtopics

### C4.1 Populations and Communities (SL + HL)

- **Population**: group of organisms of the same species living in the same area
- Population dynamics:
  - Natality (birth rate), mortality (death rate), immigration, emigration
  - Exponential growth (J-curve) vs. logistic growth (S-curve)
  - **Carrying capacity** ($K$): maximum population size an environment can sustain
  - Limiting factors: density-dependent (competition, predation, disease) and density-independent (weather, natural disasters)
- Sampling techniques:
  - Quadrat sampling for sessile organisms
  - Mark-recapture (Lincoln index) for mobile organisms: $N = \frac{n_1 \times n_2}{n_m}$
- **Community**: all populations of different species in an area
- Species interactions:
  - **Predation**: predator-prey cycles
  - **Competition**: intraspecific (within species) and interspecific (between species)
  - **Mutualism**: both species benefit (e.g., mycorrhizae, pollination)
  - **Parasitism**: one benefits at the expense of the other
- **Ecological succession**: change in community composition over time
  - Primary succession: colonization of bare substrate (e.g., volcanic rock)
  - Secondary succession: re-establishment after disturbance (e.g., after fire)

#### HL Extension -- C4.1
- Population growth models (exponential and logistic equations)
- r-strategists vs. K-strategists
- Top-down vs. bottom-up control of populations

### C4.2 Transfers of Energy and Matter (SL + HL)

- **Food chains and food webs**: represent energy flow through trophic levels
  - Producers → primary consumers → secondary consumers → tertiary consumers
  - Decomposers break down dead organic matter at all levels
- **Energy flow**:
  - Energy enters ecosystems as sunlight; only ~1--2% is captured by producers
  - Energy is lost at each trophic level (~10% efficiency) as heat from respiration
  - Energy pyramids: always upright (less energy at each successive level)
- **Nutrient cycling**: matter is recycled, energy is not
  - **Carbon cycle**: photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, decomposition
  - **Nitrogen cycle**: nitrogen fixation, nitrification, denitrification, assimilation
- **Biomass**: total mass of organic matter; pyramids of biomass
- Gross primary productivity (GPP) and net primary productivity (NPP): $NPP = GPP - R$

#### HL Extension -- C4.2
- Detailed biogeochemical cycles (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus)
- Calculation of efficiency of energy transfers between trophic levels
- Impact of human activities on nutrient cycles

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Carrying capacity ($K$)** | Maximum population size sustainable by the available resources |
| **Trophic level** | Position of an organism in a food chain (producer, primary consumer, etc.) |
| **Biomass** | Total mass of living organic matter in an area or trophic level |
| **GPP** | Gross primary productivity: total energy fixed by producers |
| **NPP** | Net primary productivity: energy remaining after producer respiration ($GPP - R$) |
| **Ecological succession** | Gradual change in species composition of a community over time |
| **Nitrogen fixation** | Conversion of atmospheric N2 to ammonia by bacteria |
| **Lincoln index** | Formula for estimating population size using mark-recapture data |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Energy is recycled in ecosystems"** -- Energy flows through ecosystems and is lost as heat; only matter is recycled.
2. **"Top predators have the most energy"** -- Top predators have the least energy; energy is lost at each trophic level.
3. **"Decomposers are at the end of the food chain"** -- Decomposers operate at all trophic levels, breaking down dead matter.
4. **"Succession always ends in a forest"** -- The climax community depends on the environment (could be grassland, desert, tundra).
5. **"Populations always grow to carrying capacity"** -- External factors can keep populations well below $K$.
