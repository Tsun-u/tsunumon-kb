# A2: Unity and Diversity of Cells

> Theme A: Unity and Diversity | Level 2: Cells

## Subtopics

### A2.1 Origins of Cells (HL only)

- Conditions on early Earth and the origin of organic molecules
- Miller--Urey experiment: abiotic synthesis of amino acids
- RNA world hypothesis: RNA as both information carrier and catalyst (ribozymes)
- Endosymbiotic theory:
  - Mitochondria evolved from aerobic prokaryotes engulfed by ancestral eukaryotes
  - Chloroplasts evolved from photosynthetic cyanobacteria
  - Evidence: double membranes, own DNA (circular), 70S ribosomes, binary fission
- Cell theory: all cells come from pre-existing cells (biogenesis)
- Spontaneous generation vs. biogenesis (Pasteur's experiment)

### A2.2 Cell Structure (SL + HL)

- **Prokaryotic cells**: no membrane-bound nucleus; features include:
  - Nucleoid region (naked circular DNA), ribosomes (70S), cell wall (peptidoglycan)
  - Plasmids, pili, flagella, capsule (in some)
- **Eukaryotic cells**: membrane-bound nucleus and organelles
  - Nucleus, rough/smooth ER, Golgi apparatus, mitochondria, lysosomes
  - Plant cells additionally: chloroplasts, cell wall (cellulose), large central vacuole
- Cell size and surface area to volume ratio
  - As cell size increases, SA:V ratio decreases
  - Limits on cell size due to diffusion constraints
- Ultrastructure comparison: prokaryotic vs. eukaryotic cells
- Light microscopy vs. electron microscopy (TEM, SEM)

#### HL Extension -- A2.2
- Detailed organelle ultrastructure and functions
- Free ribosomes vs. bound ribosomes
- Cytoskeleton: microtubules, microfilaments, intermediate filaments

### A2.3 Viruses (HL only)

- Virus structure: nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) enclosed in a protein capsid
- Viruses are **not considered living** (no metabolism, no independent reproduction)
- Obligate intracellular parasites: require host cell machinery
- Bacteriophage structure and lytic cycle
- Retroviruses: RNA genome, reverse transcriptase (e.g., HIV)
- Viral diversity: enveloped vs. non-enveloped, DNA vs. RNA viruses

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Prokaryote** | Organism whose cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus (Bacteria, Archaea) |
| **Eukaryote** | Organism whose cells have a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles |
| **Endosymbiosis** | Theory that mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as engulfed prokaryotes |
| **Nucleoid** | Region in prokaryotic cells where circular DNA is concentrated |
| **Capsid** | Protein coat surrounding the nucleic acid of a virus |
| **Retrovirus** | RNA virus that uses reverse transcriptase to make DNA from its RNA genome |
| **Ribozyme** | RNA molecule with catalytic (enzymatic) activity |
| **SA:V ratio** | Surface area to volume ratio; decreases as cell size increases |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Prokaryotes are primitive and simple"** -- Prokaryotes are highly adapted and diverse; "simple" structure does not mean less evolved.
2. **"Viruses are living organisms"** -- Viruses lack metabolism and cannot reproduce independently; they are at the boundary of life.
3. **"All bacteria are harmful"** -- Most bacteria are harmless or beneficial (e.g., gut microbiome, nitrogen fixation).
4. **"Endosymbiosis is just a hypothesis"** -- Extensive evidence (double membranes, own DNA, 70S ribosomes) strongly supports the endosymbiotic theory.
5. **"Bigger cells are better"** -- Increased cell size reduces SA:V ratio, making diffusion less efficient.
