# Unit 7: Natural Selection

> AP Exam Weight: 13-20% | ~16-18 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: EVO, SYI, IST

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 7.1: Introduction to Natural Selection

**Learning Objective EVO-1.B**: Describe the conditions under which natural selection occurs.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-1.B.1: Natural selection acts on phenotypic variation within a population.
- EVO-1.B.2: Conditions required for natural selection:
  1. **Variation**: Individuals in a population differ in their traits.
  2. **Heritability**: The variation must be genetically heritable (at least partially).
  3. **Differential reproduction**: Individuals with favorable traits survive and reproduce more successfully (higher fitness).
  4. **Overproduction**: Populations produce more offspring than the environment can support.
- EVO-1.B.3: Natural selection acts on individuals, but evolution occurs in populations.

### Topic 7.2: Natural Selection

**Learning Objective EVO-1.C**: Explain how natural selection affects populations.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-1.C.1: Three modes of natural selection:
  - **Directional selection**: Favors one extreme phenotype, shifting the population mean in one direction (e.g., antibiotic resistance in bacteria).
  - **Stabilizing selection**: Favors intermediate phenotypes, reducing variation (e.g., human birth weight).
  - **Disruptive selection**: Favors both extreme phenotypes over the intermediate, potentially leading to speciation (e.g., beak size in black-bellied seedcrackers).
- EVO-1.C.2: **Sexual selection**: A form of natural selection where individuals with certain traits are more likely to obtain mates (e.g., peacock tail plumage, elk antler size).
  - Intersexual selection: mate choice (usually female choosing male)
  - Intrasexual selection: competition between members of the same sex

### Topic 7.3: Artificial Selection

**Learning Objective EVO-1.D**: Describe the role of artificial selection in the development of organisms.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-1.D.1: Artificial selection (selective breeding) is the intentional reproduction of individuals with desirable traits by humans.
- EVO-1.D.2: Examples: domestication of dogs from wolves, crop plants (corn from teosinte), livestock breeding.
- EVO-1.D.3: Artificial selection demonstrates that heritable variation exists within populations and can be acted upon to change populations over time, supporting the concept of natural selection.

### Topic 7.4: Population Genetics

**Learning Objective EVO-1.E**: Describe the factors that can affect allele frequencies in a population.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-1.E.1: Evolution is defined as a change in allele frequencies in a population over time.
- EVO-1.E.2: Mechanisms of evolution (factors that change allele frequencies):
  - **Natural selection**: Differential survival and reproduction based on fitness.
  - **Genetic drift**: Random changes in allele frequencies, most significant in small populations.
    - **Bottleneck effect**: A drastic reduction in population size (e.g., natural disaster) reduces genetic diversity.
    - **Founder effect**: A small group colonizes a new area, carrying only a subset of the original population's genetic diversity.
  - **Gene flow (migration)**: The movement of alleles between populations through migration of individuals; tends to reduce differences between populations.
  - **Mutation**: Random changes in DNA that introduce new alleles into a population; the ultimate source of all genetic variation.
  - **Sexual selection**: Nonrandom mating based on phenotypic traits.

### Topic 7.5: Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

**Learning Objective EVO-1.F**: Describe the conditions required for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium and use the Hardy-Weinberg equations to analyze population genetics.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-1.F.1: **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium** describes a non-evolving population where allele and genotype frequencies remain constant across generations.
- EVO-1.F.2: Five conditions required (if ANY is violated, the population may evolve):
  1. No mutations
  2. Random mating
  3. No natural selection (all genotypes equally fit)
  4. Very large population size (no genetic drift)
  5. No gene flow (no migration in or out)
- EVO-1.F.3: **Hardy-Weinberg equations**:
  - Allele frequencies: **p + q = 1** (where p = frequency of dominant allele, q = frequency of recessive allele)
  - Genotype frequencies: **p² + 2pq + q² = 1** (where p² = homozygous dominant, 2pq = heterozygous, q² = homozygous recessive)
- EVO-1.F.4: The Hardy-Weinberg model serves as a null hypothesis; deviations from expected frequencies indicate that evolution is occurring.

### Topic 7.6: Evidence of Evolution

**Learning Objective EVO-1.G**: Describe the types of evidence that support the theory of evolution.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-1.G.1: Lines of evidence for evolution:
  - **Fossil record**: Shows changes in organisms over time; transitional fossils (e.g., Tiktaalik, Archaeopteryx) demonstrate intermediate forms.
  - **Comparative anatomy**:
    - **Homologous structures**: Structures with similar underlying anatomy but different functions (e.g., human arm, whale flipper, bat wing); evidence of common ancestry.
    - **Analogous structures**: Structures with similar function but different anatomy (e.g., bird wing vs. insect wing); evidence of convergent evolution, NOT common ancestry.
    - **Vestigial structures**: Reduced or nonfunctional structures inherited from ancestors (e.g., human appendix, whale pelvis bones).
  - **Molecular biology / Biochemistry**: DNA and protein sequence similarities; more closely related organisms share more similar sequences. All life uses the same genetic code (near-universal).
  - **Biogeography**: Geographic distribution of species reflects evolutionary history (e.g., island species, continental drift patterns).
  - **Embryology**: Similar embryonic development patterns among related organisms (e.g., pharyngeal pouches in vertebrate embryos).
  - **Direct observation**: Antibiotic resistance in bacteria, pesticide resistance in insects, changes in beak size in Darwin's finches.

### Topic 7.7: Common Ancestry

**Learning Objective EVO-2.A**: Describe the evidence for common ancestry of all living organisms.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-2.A.1: All living organisms share a common ancestor, supported by:
  - Universal genetic code (DNA → RNA → protein)
  - Shared metabolic pathways (glycolysis, citric acid cycle)
  - Conserved genes across diverse species (e.g., Hox genes, cytochrome c)
  - Similar cell structures (plasma membranes, ribosomes)
- EVO-2.A.2: Homologous structures and molecular homologies (shared DNA/protein sequences) provide evidence of divergence from a common ancestor.

### Topic 7.8: Continuing Evolution

**Learning Objective EVO-2.B**: Describe how evolution continues to occur.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-2.B.1: Evolution is an ongoing process. Examples include:
  - Antibiotic resistance in bacteria (MRSA)
  - Pesticide resistance in agricultural pests
  - Changes in allele frequencies in real-time populations (e.g., Darwin's finches beak size, peppered moths)
- EVO-2.B.2: Coevolution: two or more species exert selective pressure on each other, driving reciprocal evolutionary change (e.g., predator-prey, pollinator-flower relationships).

### Topic 7.9: Phylogeny

**Learning Objective EVO-2.C**: Describe how phylogenetic trees and cladograms represent evolutionary relationships.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-2.C.1: A **phylogenetic tree** (cladogram) is a branching diagram showing evolutionary relationships among organisms based on shared derived characteristics.
- EVO-2.C.2: Key features:
  - **Node**: A point where a lineage splits (represents a common ancestor).
  - **Branch**: Represents a lineage evolving over time.
  - **Root**: The common ancestor of all organisms in the tree.
  - **Sister taxa**: Two groups that share the most recent common ancestor.
  - **Outgroup**: A taxon outside the group of interest, used for comparison.
- EVO-2.C.3: Cladograms are constructed using **shared derived characters** (synapomorphies) — traits that evolved in the common ancestor of a clade.
- EVO-2.C.4: Molecular data (DNA sequences, protein sequences) can be used to construct and refine phylogenetic trees.

### Topic 7.10: Speciation

**Learning Objective EVO-3.A**: Describe the process of speciation.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-3.A.1: **Speciation** is the formation of new species from an ancestral population.
- EVO-3.A.2: **Biological species concept**: A species is defined as a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring, and are reproductively isolated from other groups.
- EVO-3.A.3: Types of speciation:
  - **Allopatric speciation**: Geographic isolation separates populations, which then diverge genetically (e.g., Grand Canyon squirrels, Darwin's finches).
  - **Sympatric speciation**: Speciation occurs within the same geographic area, often through polyploidy (especially in plants), habitat differentiation, or sexual selection.
- EVO-3.A.4: **Reproductive isolating mechanisms**:
  - **Prezygotic barriers** (prevent mating or fertilization): temporal isolation, habitat isolation, behavioral isolation, mechanical isolation, gametic isolation.
  - **Postzygotic barriers** (prevent viable/fertile offspring): reduced hybrid viability, reduced hybrid fertility, hybrid breakdown.

### Topic 7.11: Extinction

**Learning Objective EVO-3.B**: Describe the causes and consequences of extinction.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-3.B.1: Extinction occurs when a species can no longer survive and reproduce in its environment.
- EVO-3.B.2: Causes: environmental change, habitat destruction, competition, predation, disease, catastrophic events (e.g., asteroid impact).
- EVO-3.B.3: Mass extinctions have occurred multiple times in Earth's history (e.g., end-Permian, end-Cretaceous) and have been followed by adaptive radiations.
- EVO-3.B.4: **Adaptive radiation**: The diversification of a species into multiple new species that occupy different ecological niches, often following a mass extinction or colonization of a new environment (e.g., Darwin's finches, Hawaiian honeycreepers, mammalian radiation after dinosaur extinction).

### Topic 7.12: Variations in Populations

**Learning Objective SYI-3.A**: Describe the role of genetic variation in populations.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- SYI-3.A.1: Genetic diversity within a population is essential for adaptation and survival.
- SYI-3.A.2: Sources of genetic variation: mutation, sexual reproduction (crossing over, independent assortment, random fertilization), and gene flow.
- SYI-3.A.3: Populations with greater genetic diversity are more likely to have individuals that can survive environmental changes.

### Topic 7.13: Origins of Life on Earth

**Learning Objective EVO-1.H**: Describe scientific evidence for the origin of life on Earth.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-1.H.1: Earth is approximately 4.6 billion years old; the earliest evidence of life dates to ~3.5 billion years ago.
- EVO-1.H.2: The Miller-Urey experiment (1953) demonstrated that organic molecules (amino acids) could form under conditions simulating early Earth's atmosphere.
- EVO-1.H.3: **RNA world hypothesis**: RNA may have been the first self-replicating molecule, serving as both genetic material and catalyst (ribozyme) before DNA and proteins evolved.
- EVO-1.H.4: Key events in the history of life:
  - Prokaryotes were the first organisms.
  - Photosynthetic cyanobacteria produced oxygen, leading to the Great Oxidation Event.
  - Eukaryotes evolved through endosymbiosis (mitochondria and chloroplasts from engulfed prokaryotes).
  - Multicellularity evolved independently in multiple lineages.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Evolution** | A change in the allele frequencies of a population over successive generations |
| **Natural selection** | The process by which individuals with favorable heritable traits are more likely to survive, reproduce, and pass those traits to the next generation |
| **Fitness** | The relative ability of an individual to survive and reproduce in a particular environment; measured by reproductive success |
| **Adaptation** | A heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness in a particular environment; also the process by which populations become better suited to their environment |
| **Directional selection** | Natural selection that favors one extreme phenotype, shifting the population distribution in one direction |
| **Stabilizing selection** | Natural selection that favors intermediate phenotypes, reducing phenotypic variation |
| **Disruptive selection** | Natural selection that favors both extreme phenotypes over the intermediate, increasing phenotypic variation |
| **Sexual selection** | A form of natural selection in which individuals with certain traits are more likely to obtain mates |
| **Genetic drift** | Random changes in allele frequencies in a population, most significant in small populations; NOT driven by fitness |
| **Bottleneck effect** | A drastic, non-selective reduction in population size that reduces genetic diversity |
| **Founder effect** | The reduced genetic diversity that results when a small subgroup establishes a new, isolated population |
| **Gene flow** | The transfer of alleles between populations through the migration of individuals or their gametes |
| **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium** | A theoretical state in which allele and genotype frequencies remain constant in a population that is not evolving |
| **Allele frequency** | The proportion of a specific allele relative to all alleles for that gene in a population |
| **Homologous structures** | Structures in different species that share a common underlying anatomy due to descent from a common ancestor, regardless of current function |
| **Analogous structures** | Structures in different species that have similar function but different evolutionary origins; result from convergent evolution |
| **Vestigial structure** | A reduced or nonfunctional structure that is homologous to a functional structure in a related species or ancestor |
| **Convergent evolution** | The independent evolution of similar features in unrelated lineages, driven by similar selective pressures |
| **Divergent evolution** | The accumulation of differences between closely related populations, often leading to speciation |
| **Coevolution** | The reciprocal evolutionary change in interacting species, each exerting selective pressure on the other |
| **Phylogenetic tree** | A branching diagram showing the inferred evolutionary relationships among organisms based on shared characteristics |
| **Cladogram** | A phylogenetic diagram that groups organisms by shared derived characteristics (synapomorphies) |
| **Clade** | A group consisting of an ancestor and all of its descendants; a monophyletic group |
| **Synapomorphy** | A shared derived character that distinguishes a clade from other organisms |
| **Speciation** | The process by which one species splits into two or more separate species |
| **Allopatric speciation** | Speciation resulting from geographic isolation of subpopulations |
| **Sympatric speciation** | Speciation occurring within a single geographic area, without physical barriers |
| **Reproductive isolation** | The inability of members of two populations to interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring |
| **Prezygotic barrier** | A reproductive barrier that prevents mating or fertilization between species |
| **Postzygotic barrier** | A reproductive barrier that occurs after fertilization, resulting in inviable or infertile hybrid offspring |
| **Adaptive radiation** | The rapid diversification of a single ancestral lineage into many species that occupy different ecological niches |
| **Polyploidy** | A condition in which an organism has more than two complete sets of chromosomes; a common mechanism of sympatric speciation in plants |
| **Extinction** | The complete disappearance of a species; occurs when the last individual of that species dies |
| **RNA world hypothesis** | The hypothesis that RNA was the first self-replicating molecule, acting as both genetic material and enzyme before DNA and proteins evolved |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Evolution is NOT goal-directed or intentional**: Organisms do NOT evolve "in order to" adapt. Evolution has no direction or purpose. Natural selection acts on existing variation; it does not create variation on demand.

2. **Individuals do NOT evolve; POPULATIONS evolve**: An individual organism cannot change its alleles during its lifetime. Evolution is measured as changes in allele frequencies within a population over generations.

3. **"Survival of the fittest" does NOT mean "survival of the strongest"**: Fitness in biology means reproductive success — the ability to survive AND produce viable offspring. A "fit" organism is one that passes its genes to the next generation.

4. **Natural selection is NOT the only mechanism of evolution**: Genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, and sexual selection also change allele frequencies. Genetic drift can cause evolution in small populations even without selection.

5. **Homologous structures indicate COMMON ANCESTRY; analogous structures indicate CONVERGENT EVOLUTION**: Bat wings and human arms are homologous (same bone structure, different function). Bat wings and insect wings are analogous (similar function, different structure). Do NOT confuse these.

6. **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium is a THEORETICAL ideal, not a real state**: No natural population perfectly meets all five conditions. It serves as a null hypothesis against which to detect evolution.

7. **Mutations are RANDOM and not directed by environmental need**: Bacteria do not "develop" antibiotic resistance in response to antibiotics. Rather, random mutations that confer resistance already exist in the population, and antibiotics select for those resistant individuals.

8. **"Missing links" is a misleading term**: The fossil record contains many transitional fossils (e.g., Tiktaalik). Evolution produces a branching tree, not a linear chain. Every fossil is a "link."

9. **Speciation requires REPRODUCTIVE ISOLATION, not just geographic separation**: Geographic separation (allopatric speciation) is one mechanism, but reproductive isolation (inability to interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring) is the defining criterion for speciation.

10. **Genetic drift is NOT the same as natural selection**: Genetic drift is random and does not involve fitness differences. It is most significant in small populations. Natural selection is non-random and driven by fitness differences.
