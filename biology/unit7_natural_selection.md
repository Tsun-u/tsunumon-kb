# Unit 7: Natural Selection

> AP Exam Weight: 13-20% | ~19-21 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: 1 Evolution · 3 Information Storage & Transmission · 4 Systems Interactions

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 7.1: Introduction to Natural Selection

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to identify and explain the necessary conditions that allow natural selection to operate within a population.

**Core Concepts:**
- Natural selection operates on observable physical traits (phenotypes), not directly on genes.
- Four prerequisites must be in place for natural selection to work:
  1. **Variation**: Members of a population must differ from one another in at least some heritable traits.
  2. **Heritability**: Trait differences must have a genetic basis so they can be passed to offspring.
  3. **Differential reproduction**: Individuals whose traits give them an advantage must leave more offspring (higher fitness) than those without the advantage.
  4. **Overproduction**: More offspring must be born than the environment can support, creating competition for survival.
- Selection acts on individual organisms; evolutionary change is measured at the population level.

### Topic 7.2: Natural Selection

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to distinguish among the different modes of natural selection and predict how each mode shifts the distribution of phenotypes in a population.

**Core Concepts:**
- Three patterns of selection alter phenotype distributions differently:
  - **Directional selection**: Shifts the population toward one extreme phenotype (e.g., increasing antibiotic resistance in bacterial populations).
  - **Stabilizing selection**: Culls both extremes, narrowing variation around the intermediate phenotype (e.g., human newborn birth weight).
  - **Disruptive selection**: Favors individuals at both extremes over those in the middle, potentially splitting a population into two distinct groups (e.g., beak size variation in black-bellied seedcrackers).
- **Sexual selection**: A special case of natural selection in which traits influence mating success rather than direct survival (e.g., peacock tail plumage, elk antler size).
  - Intersexual selection: one sex (usually females) actively chooses among potential mates
  - Intrasexual selection: members of the same sex compete directly with each other for mating opportunities

### Topic 7.3: Artificial Selection

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain how human-directed selective breeding demonstrates the principles of natural selection and heritable variation.

**Core Concepts:**
- Artificial selection (selective breeding) is when humans deliberately choose which individuals reproduce based on desired traits.
- Historical and modern examples include: the domestication of dogs from wolf ancestors, the transformation of teosinte into modern corn, and livestock improvement programs.
- Artificial selection provides a clear demonstration that heritable variation exists and that selecting for specific traits can shift population characteristics across generations — directly paralleling how natural selection operates.

### Topic 7.4: Population Genetics

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to identify the mechanisms that drive changes in allele frequencies and explain how each mechanism produces evolutionary change.

**Core Concepts:**
- At the population level, evolution is defined as a measurable shift in allele frequencies across generations.
- Five primary mechanisms alter allele frequencies:
  - **Natural selection**: Non-random survival and reproduction based on trait-linked fitness differences.
  - **Genetic drift**: Chance fluctuations in allele frequencies, which have the greatest impact in small populations.
    - **Bottleneck effect**: A sudden, severe reduction in population size (e.g., from a natural disaster) sharply cuts genetic diversity.
    - **Founder effect**: When a small subset of a population colonizes a new area, they carry only a fraction of the original genetic diversity, causing allele frequencies to differ from the source population.
  - **Gene flow**: The movement of alleles between populations as individuals migrate; generally homogenizes populations and reduces divergence.
  - **Mutation**: Heritable changes in DNA sequences; the only mechanism that introduces entirely new alleles into a gene pool.
  - **Sexual selection**: Mate-choice patterns that favor certain phenotypes, leading to non-random mating and allele frequency shifts.

### Topic 7.5: Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to state the conditions required for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, apply the Hardy-Weinberg equations to calculate expected allele and genotype frequencies, and use deviations from these expectations to infer whether a population is evolving.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium** is a mathematical baseline describing a population in which allele and genotype frequencies do not change from one generation to the next — i.e., a non-evolving population.
- Five conditions must all be met simultaneously to maintain equilibrium (violation of any one allows evolution to occur):
  1. No new mutations
  2. Completely random mating
  3. No natural selection (all genotypes produce the same number of surviving offspring)
  4. Very large (effectively infinite) population size (no genetic drift)
  5. No gene flow (the population is isolated from others)
- **Hardy-Weinberg equations**:
  - Allele frequencies: **p + q = 1** (p = frequency of dominant allele; q = frequency of recessive allele)
  - Genotype frequencies: **p² + 2pq + q² = 1** (p² = homozygous dominant; 2pq = heterozygous; q² = homozygous recessive)
- The Hardy-Weinberg model functions as a null hypothesis: if observed genotype frequencies differ significantly from Hardy-Weinberg predictions, one or more evolutionary forces are at work.

### Topic 7.6: Evidence of Evolution

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to evaluate multiple independent lines of scientific evidence that collectively support the theory of evolution by natural selection.

**Core Concepts:**
- Several independent fields of science converge on the same conclusion — that life has changed over time through descent with modification:
  - **Fossil record**: Preserved remains and traces document changes in life forms over geological time; transitional fossils (e.g., Tiktaalik, Archaeopteryx) capture intermediate stages between ancestral and derived forms.
  - **Comparative anatomy**:
    - **Homologous structures**: Anatomically similar structures across species that reflect inheritance from a shared ancestor, even when their current functions differ (e.g., human arm, whale flipper, bat wing).
    - **Analogous structures**: Structures that serve similar functions in different species but arose independently through convergent evolution, not from a common ancestor (e.g., bird wing vs. insect wing).
    - **Vestigial structures**: Remnants of structures that were functional in ancestors but have been reduced or lost in function in descendants (e.g., human appendix, pelvic bones in whales).
  - **Molecular biology and biochemistry**: All living things share the same genetic code; more closely related species have more similar DNA and protein sequences. Conserved genes (e.g., cytochrome c) span vastly different organisms.
  - **Biogeography**: The geographic distribution of species mirrors evolutionary history and continental drift patterns (e.g., unique fauna on islands, marsupial distribution in Australia).
  - **Embryology**: Closely related vertebrates pass through similar embryonic stages (e.g., pharyngeal pouches appear in all vertebrate embryos), reflecting shared developmental heritage.
  - **Direct observation**: Evolution has been observed in real time — antibiotic resistance in bacteria, pesticide resistance in insects, and documented changes in beak sizes of Darwin's finches following drought.

### Topic 7.7: Common Ancestry

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to use molecular and structural evidence to support the conclusion that all life on Earth shares a common ancestor.

**Core Concepts:**
- Multiple converging lines of evidence indicate that all living organisms are related through descent from a single common ancestor:
  - A universal genetic code (DNA → RNA → protein) operates in virtually every organism on Earth.
  - Core metabolic pathways (glycolysis, the citric acid cycle) are shared across all domains of life.
  - Highly conserved genes such as Hox genes and the cytochrome c gene appear across distantly related lineages.
  - Fundamental cell structures — plasma membranes, ribosomes — are shared by all known life.
- Homologous anatomical structures and molecular homologies (similarities in DNA and protein sequences) reveal divergence from shared ancestral forms.

### Topic 7.8: Continuing Evolution

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to provide evidence that evolution is an ongoing process and explain how coevolution drives reciprocal change in interacting species.

**Core Concepts:**
- Evolution does not stop — populations continue to change in response to selective pressures. Current examples include:
  - The rise of methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) through antibiotic selection
  - Development of pesticide resistance in agricultural pest populations
  - Measurable shifts in allele frequencies in Darwin's finch populations following environmental change, and the classic peppered moth case
- **Coevolution**: When two or more interacting species each exert selective pressure on the other, driving coordinated evolutionary change in both (e.g., predator-prey arms races, reciprocal adaptations between pollinators and flowering plants).

### Topic 7.9: Phylogeny

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to interpret phylogenetic trees and cladograms to determine evolutionary relationships, identify common ancestors, and explain how molecular and morphological data are used to construct them.

**Core Concepts:**
- A **phylogenetic tree** (or cladogram) is a branching diagram that depicts inferred evolutionary relationships among taxa based on shared derived characteristics.
- Structural components and their meanings:
  - **Node**: A branching point representing a hypothetical common ancestor of the lineages that diverge from it.
  - **Branch**: A lineage evolving through time; branch length may or may not represent time depending on the type of tree.
  - **Root**: The deepest common ancestor of all organisms shown in the tree.
  - **Sister taxa**: Groups that diverge from the same node — they share a more recent common ancestor with each other than with any other group.
  - **Outgroup**: A taxon used as a reference point that is more distantly related than the taxa under study.
- Cladograms are built using **shared derived characters** (synapomorphies) — traits that first appeared in a common ancestor and are inherited by all members of a clade.
- DNA and protein sequence comparisons provide quantitative molecular data that can be used to build and test phylogenetic hypotheses.

### Topic 7.10: Speciation

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain the mechanisms through which new species form and distinguish between the types of reproductive barriers that maintain species boundaries.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Speciation** is the evolutionary process by which a single ancestral species gives rise to two or more distinct species.
- **Biological species concept**: A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed under natural conditions and produce viable, fertile offspring, and that are reproductively isolated from all other such groups.
- Two major pathways to speciation:
  - **Allopatric speciation**: A geographic barrier physically separates a population into isolated groups, which then diverge genetically through independent selective pressures and drift until they can no longer interbreed (e.g., Grand Canyon squirrels, Galápagos finches).
  - **Sympatric speciation**: New species arise within the same geographic range, most commonly via polyploidy in plants, habitat specialization, or assortative mating based on phenotypic differences.
- **Reproductive isolating mechanisms** prevent gene flow between species:
  - **Prezygotic barriers** (block mating or fertilization): temporal, habitat, behavioral, mechanical, and gametic isolation.
  - **Postzygotic barriers** (allow fertilization but prevent viable/fertile offspring): reduced hybrid viability, reduced hybrid fertility, and hybrid breakdown.

#### Extinction and Adaptive Radiation (Fall 2025: integrated into Unit 7, not a standalone topic)

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain the causes of extinction and describe how mass extinctions have historically been followed by bursts of evolutionary diversification.

**Core Concepts:**
- Extinction is the irreversible loss of a species when no surviving individuals remain capable of reproduction in any environment.
- Causes range from gradual (habitat loss, sustained competition, disease) to catastrophic (asteroid impacts, large-scale volcanic activity, rapid climate shifts).
- Earth's history includes several mass extinction events (e.g., end-Permian and end-Cretaceous) that eliminated large fractions of existing species.
- **Adaptive radiation**: Following a mass extinction or colonization of a new environment, surviving or founding lineages rapidly diversify to fill available ecological niches, giving rise to many new species in a relatively short geological time (e.g., Darwin's finches, Hawaiian honeycreepers, the mammalian explosion following dinosaur extinction).

### Topic 7.11: Variations in Populations

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain why maintaining genetic variation within a population is critical for long-term survival and adaptability.

**Core Concepts:**
- A population's capacity to respond to future environmental challenges depends on the amount of genetic diversity it harbors.
- Genetic variation arises through: random mutations, sexual reproduction processes (crossing over during meiosis, independent assortment of chromosomes, random fertilization), and the introduction of new alleles via gene flow.
- Populations with higher genetic diversity contain a broader range of phenotypes, increasing the probability that at least some individuals can survive and reproduce when conditions change.

### Topic 7.12: Origins of Life on Earth

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to summarize the scientific evidence and hypotheses regarding the origin of life and the early history of living organisms on Earth.

**Core Concepts:**
- The Earth formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago; the oldest confirmed microbial fossils date to roughly 3.5 billion years ago.
- The Miller-Urey experiment (1953) showed that amino acids and other organic molecules can form spontaneously when simple inorganic gases are exposed to energy sources resembling conditions on early Earth.
- **RNA world hypothesis**: Scientists hypothesize that RNA served as the first self-replicating, information-carrying molecule before the DNA-protein system evolved, because RNA can both store genetic information and catalyze chemical reactions (ribozymes).
- A rough chronology of major transitions in the history of life:
  - The earliest life forms were prokaryotes.
  - Photosynthetic cyanobacteria released oxygen as a byproduct, fundamentally transforming Earth's atmosphere (the Great Oxidation Event).
  - Eukaryotic cells arose through endosymbiosis — ancient prokaryotes were engulfed and became mitochondria and chloroplasts.
  - Multicellularity evolved independently in several distinct lineages.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Evolution** | A change in the allele frequencies of a population over successive generations |
| **Natural selection** | The process by which individuals with favorable heritable traits are more likely to survive, reproduce, and pass those traits to the next generation |
| **Fitness** | The relative ability of an individual to survive and reproduce in a particular environment; measured by reproductive success |
| **Adaptation** | A heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness in a particular environment; also the process by which populations become better suited to their environment |
| **Directional selection** | Natural selection that favors one extreme phenotype, shifting the population distribution in one direction |
| **Stabilizing selection** | Natural selection that favors intermediate phenotypes, reducing phenotypic variation |
| **Disruptive selection** | Natural selection that favors both extreme phenotypes over the intermediate, increasing phenotypic variation |
| **Sexual selection** | A form of natural selection in which individuals with certain traits are more likely to obtain mates |
| **Genetic drift** | Random changes in allele frequencies in a population, most significant in small populations; NOT driven by fitness |
| **Bottleneck effect** | A drastic, non-selective reduction in population size that reduces genetic diversity |
| **Founder effect** | The reduced genetic diversity that results when a small subgroup establishes a new, isolated population |
| **Gene flow** | The transfer of alleles between populations through the migration of individuals or their gametes |
| **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium** | A theoretical state in which allele and genotype frequencies remain constant in a population that is not evolving |
| **Allele frequency** | The proportion of a specific allele relative to all alleles for that gene in a population |
| **Homologous structures** | Structures in different species that share a common underlying anatomy due to descent from a common ancestor, regardless of current function |
| **Analogous structures** | Structures in different species that have similar function but different evolutionary origins; result from convergent evolution |
| **Vestigial structure** | A reduced or nonfunctional structure that is homologous to a functional structure in a related species or ancestor |
| **Convergent evolution** | The independent evolution of similar features in unrelated lineages, driven by similar selective pressures |
| **Divergent evolution** | The accumulation of differences between closely related populations, often leading to speciation |
| **Coevolution** | The reciprocal evolutionary change in interacting species, each exerting selective pressure on the other |
| **Phylogenetic tree** | A branching diagram showing the inferred evolutionary relationships among organisms based on shared characteristics |
| **Cladogram** | A phylogenetic diagram that groups organisms by shared derived characteristics (synapomorphies) |
| **Clade** | A group consisting of an ancestor and all of its descendants; a monophyletic group |
| **Synapomorphy** | A shared derived character that distinguishes a clade from other organisms |
| **Speciation** | The process by which one species splits into two or more separate species |
| **Allopatric speciation** | Speciation resulting from geographic isolation of subpopulations |
| **Sympatric speciation** | Speciation occurring within a single geographic area, without physical barriers |
| **Reproductive isolation** | The inability of members of two populations to interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring |
| **Prezygotic barrier** | A reproductive barrier that prevents mating or fertilization between species |
| **Postzygotic barrier** | A reproductive barrier that occurs after fertilization, resulting in inviable or infertile hybrid offspring |
| **Adaptive radiation** | The rapid diversification of a single ancestral lineage into many species that occupy different ecological niches |
| **Polyploidy** | A condition in which an organism has more than two complete sets of chromosomes; a common mechanism of sympatric speciation in plants |
| **Extinction** | The complete disappearance of a species; occurs when the last individual of that species dies |
| **RNA world hypothesis** | The hypothesis that RNA was the first self-replicating molecule, acting as both genetic material and enzyme before DNA and proteins evolved |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Evolution is NOT goal-directed or intentional**: Organisms do NOT evolve "in order to" adapt. Evolution has no direction or purpose. Natural selection acts on existing variation; it does not create variation on demand.

2. **Individuals do NOT evolve; POPULATIONS evolve**: An individual organism cannot change its alleles during its lifetime. Evolution is measured as changes in allele frequencies within a population over generations.

3. **"Survival of the fittest" does NOT mean "survival of the strongest"**: Fitness in biology means reproductive success — the ability to survive AND produce viable offspring. A "fit" organism is one that passes its genes to the next generation.

4. **Natural selection is NOT the only mechanism of evolution**: Genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, and sexual selection also change allele frequencies. Genetic drift can cause evolution in small populations even without selection.

5. **Homologous structures indicate COMMON ANCESTRY; analogous structures indicate CONVERGENT EVOLUTION**: Bat wings and human arms are homologous (same bone structure, different function). Bat wings and insect wings are analogous (similar function, different structure). Do NOT confuse these.

6. **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium is a THEORETICAL ideal, not a real state**: No natural population perfectly meets all five conditions. It serves as a null hypothesis against which to detect evolution.

7. **Mutations are RANDOM and not directed by environmental need**: Bacteria do not "develop" antibiotic resistance in response to antibiotics. Rather, random mutations that confer resistance already exist in the population, and antibiotics select for those resistant individuals.

8. **"Missing links" is a misleading term**: The fossil record contains many transitional fossils (e.g., Tiktaalik). Evolution produces a branching tree, not a linear chain. Every fossil is a "link."

9. **Speciation requires REPRODUCTIVE ISOLATION, not just geographic separation**: Geographic separation (allopatric speciation) is one mechanism, but reproductive isolation (inability to interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring) is the defining criterion for speciation.

10. **Genetic drift is NOT the same as natural selection**: Genetic drift is random and does not involve fitness differences. It is most significant in small populations. Natural selection is non-random and driven by fitness differences.
