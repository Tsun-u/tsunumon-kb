# Unit 4: Cell Communication and Cell Cycle

> AP Exam Weight: 10-15% | ~10-14 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: IST, SYI, ENE

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 4.1: Cell Communication

**Learning Objective IST-3.A**: Describe the ways that cells can communicate with one another.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-3.A.1: Cells communicate through direct contact or by signaling molecules released into the extracellular environment.
- IST-3.A.2: Types of cell signaling:
  - **Direct contact**: Gap junctions (animal cells) and plasmodesmata (plant cells) allow cytoplasmic connections between adjacent cells.
  - **Paracrine signaling**: Local signaling; signaling molecules act on nearby target cells (short distance).
  - **Endocrine signaling**: Long-distance signaling; hormones are released into the bloodstream and act on distant target cells.
  - **Autocrine signaling**: A cell signals itself.
  - **Synaptic signaling**: Neurotransmitters are released across a synapse to signal adjacent nerve or muscle cells.

### Topic 4.2: Introduction to Signal Transduction

**Learning Objective IST-3.B**: Describe the components of a signal transduction pathway.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-3.B.1: Signal transduction pathways consist of three stages:
  1. **Reception**: A signaling molecule (ligand) binds to a specific receptor protein on or in the target cell.
  2. **Transduction**: A series of molecular changes (often a phosphorylation cascade) relays and amplifies the signal.
  3. **Response**: The cell produces a specific cellular response (e.g., gene expression, enzyme activation, cell division).

### Topic 4.3: Signal Transduction

**Learning Objective IST-3.C**: Describe the role of components of a signal transduction pathway in producing a cellular response.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-3.C.1: Ligands bind to receptors with specificity (shape complementarity).
- IST-3.C.2: Types of receptors:
  - **Intracellular receptors**: For hydrophobic signaling molecules (e.g., steroid hormones) that can cross the membrane.
  - **Membrane receptors**: For hydrophilic signaling molecules that cannot cross the membrane. Types include G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs), and ligand-gated ion channels.
- IST-3.C.3: Signal amplification: each step in a cascade can activate multiple molecules at the next step, amplifying the signal.
- IST-3.C.4: Second messengers (e.g., cAMP, Ca2+, IP3) relay signals within the cell.
- IST-3.C.5: Phosphorylation cascades: kinases add phosphate groups to proteins, activating them; phosphatases remove phosphate groups, deactivating them.

### Topic 4.4: Changes in Signal Transduction Pathways

**Learning Objective IST-3.D**: Describe the effects of changes in signal transduction pathways on cellular response.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-3.D.1: Mutations in genes encoding components of signal transduction pathways can alter cellular response.
- IST-3.D.2: Chemicals that interfere with signaling molecules, receptors, or pathway components can disrupt normal cellular function (e.g., drugs, toxins).
- IST-3.D.3: Oncogenes (mutated proto-oncogenes) and malfunctioning tumor suppressor genes can lead to uncontrolled cell division (cancer).

### Topic 4.5: Feedback

**Learning Objective IST-3.E**: Describe the role of feedback mechanisms in maintaining homeostasis.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-3.E.1: **Negative feedback**: The output of a process inhibits the process, maintaining homeostasis (e.g., thermoregulation, blood glucose regulation via insulin/glucagon).
- IST-3.E.2: **Positive feedback**: The output of a process stimulates or amplifies the process (e.g., blood clotting cascade, oxytocin during childbirth, fruit ripening by ethylene).
- IST-3.E.3: Negative feedback is far more common in biological systems and serves to maintain stable internal conditions (homeostasis).

### Topic 4.6: Cell Cycle

**Learning Objective IST-1.B**: Describe the stages of the cell cycle.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.B.1: The cell cycle consists of:
  - **Interphase** (G1, S, G2): the cell grows, replicates DNA, and prepares for division.
    - G1: Cell growth, normal metabolic activity
    - S: DNA synthesis (replication)
    - G2: Further growth, preparation for mitosis, organelle duplication
  - **Mitotic (M) phase**: mitosis (nuclear division) + cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division)
- IST-1.B.2: Mitosis stages: prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase.
  - Prophase: chromatin condenses into chromosomes; spindle fibers form
  - Metaphase: chromosomes align at the metaphase plate
  - Anaphase: sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles
  - Telophase: nuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes decondense
  - Cytokinesis: cleavage furrow (animals) or cell plate (plants)
- IST-1.B.3: Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells (2n → 2n).
- IST-1.B.4: G0 phase: some cells exit the cell cycle and enter a non-dividing state (e.g., mature neurons, muscle cells).

### Topic 4.7: Regulation of Cell Cycle

**Learning Objective IST-1.C**: Explain how the cell cycle is regulated.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.C.1: Checkpoints regulate cell cycle progression:
  - **G1 checkpoint** (restriction point): checks for cell size, nutrients, growth factors, and DNA damage.
  - **G2 checkpoint**: checks for DNA replication completion and DNA damage.
  - **M checkpoint** (spindle assembly checkpoint): ensures all chromosomes are properly attached to spindle fibers at the metaphase plate.
- IST-1.C.2: Cyclins and cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) are key regulatory proteins. Cyclin levels fluctuate during the cell cycle; CDKs are activated when bound to cyclins.
- IST-1.C.3: Internal and external signals regulate cell division:
  - Growth factors (external signals) stimulate division.
  - Density-dependent inhibition: cells stop dividing when in contact with neighboring cells.
  - Anchorage dependence: cells must be attached to a substrate to divide.
- IST-1.C.4: Disruption of cell cycle regulation can lead to uncontrolled cell division (cancer).
  - Proto-oncogenes → mutate to oncogenes → promote excessive cell division.
  - Tumor suppressor genes (e.g., p53, Rb) → loss of function → loss of cell cycle braking.
- IST-1.C.5: Apoptosis (programmed cell death) is a normal, regulated process that eliminates damaged or unnecessary cells.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Signal transduction** | The process by which a signal on the surface of a cell is converted into a specific cellular response through a series of molecular changes |
| **Ligand** | A molecule that binds specifically to a receptor protein, triggering a cellular response |
| **Receptor** | A protein that binds to a specific signaling molecule (ligand) and initiates a cellular response |
| **G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR)** | A membrane receptor that works with a G protein to activate or inhibit an intracellular signaling pathway when a ligand binds |
| **Receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK)** | A membrane receptor that, upon ligand binding, dimerizes and autophosphorylates tyrosine residues, activating intracellular signaling |
| **Second messenger** | A small intracellular signaling molecule (e.g., cAMP, Ca2+, IP3) that relays and amplifies signals from receptors on the cell surface |
| **cAMP (cyclic AMP)** | A second messenger produced from ATP by adenylyl cyclase; activates protein kinase A (PKA) |
| **Kinase** | An enzyme that transfers a phosphate group from ATP to a substrate protein (phosphorylation), typically activating it |
| **Phosphatase** | An enzyme that removes a phosphate group from a protein (dephosphorylation), typically deactivating it |
| **Phosphorylation cascade** | A series of sequential kinase activations where each kinase phosphorylates and activates the next, amplifying the signal |
| **Negative feedback** | A regulatory mechanism where the output of a process inhibits the process, maintaining homeostasis |
| **Positive feedback** | A regulatory mechanism where the output of a process stimulates or amplifies the process |
| **Homeostasis** | The maintenance of a relatively stable internal environment despite changes in external conditions |
| **Cell cycle** | The ordered sequence of events from one cell division to the next, including interphase (G1, S, G2) and the mitotic (M) phase |
| **Interphase** | The phase of the cell cycle during which the cell grows and replicates its DNA; consists of G1, S, and G2 phases |
| **Mitosis** | The division of the cell nucleus into two genetically identical daughter nuclei; includes prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase |
| **Cytokinesis** | The division of the cytoplasm following mitosis, producing two separate daughter cells |
| **Chromosome** | A structure of condensed DNA and associated histone proteins; humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) |
| **Sister chromatids** | Two identical copies of a chromosome that are joined at the centromere, produced during DNA replication in S phase |
| **Centromere** | The constricted region of a chromosome where sister chromatids are joined and where the kinetochore forms |
| **Spindle fibers** | Microtubules that form the mitotic spindle and are responsible for moving chromosomes during cell division |
| **Checkpoint** | A control point in the cell cycle where stop/go signals regulate whether the cell proceeds to the next phase |
| **Cyclin** | A regulatory protein whose concentration fluctuates cyclically during the cell cycle; activates CDKs when bound to them |
| **Cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK)** | A protein kinase that is active only when bound to a cyclin; phosphorylates target proteins to drive cell cycle progression |
| **Proto-oncogene** | A normal gene that codes for proteins promoting cell growth and division; can mutate into an oncogene |
| **Oncogene** | A mutated proto-oncogene that promotes uncontrolled cell division; acts as a "stuck accelerator" |
| **Tumor suppressor gene** | A gene that codes for proteins that inhibit cell division or promote apoptosis; loss of function contributes to cancer (e.g., p53, Rb) |
| **Apoptosis** | Programmed cell death; a normal, regulated process involving controlled dismantling of cell components |
| **Cancer** | A disease characterized by uncontrolled cell division resulting from mutations in genes that regulate the cell cycle |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Signal transduction is NOT simply a ligand entering the cell**: Most signaling molecules (ligands) do NOT enter the cell. They bind to surface receptors, which then relay the signal internally. Only small, hydrophobic molecules (e.g., steroid hormones) cross the membrane to bind intracellular receptors.

2. **Mitosis is NOT the same as cell division**: Mitosis is specifically the division of the nucleus. Cell division also includes cytokinesis (division of the cytoplasm). The complete process is properly called the mitotic phase or M phase.

3. **Mitosis produces 2 cells; meiosis produces 4 cells**: Mitosis: 1 cell → 2 genetically identical diploid cells. Do NOT confuse with meiosis (Unit 5), which produces 4 genetically unique haploid cells.

4. **Sister chromatids are NOT the same as homologous chromosomes**: Sister chromatids are identical copies of ONE chromosome, joined at the centromere. Homologous chromosomes are pairs of chromosomes (one from each parent) that carry genes for the same traits but may have different alleles.

5. **Cancer is NOT caused by a single mutation**: Cancer typically results from the accumulation of multiple mutations in proto-oncogenes AND tumor suppressor genes over time.

6. **Negative feedback maintains homeostasis; positive feedback does NOT**: Positive feedback amplifies a response and drives a process to completion. It does NOT maintain stable conditions. Examples of positive feedback are relatively rare (blood clotting, childbirth contractions).

7. **G0 is NOT the same as G1**: G0 is a distinct non-dividing state that some cells enter. Cells in G0 are metabolically active but have exited the cell cycle, whereas cells in G1 are still within the cycle and may proceed to S phase.

8. **Apoptosis is NOT the same as necrosis**: Apoptosis is programmed, orderly cell death (normal process). Necrosis is uncontrolled cell death due to injury, infection, or toxins (pathological process).
