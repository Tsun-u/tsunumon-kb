# Unit 5: Heredity

> AP Exam Weight: 8-11% | ~8-10 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: IST, SYI, EVO

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 5.1: Meiosis

**Learning Objective IST-1.D**: Describe the events that occur in the stages of meiosis.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.D.1: Meiosis is a type of cell division that produces four genetically unique haploid (n) daughter cells from one diploid (2n) parent cell.
- IST-1.D.2: Meiosis consists of two rounds of division:
  - **Meiosis I** (reductional division): Homologous chromosomes separate. Diploid (2n) → two haploid (n) cells.
    - Prophase I: Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis), forming tetrads; crossing over occurs.
    - Metaphase I: Tetrads align at the metaphase plate; orientation is random (independent assortment).
    - Anaphase I: Homologous chromosomes separate (NOT sister chromatids).
    - Telophase I / Cytokinesis: Two haploid cells form.
  - **Meiosis II** (equational division): Sister chromatids separate. Similar to mitosis.
    - Prophase II → Metaphase II → Anaphase II (sister chromatids separate) → Telophase II / Cytokinesis
    - Result: Four haploid cells.

### Topic 5.2: Meiosis and Genetic Diversity

**Learning Objective IST-1.E**: Describe the mechanisms that generate genetic diversity.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.E.1: Three sources of genetic variation during sexual reproduction:
  1. **Crossing over** (recombination): During prophase I, homologous chromosomes exchange segments of DNA at chiasmata. Creates new combinations of alleles on a single chromosome.
  2. **Independent assortment**: During metaphase I, the random orientation of homologous pairs at the metaphase plate means each gamete receives a random mix of maternal and paternal chromosomes. For n chromosome pairs, there are 2^n possible combinations (in humans, 2^23 = ~8.4 million).
  3. **Random fertilization**: Any sperm can fuse with any egg, further increasing genetic diversity.
- IST-1.E.2: Genetic variation is the raw material for natural selection and evolution.

### Topic 5.3: Mendelian Genetics

**Learning Objective IST-1.F**: Explain Mendel's laws of inheritance.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.F.1: **Law of Segregation**: Each organism has two alleles for each gene, and these alleles separate during gamete formation so that each gamete carries only one allele.
- IST-1.F.2: **Law of Independent Assortment**: Genes located on different chromosomes (or far apart on the same chromosome) assort independently during gamete formation.
- IST-1.F.3: Key terminology:
  - **Dominant allele**: The allele that is expressed in the heterozygous condition (represented by uppercase letter).
  - **Recessive allele**: The allele that is only expressed in the homozygous condition (represented by lowercase letter).
  - **Genotype**: The genetic makeup of an organism (e.g., Aa, BB, bb).
  - **Phenotype**: The observable physical or biochemical trait of an organism.
  - **Homozygous**: Having two identical alleles for a trait (AA or aa).
  - **Heterozygous**: Having two different alleles for a trait (Aa).
- IST-1.F.4: Punnett squares can be used to predict the probability of genotypes and phenotypes in offspring.
- IST-1.F.5: A testcross (crossing with a homozygous recessive individual) can determine the genotype of an individual with a dominant phenotype.

### Topic 5.4: Non-Mendelian Genetics

**Learning Objective IST-1.G**: Explain deviations from Mendel's models of inheritance.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.G.1: **Incomplete dominance**: The heterozygous phenotype is intermediate between the two homozygous phenotypes (e.g., red x white = pink flowers in snapdragons).
- IST-1.G.2: **Codominance**: Both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygous condition (e.g., AB blood type, roan coat color).
- IST-1.G.3: **Multiple alleles**: More than two alleles exist for a gene in a population (e.g., ABO blood type: IA, IB, i).
- IST-1.G.4: **Polygenic inheritance**: A single trait is influenced by multiple genes (e.g., skin color, height). Produces a continuous range of phenotypes (bell curve distribution).
- IST-1.G.5: **Pleiotropy**: One gene affects multiple phenotypic traits (e.g., sickle cell allele affects hemoglobin shape, oxygen transport, and organ function).
- IST-1.G.6: **Epistasis**: The expression of one gene is modified by another gene at a different locus.
- IST-1.G.7: **Linked genes**: Genes located close together on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together; crossing over can separate linked genes (recombination frequency used to map genes).

### Topic 5.5: Environmental Effects on Phenotype

**Learning Objective IST-1.H**: Explain how the environment can affect the phenotype.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.H.1: The phenotype is the result of the interaction between the genotype and the environment.
- IST-1.H.2: Examples:
  - Temperature-sensitive coat color in Siamese cats and Himalayan rabbits (darker color at cooler extremities).
  - Hydrangea flower color affected by soil pH.
  - Height in humans affected by nutrition.
  - Phenylketonuria (PKU): genetic disorder where environmental modification (diet) can prevent symptoms.
- IST-1.H.3: Identical twins (same genotype) can show phenotypic differences due to environmental influences.

### Topic 5.6: Chromosomal Inheritance

**Learning Objective IST-1.I**: Explain chromosomal inheritance and its relationship to Mendel's laws.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-1.I.1: The chromosome theory of inheritance states that genes are located on chromosomes, and the behavior of chromosomes during meiosis accounts for inheritance patterns.
- IST-1.I.2: **Sex-linked traits**: Genes located on the X chromosome show distinctive inheritance patterns. Males (XY) are hemizygous for X-linked genes and express all X-linked alleles. X-linked recessive traits are more common in males (e.g., color blindness, hemophilia, Duchenne muscular dystrophy).
- IST-1.I.3: **Nondisjunction**: The failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis, resulting in gametes with abnormal chromosome numbers.
  - Monosomy: one fewer chromosome (2n-1)
  - Trisomy: one extra chromosome (2n+1)
  - Examples: Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner syndrome (45,X), Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY)
- IST-1.I.4: Chromosomal alterations:
  - Deletion: loss of a chromosome segment
  - Duplication: repetition of a chromosome segment
  - Inversion: reversal of a chromosome segment
  - Translocation: transfer of a segment to a nonhomologous chromosome

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Meiosis** | A type of cell division that reduces the chromosome number by half, producing four genetically unique haploid gametes from one diploid cell |
| **Diploid (2n)** | A cell containing two complete sets of chromosomes (one from each parent); somatic cells are diploid |
| **Haploid (n)** | A cell containing one complete set of chromosomes; gametes (sperm and egg) are haploid |
| **Gamete** | A reproductive cell (sperm or egg) that is haploid; fuses with another gamete during fertilization |
| **Homologous chromosomes** | A pair of chromosomes (one maternal, one paternal) that have the same length, centromere position, and gene loci, but may carry different alleles |
| **Tetrad (bivalent)** | A structure formed during prophase I of meiosis when two homologous chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids) pair up via synapsis; contains four chromatids total |
| **Synapsis** | The pairing of homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis |
| **Crossing over (recombination)** | The exchange of DNA segments between homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis, at points called chiasmata |
| **Chiasma (pl. chiasmata)** | The physical site where crossing over occurs; the X-shaped structure visible during prophase I |
| **Independent assortment** | The random orientation and separation of homologous chromosomes during meiosis I, resulting in gametes with random combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes |
| **Allele** | An alternative form of a gene; different alleles may produce different phenotypes |
| **Dominant** | An allele whose phenotype is expressed in both the homozygous (AA) and heterozygous (Aa) conditions |
| **Recessive** | An allele whose phenotype is expressed only in the homozygous condition (aa) |
| **Genotype** | The genetic composition of an organism, typically represented by letter symbols (e.g., Aa, BB) |
| **Phenotype** | The observable characteristics of an organism, determined by genotype and environmental influences |
| **Homozygous** | Having two identical alleles for a particular gene (AA or aa) |
| **Heterozygous** | Having two different alleles for a particular gene (Aa) |
| **Punnett square** | A diagram used to predict the genotypic and phenotypic ratios of offspring from a genetic cross |
| **Testcross** | A cross between an individual with a dominant phenotype (unknown genotype) and a homozygous recessive individual to determine the unknown genotype |
| **Incomplete dominance** | A pattern of inheritance where the heterozygous phenotype is intermediate between the two homozygous phenotypes |
| **Codominance** | A pattern of inheritance where both alleles are fully and simultaneously expressed in the heterozygous condition |
| **Polygenic inheritance** | A pattern where multiple genes contribute to a single phenotypic trait, resulting in continuous variation |
| **Pleiotropy** | A single gene influencing multiple phenotypic traits |
| **Epistasis** | The interaction between genes at different loci where one gene modifies the expression of another |
| **Sex-linked (X-linked)** | A gene located on the X chromosome; males are hemizygous and express all X-linked alleles |
| **Nondisjunction** | The failure of homologous chromosomes (meiosis I) or sister chromatids (meiosis II) to separate properly during cell division |
| **Aneuploidy** | A condition in which a cell has an abnormal number of chromosomes due to nondisjunction (e.g., trisomy, monosomy) |
| **Autosomal** | Referring to genes located on autosomes (non-sex chromosomes); chromosomes 1-22 in humans |
| **Recombination frequency** | The percentage of recombinant offspring in a testcross; used to estimate the distance between linked genes on a chromosome |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Meiosis is NOT the same as mitosis**: Meiosis involves TWO rounds of division and produces FOUR haploid cells. Mitosis involves ONE division and produces TWO diploid cells. Meiosis includes crossing over and independent assortment; mitosis does not.

2. **Dominant does NOT mean "better" or "more common"**: Dominant simply means the allele is expressed in the heterozygous condition. A dominant allele can be rare in a population (e.g., achondroplasia), and a recessive allele can be common.

3. **Incomplete dominance is NOT the same as codominance**: In incomplete dominance, the heterozygote shows a BLENDED intermediate phenotype (e.g., pink). In codominance, BOTH phenotypes are fully expressed simultaneously (e.g., AB blood type shows both A and B antigens).

4. **Genes are NOT always on different chromosomes**: Mendel's law of independent assortment applies only to genes on different chromosomes or far apart on the same chromosome. Linked genes (close together on the same chromosome) tend to be inherited together.

5. **Sex-linked does NOT mean the trait only appears in one sex**: Both males and females can express X-linked traits. However, X-linked recessive traits are MORE COMMON in males because males have only one X chromosome (hemizygous).

6. **Crossing over happens in meiosis I, NOT in meiosis II or mitosis**: Crossing over specifically occurs during prophase I when homologous chromosomes are paired in tetrads.

7. **Nondisjunction can occur in meiosis I OR meiosis II**: Nondisjunction in meiosis I means homologous chromosomes fail to separate. Nondisjunction in meiosis II means sister chromatids fail to separate. Both lead to aneuploidy but affect gametes differently.

8. **Phenotype is not determined by genotype alone**: Environmental factors can significantly influence phenotype. The relationship is: Phenotype = Genotype + Environment.
