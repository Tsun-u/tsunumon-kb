## Subtopics

### C2.1 Chemical Signaling (SL + HL)

- Cell signaling: communication between cells using chemical messengers
- **Hormones**: chemical messengers transported in blood to target cells
  - Endocrine glands: produce and secrete hormones (e.g., pancreas, thyroid, adrenal)
  - Target cells: have specific receptors for particular hormones
- **Peptide hormones** (e.g., insulin):
  - Hydrophilic; bind to receptors on cell surface
  - Trigger intracellular signaling cascade (second messengers, e.g., cAMP)
- **Steroid hormones** (e.g., estrogen, testosterone):
  - Hydrophobic; pass through cell membrane
  - Bind to intracellular receptors; act as transcription factors
- **Blood glucose regulation**:
  - Insulin (beta cells of pancreas): lowers blood glucose by stimulating glucose uptake and glycogen synthesis
  - Glucagon (alpha cells of pancreas): raises blood glucose by stimulating glycogen breakdown
  - Negative feedback loop maintains homeostasis
- **Diabetes**: Type 1 (autoimmune, no insulin production) vs. Type 2 (insulin resistance)

### C2.2 Neural Signaling (SL + HL)

- **Neurons**: cells specialized for transmitting electrical impulses
  - Structure: cell body, dendrites, axon, myelin sheath, synaptic terminals
- **Resting potential**: ~−70 mV; maintained by Na+/K+ ATPase (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in)
- **Action potential**: rapid depolarization and repolarization
  - Threshold reached → voltage-gated Na+ channels open → depolarization
  - Na+ channels close, K+ channels open → repolarization
  - Refractory period prevents backward propagation
- **Saltatory conduction**: action potential jumps between nodes of Ranvier (myelinated axons); faster
- **Synaptic transmission**:
  - Action potential arrives at presynaptic terminal
  - Ca2+ influx → vesicles release neurotransmitter into synaptic cleft
  - Neurotransmitter binds to receptors on postsynaptic membrane
  - Excitatory (depolarization) or inhibitory (hyperpolarization) response
- Neurotransmitters: acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, GABA, norepinephrine
- Effects of drugs on synaptic transmission (e.g., blocking reuptake, mimicking neurotransmitters)

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Hormone** | Chemical messenger produced by endocrine glands, transported in blood to target cells |
| **Receptor** | Protein that binds a specific signaling molecule and triggers a cellular response |
| **Resting potential** | Voltage difference across a neuron's membrane at rest (~−70 mV) |
| **Action potential** | Rapid reversal and restoration of membrane potential along an axon |
| **Synapse** | Junction between two neurons or between a neuron and an effector cell |
| **Neurotransmitter** | Chemical released at a synapse to transmit a signal to the next cell |
| **Myelin sheath** | Insulating layer around axons that speeds up nerve impulse transmission |
| **Saltatory conduction** | Jumping of action potential between nodes of Ranvier in myelinated axons |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Nerves and neurons are the same thing"** -- A nerve is a bundle of many neuron axons; a neuron is a single cell.
2. **"Nerve impulses travel at the speed of electricity"** -- Action potentials are much slower than electrical currents (~1--120 m/s depending on myelination).
3. **"Insulin directly lowers blood sugar"** -- Insulin signals target cells to take up glucose; it does not destroy glucose.
4. **"Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes are the same disease"** -- Type 1 is autoimmune (no insulin); Type 2 is insulin resistance.
5. **"Neurotransmitters only excite the next neuron"** -- Some neurotransmitters are inhibitory (e.g., GABA).
