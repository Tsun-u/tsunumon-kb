# D3: Continuity and Change -- Organisms

> Theme D: Continuity and Change | Level 3: Organisms

## Subtopics

### D3.1 Reproduction (SL + HL)

- **Asexual reproduction**: one parent, genetically identical offspring (clones)
  - Binary fission (bacteria), budding (yeast), vegetative propagation (plants)
  - Advantage: rapid reproduction, no mate needed
  - Disadvantage: no genetic variation
- **Sexual reproduction**: two parents, genetically diverse offspring
  - Gamete formation by meiosis → fertilization → zygote
  - Advantage: genetic variation (adaptation to changing environments)
  - Disadvantage: requires mate, slower reproduction
- Human reproductive system:
  - Male: testes, sperm production (spermatogenesis), seminal vesicles, prostate
  - Female: ovaries, egg production (oogenesis), uterus, fallopian tubes
- Fertilization: sperm penetrates egg → fusion of haploid nuclei → diploid zygote
- Embryonic development: cleavage → blastocyst → implantation → gastrulation

- Hormonal control of the menstrual cycle:
  - FSH: stimulates follicle development
  - LH: triggers ovulation
  - Estrogen: thickens endometrium, triggers LH surge
  - Progesterone: maintains endometrium
- IVF (in vitro fertilization) and ethical considerations
- Spermatogenesis vs. oogenesis comparison

### D3.2 Inheritance (SL + HL)

- **Mendel's laws**:
  - Law of segregation: two alleles separate during gamete formation
  - Law of independent assortment: genes on different chromosomes assort independently
- Key terms: gene, allele, genotype, phenotype, dominant, recessive, homozygous, heterozygous
- **Monohybrid crosses**: Punnett squares, test crosses
- **Dihybrid crosses**: expected 9:3:3:1 ratio (independent assortment)
- **Codominance**: both alleles expressed equally (e.g., ABO blood groups: $I^A$ and $I^B$)
- **Sex-linked inheritance**: genes on X chromosome (e.g., color blindness, hemophilia)
  - Males (XY) more likely to express X-linked recessive traits
- **Pedigree analysis**: interpreting family trees for inheritance patterns
- **Polygenic inheritance**: multiple genes affect one trait (e.g., skin color, height)

#### HL Extension -- D3.2
- Chi-squared test for testing genetic ratios
- Gene linkage and recombination frequency
- Epistasis: one gene masks the expression of another
- Autosomal vs. sex-linked inheritance analysis

### D3.3 Homeostasis (SL + HL)

- **Homeostasis**: maintenance of a constant internal environment
- **Negative feedback**: response counteracts the change to restore set point
  - Body temperature regulation: hypothalamus as thermostat
    - Too hot: vasodilation, sweating, reduced metabolic rate
    - Too cold: vasoconstriction, shivering, increased metabolic rate
  - Blood glucose regulation (also covered in C2.1)
- **Positive feedback**: response amplifies the change (rare)
  - Examples: oxytocin in childbirth, blood clotting cascade
- **Endotherms** vs. **ectotherms**: internal vs. environmental heat sources
- Osmoregulation: maintaining water/ion balance (kidneys, ADH)

- Detailed thermoregulation pathways
- Hormonal interactions in homeostasis
- Feedback loops in the endocrine system (hypothalamus-pituitary-target gland axis)

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Allele** | Alternative form of a gene at a specific locus |
| **Genotype** | Genetic makeup of an organism (e.g., Bb) |
| **Phenotype** | Observable characteristics resulting from genotype and environment |
| **Dominant allele** | Allele expressed in both homozygous and heterozygous conditions |
| **Recessive allele** | Allele expressed only in the homozygous condition |
| **Codominance** | Both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygous condition |
| **Sex-linked** | Gene located on a sex chromosome (usually X) |
| **Negative feedback** | Mechanism where the response reduces the original stimulus to maintain homeostasis |
| **Homeostasis** | Maintenance of stable internal conditions despite external changes |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"Dominant means more common"** -- Dominance refers to how alleles interact in heterozygotes, not frequency in a population.
2. **"Traits skip generations randomly"** -- Recessive traits appear when two carrier parents produce a homozygous recessive offspring; the pattern is predictable.
3. **"Sex-linked always means X-linked"** -- While most sex-linked genes are on the X chromosome, some are on the Y chromosome.
4. **"Homeostasis means conditions never change"** -- Homeostasis involves constant adjustments; conditions fluctuate around a set point.
5. **"Positive feedback is always harmful"** -- Positive feedback has important physiological roles (childbirth, blood clotting).
