# B3: Form and Function of Organisms

> Theme B: Form and Function | Level 3: Organisms

## Subtopics

### B3.1 Gas Exchange (SL + HL)

- Gas exchange surfaces: adapted for efficient diffusion
  - Large surface area, thin walls, moist surfaces, good blood supply, ventilation
- **Lungs** (mammals):
  - Trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli
  - Alveoli: thin walls (one cell thick), surrounded by capillaries, surfactant reduces surface tension
  - Ventilation: diaphragm and intercostal muscles create pressure changes
- **Gills** (fish): countercurrent flow maximizes gas exchange efficiency
- **Insects**: tracheal system with spiracles; direct gas delivery to cells
- **Plants**: stomata for gas exchange; guard cells regulate opening/closing

#### HL Extension -- B3.1
- Ventilation rate and its regulation
- Gas exchange in high-altitude organisms
- Detailed mechanics of breathing (pressure-volume relationships)

### B3.2 Transport (SL + HL)

- Need for transport systems: organisms too large for diffusion alone
- **Mammalian circulatory system**:
  - Double circulation: pulmonary circuit (lungs) and systemic circuit (body)
  - Heart structure: four chambers, valves, septum
  - Cardiac cycle: atrial systole → ventricular systole → diastole
  - Arteries (thick walls, elastic), veins (valves, thinner walls), capillaries (one cell thick)
- **Blood**: plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets
  - Hemoglobin: oxygen transport; oxygen dissociation curve
- **Plant transport**:
  - **Xylem**: water and mineral transport (transpiration pull, cohesion-tension theory)
  - **Phloem**: organic nutrient transport (translocation, pressure-flow hypothesis)
  - Transpiration: factors affecting rate (temperature, humidity, wind, light)

#### HL Extension -- B3.2
- Bohr effect on oxygen dissociation curve
- Myoglobin vs. hemoglobin affinity
- Control of heart rate (medulla, SAN, AVN)
- Detailed pressure-flow model of phloem translocation

### B3.3 Muscle and Motility (HL only)

- Skeletal muscle structure: muscle fibers, myofibrils, sarcomeres
- **Sliding filament theory**:
  - Actin (thin filaments) and myosin (thick filaments)
  - Cross-bridge cycling: myosin heads bind actin, power stroke, release
  - Role of ATP, calcium ions, troponin, tropomyosin
- Sarcomere contraction: H-zone and I-band shorten; A-band stays constant
- Antagonistic muscle pairs (e.g., biceps and triceps)
- Types of muscle: skeletal, cardiac, smooth

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Ventilation** | Mechanical process of moving air in and out of the lungs |
| **Countercurrent flow** | Blood and water flow in opposite directions in fish gills to maximize gas exchange |
| **Double circulation** | Blood passes through the heart twice per complete circuit (pulmonary + systemic) |
| **Transpiration** | Loss of water vapor from plant leaves through stomata |
| **Cohesion-tension theory** | Water molecules pulled up xylem by transpiration; cohesion maintains continuous column |
| **Translocation** | Movement of organic solutes through phloem |
| **Sarcomere** | Functional unit of muscle contraction; from Z-line to Z-line |
| **Sliding filament theory** | Muscle contraction occurs when actin slides over myosin, shortening the sarcomere |

## Common Misconceptions

1. **"We breathe in oxygen and breathe out carbon dioxide"** -- Exhaled air still contains ~16% oxygen; we only extract a fraction.
2. **"Arteries always carry oxygenated blood"** -- Pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood to the lungs.
3. **"Veins carry blood because of heart pressure"** -- Venous return depends on skeletal muscle contractions and valves; heart pressure is minimal in veins.
4. **"Transpiration is bad for plants"** -- Transpiration is a necessary consequence of gas exchange and drives water uptake.
5. **"Muscles push and pull"** -- Muscles can only contract (pull); antagonistic pairs provide opposing movements.
