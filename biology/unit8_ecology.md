## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 8.1: Responses to the Environment

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to connect specific behavioral and physiological responses of organisms to the internal or external environmental changes that trigger them.

**Core Concepts:**
- Organisms detect and respond to environmental changes through a variety of behavioral and physiological mechanisms.
  - **Taxis**: Oriented movement directly toward (positive) or away from (negative) a stimulus (e.g., phototaxis toward light, chemotaxis toward food chemicals).
  - **Kinesis**: Non-directed change in movement speed or turning rate that depends on stimulus intensity rather than direction (e.g., woodlice move faster and turn more frequently in dry conditions).
  - **Photoperiodism**: Timing of biological events based on day-length signals, governing seasonal processes such as flowering in plants, reproductive cycles in animals, and migration cues.
  - **Phototropism**: Growth direction in plants biased toward or away from light, mediated by uneven redistribution of the hormone auxin.
  - **Nocturnal/diurnal activity patterns**: Scheduling of active periods to coincide with favorable light conditions.
  - **Fight-or-flight response**: A rapid physiological mobilization triggered by perceived threat, driven by epinephrine release and sympathetic nervous system activation.
- Organisms also exchange information with one another in ways that alter behavior in response to environmental cues:
  - Warning signals in social species (e.g., alarm calls in prairie dogs; aposematic coloration in toxic prey)
  - Plant anti-herbivory communication (e.g., volatile chemical release that attracts natural enemies of herbivores; induction of defensive secondary metabolites)
  - Chemical signaling via pheromones (e.g., ant trail marking, moth mate attraction)

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain how behavioral responses improve individual fitness and contribute to population-level success.

**Core Concepts:**
- Behaviors that reliably increase survival or reproduction tend to be maintained by natural selection, ultimately benefiting population persistence.
  - Optimal foraging strategies that balance energy gain against search and handling costs
  - Cooperative behaviors that enhance group survival (e.g., sentinel alarm calling, coordinated hunting, reciprocal altruism)
  - Multimodal communication — through visual, acoustic, chemical, and tactile channels — that coordinates group behavior

### Topic 8.2: Energy Flow Through Ecosystems

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to compare the energy-acquisition strategies of autotrophs and heterotrophs and trace how energy enters ecosystems.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Autotrophs** build organic molecules from inorganic materials using external energy inputs.
  - **Photosynthetic organisms** (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) harvest energy from sunlight to drive carbon fixation.
  - **Chemosynthetic organisms** oxidize inorganic compounds such as hydrogen sulfide or ammonia to obtain energy, independent of sunlight (e.g., bacteria at deep-sea hydrothermal vents).
- **Heterotrophs** obtain energy by consuming organic molecules produced by other organisms.
  - Primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers directly.
  - Secondary consumers prey on primary consumers.
  - Tertiary consumers prey on secondary consumers.
  - **Decomposers and detritivores** break down dead organisms and waste products, returning nutrients to the abiotic environment.

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to predict how changes in energy availability ripple through trophic levels and alter ecosystem structure.

**Core Concepts:**
- Shifts in energy availability — such as a reduction in sunlight from cloud cover, drought, or turbidity — reduce producer biomass and propagate through all consumer trophic levels.
- A disruption at the producer level can compress or expand every level above it, reshaping population sizes and community composition throughout the ecosystem.

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to trace energy flow through an ecosystem and explain why energy is not recycled while matter is.

**Core Concepts:**
- Energy enters ecosystems from the sun, moves through producers, then consumers, and ultimately exits as heat — it flows in one direction and is never recycled.
- **Trophic levels** organize organisms by feeding position within the energy-transfer sequence.
- **10% rule**: Roughly 10% of the energy stored at one trophic level is incorporated into biomass at the next level; the remainder is dissipated as heat during cellular respiration.
- **Gross primary productivity (GPP)**: The total rate at which producers capture and store energy through photosynthesis per unit time.
- **Net primary productivity (NPP)**: The energy remaining after producers use some for their own respiration; NPP = GPP − R. NPP is the energy pool available to the first consumer level.
- **Food chains** depict linear energy transfer; **food webs** capture the full complexity of overlapping feeding relationships within a real ecosystem.
- **Ecological pyramids** visualize the distribution of energy, biomass, or numbers across trophic levels:
  - Energy pyramids are always upright — each level contains less energy than the one below.
  - Biomass pyramids are usually upright, with exceptions in aquatic systems where rapidly dividing phytoplankton support larger zooplankton biomass.
  - Number pyramids can be inverted (e.g., a single large tree supports thousands of insects).
- **Biogeochemical cycles**: Unlike energy, matter is recycled continuously through ecosystems.
  - **Carbon cycle**: CO₂ is fixed by photosynthesis into organic compounds and released back to the atmosphere by respiration, combustion, and decomposition.
  - **Nitrogen cycle**: Atmospheric N₂ is converted to NH₃ by nitrogen-fixing bacteria; nitrifying bacteria then oxidize it to NO₂⁻/NO₃⁻; plants assimilate nitrate; decomposition releases NH₃ (ammonification); denitrifying bacteria return N₂ to the atmosphere.
  - **Water cycle**: Water moves through evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, and runoff.
  - **Phosphorus cycle**: Has no atmospheric reservoir; phosphorus cycles through rocks, soil, water, and organisms on a geological timescale.

### Topic 8.3: Population Ecology

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to identify the biological and environmental factors that determine how fast a population grows and distinguish between exponential and logistic growth models.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Population**: All individuals of a single species occupying the same area at the same time.
- Key descriptors of a population include: total size (N), density (individuals per unit area), spatial dispersion pattern (clumped, uniform, or random), and age structure.
- **Exponential growth**: When resources are unlimited, populations grow at a constant per capita rate, producing a J-shaped curve. Equation: **dN/dt = r_max × N** (r_max = maximum per capita rate of increase; N = current population size).
- Exponential growth is observed in nature when a species first colonizes a resource-rich environment with no competitors or predators (e.g., bacteria inoculated into fresh growth medium, invasive species in a new region).
- **r-selected species**: Characterized by high reproductive output, rapid development, short lifespan, minimal parental investment, and thriving in unpredictable or disturbed environments (e.g., bacteria, annual weeds, many insects).
- **K-selected species**: Characterized by low reproductive rate, slow development, long lifespan, substantial parental investment, and population sizes maintained near carrying capacity in stable environments (e.g., elephants, whales, humans).

### Topic 8.4: Effect of Density on Populations

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain how resource constraints and density-dependent feedback limit population growth and apply the logistic growth model to predict population trajectories.

**Core Concepts:**
- Without any constraints, a population can temporarily exceed the resource base of its environment, but growth is eventually checked.
- As populations grow, both density-dependent and density-independent limiting factors reduce the per capita growth rate until a **logistic growth** pattern emerges — an S-shaped (sigmoid) trajectory.
- **Logistic growth equation**: **dN/dt = r_max × N × (K−N)/K** — as N approaches K, the term (K−N)/K shrinks toward zero, slowing growth.
- **Carrying capacity (K)**: The population size at which births and deaths balance given current resource levels. K is not a fixed constant — it shifts when resources, climate, or other environmental conditions change.
- **Density-dependent limiting factors**: Become more severe as population density increases.
  - Intraspecific and interspecific competition for food, water, space
  - Increased predation pressure on denser prey populations
  - Greater spread of infectious disease and parasites in crowded populations
  - Accumulation of metabolic waste products
- **Density-independent limiting factors**: Affect population size regardless of how dense the population is.
  - Sudden natural disasters (floods, wildfires, hurricanes)
  - Temperature extremes and other extreme weather events
  - Human-driven habitat alteration and chemical pollution

### Topic 8.5: Community Ecology

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to describe how species composition, diversity, and interspecific interactions shape community structure, and explain the patterns of ecological succession.

**Core Concepts:**
- A **community** consists of all populations of different species that live in the same area and interact with one another.
- Community structure is characterized by **species composition** (the identity of species present) and **species diversity** (combining richness — number of species — with evenness — how equitably individuals are distributed among those species).
- **Interspecific interactions** are classified by whether each participant benefits, is harmed, or is unaffected:
  - **Competition** (−/−): Both species are harmed; leads to competitive exclusion or niche partitioning.
  - **Predation** (+/−): Predator gains; prey loses; drives reciprocal coevolution of offense and defense.
  - **Herbivory** (+/−): The herbivore benefits; the plant or alga is harmed.
  - **Mutualism** (+/+): Both species gain fitness benefits (e.g., mycorrhizal fungi and plant roots, Rhizobium and legumes, cleaner fish and client fish).
  - **Commensalism** (+/0): One species benefits without affecting the other (e.g., barnacles on whale skin, epiphytic plants on tree branches).
  - **Parasitism** (+/−): The parasite extracts resources at the host's expense (e.g., tapeworms, ticks, mistletoe on host trees).
- **Competitive exclusion principle (Gause's law)**: When two species compete for an identical, limiting resource, one will consistently outcompete the other and drive it to local extinction — they cannot stably coexist.
- **Niche**: The full set of biotic and abiotic conditions within which a species can survive and reproduce.
  - **Fundamental niche**: The theoretical maximum range of conditions a species could use if competitors were absent.
  - **Realized niche**: The narrower range of conditions a species actually uses in the presence of competitors and other interactions.
- **Resource partitioning (niche partitioning)**: Similar species reduce competition by using different subsets of resources or the same resource at different times or places (e.g., warblers foraging at distinct heights in spruce trees).
- **Character displacement**: Morphological traits diverge more between two competing species where their ranges overlap than where only one species is present, reducing niche overlap and interspecific competition (e.g., beak size differences in Galápagos finches on shared islands).
- **Keystone species**: A species whose removal causes disproportionately large changes to community structure relative to its own abundance (e.g., sea otters suppressing sea urchin populations to protect kelp forests; wolves restructuring prey behavior in Yellowstone).
- **Trophic cascades**: Indirect, multi-level effects in a food web triggered when a top predator is added or removed — changes ripple downward through prey and vegetation (e.g., wolf removal → elk overgrazing → loss of streamside plants → altered river morphology).
- **Ecological succession**: The directional, predictable change in species composition in a community over time.
  - **Primary succession**: Begins on bare substrate with no soil (e.g., cooled lava flows, newly exposed rock after glacial retreat). Pioneer species such as lichens and mosses initiate soil formation.
  - **Secondary succession**: Recolonization of a disturbed area where soil and some organic matter remain (e.g., regrowth after a forest fire, agricultural abandonment, or logging). Proceeds faster than primary succession because soil is already present.

### Topic 8.6: Biodiversity

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to explain how various biotic and abiotic factors influence biodiversity levels within ecosystems and describe why biodiversity matters for ecosystem function and human well-being.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Biodiversity** operates at three nested levels:
  - **Genetic diversity**: The range of allelic variation within and across populations of a species.
  - **Species diversity**: The number of distinct species present (richness) and how evenly individuals are distributed among them (evenness).
  - **Ecosystem diversity**: The range of habitat types, ecological communities, and ecological processes across a landscape.
- **Species richness**: A count of the total number of species in a defined area.
- **Species evenness**: A measure of how similarly abundant different species are; communities where one species dominates have low evenness.
- Higher biodiversity generally enhances ecosystem stability and resilience — a greater variety of species provides functional redundancy, so the ecosystem continues to operate even when individual species are lost.
- Biodiversity underpins critical **ecosystem services**: crop pollination, freshwater purification, nutrient cycling, food security, pharmaceutical resources, carbon storage, and climate stabilization.
- Species-rich ecosystems resist colonization by invasive species more effectively and recover from disturbances more rapidly than low-diversity systems.

### Topic 8.7: Disruptions in Ecosystems

**Key Learning Goals**: Students will be able to describe the types of disruptions — natural and human-caused — that alter ecosystem structure and function, and explain the mechanisms by which human activities accelerate biodiversity loss.

**Core Concepts:**
- Ecosystems are not static; they respond to both natural variation and human-driven change.
- **Invasive species**: Non-native organisms deliberately or accidentally introduced to a new region where they often face few natural predators or pathogens, enabling rapid population growth that outcompetes native species.
  - Examples of ecologically damaging invasives: kudzu vine, cane toads, zebra mussels, Asian carp, European starlings.
- **Human-driven ecosystem disruptions**:
  - **Habitat destruction and fragmentation**: The primary driver of species extinction worldwide. Converts and isolates habitats, reducing population sizes, restricting gene flow, and creating vulnerable edge environments.
  - **Overexploitation**: Harvesting of wild populations (hunting, fishing, collection) at rates exceeding their reproductive capacity.
  - **Pollution**: Contamination of air, water, and soil with industrial chemicals, agricultural runoff, and waste products.
  - **Climate change**: Accelerating atmospheric warming driven by rising concentrations of greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide) from fossil fuel combustion and land-use change. Consequences include sea-level rise, altered precipitation patterns, range shifts for species, coral bleaching, and ocean acidification.
- **Biological magnification (biomagnification)**: Persistent, fat-soluble toxins (e.g., DDT, methylmercury, PCBs) become progressively more concentrated in tissues at each successive trophic level as organisms consume large quantities of contaminated prey.
- **Eutrophication**: Excess nutrient inputs (especially nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural fertilizer runoff) stimulate explosive algal bloom growth; when the algae die and decompose, microbial respiration depletes dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic or anoxic "dead zones" lethal to most aquatic life.
- **Geological and meteorological disturbances** have always reshaped ecosystems — volcanic eruptions, large-scale storms, and long-term climate oscillations have repeatedly destroyed habitats, created new ones, and reshuffled species distributions throughout Earth's history.
- Current rates of human-driven extinction are estimated at 100–1,000 times the pre-human background extinction rate, marking what many scientists consider a sixth mass extinction event.
- Ecosystem resilience — the capacity to absorb disturbance and recover — scales with biodiversity; high-diversity systems return to baseline faster after disruption.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Ecology** | The scientific study of interactions between organisms and their environment, including both biotic and abiotic factors |
| **Biotic factors** | Living components of an ecosystem (organisms, their interactions, and organic matter) |
| **Abiotic factors** | Nonliving components of an ecosystem (temperature, water, sunlight, soil, wind, nutrients) |
| **Population** | A group of individuals of the same species living in the same geographic area at the same time |
| **Community** | All the populations of different species living and interacting in the same area |
| **Ecosystem** | A community of organisms together with their abiotic environment |
| **Biome** | A large-scale region characterized by distinct climate, vegetation, and animal life (e.g., tropical rainforest, tundra, desert) |
| **Biosphere** | The global ecosystem; the sum of all ecosystems on Earth where life exists |
| **Taxis** | Directed movement toward (positive) or away from (negative) a stimulus |
| **Kinesis** | Non-directional change in activity rate or turning frequency in response to stimulus intensity |
| **Photoperiodism** | An organism's physiological response to the relative lengths of day and night (photoperiod) |
| **Carrying capacity (K)** | The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely given available resources |
| **Exponential growth** | Population growth in an unlimited environment; J-shaped curve; dN/dt = r_max * N |
| **Logistic growth** | Population growth that slows as the population approaches carrying capacity; S-shaped curve; dN/dt = r_max * N * (K-N)/K |
| **Density-dependent factor** | A factor whose limiting effect increases as population density increases (e.g., competition, disease, predation) |
| **Density-independent factor** | A factor that affects population size regardless of population density (e.g., weather, natural disasters) |
| **r-selected species** | Species characterized by high reproductive rate, rapid development, and small body size; thrive in unstable environments |
| **K-selected species** | Species characterized by low reproductive rate, slow development, and large body size; thrive in stable environments near carrying capacity |
| **Trophic level** | A feeding position in a food chain or food web (producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, etc.) |
| **Producer (autotroph)** | An organism that produces its own food from inorganic molecules using light energy (photosynthesis) or chemical energy (chemosynthesis) |
| **Consumer (heterotroph)** | An organism that obtains energy by feeding on other organisms |
| **Decomposer** | An organism that breaks down dead organic matter and returns nutrients to the environment (e.g., bacteria, fungi) |
| **Gross primary productivity (GPP)** | The total amount of energy fixed by photosynthesis in an ecosystem per unit time |
| **Net primary productivity (NPP)** | GPP minus the energy consumed by producers in cellular respiration; the energy available to consumers; NPP = GPP - R |
| **Biomagnification** | The progressive increase in concentration of a persistent toxin at successively higher trophic levels |
| **Keystone species** | A species whose influence on community structure is disproportionately large relative to its abundance |
| **Trophic cascade** | Indirect effects on lower trophic levels caused by the addition or removal of a top predator |
| **Competitive exclusion** | The principle that two species competing for identical resources cannot coexist indefinitely; one will be eliminated |
| **Resource partitioning** | The differentiation of niches that enables similar species to coexist by reducing competition |
| **Character displacement** | The divergence of traits in two similar species where they coexist, reducing interspecific competition |
| **Niche** | The sum of all biotic and abiotic conditions required for an organism to survive, grow, and reproduce |
| **Fundamental niche** | The full range of conditions and resources a species could potentially use in the absence of competition |
| **Realized niche** | The actual range of conditions and resources a species uses, limited by competition and other interactions |
| **Mutualism** | A symbiotic interaction in which both species benefit (+/+) |
| **Commensalism** | A symbiotic interaction in which one species benefits and the other is unaffected (+/0) |
| **Parasitism** | A symbiotic interaction in which one species (parasite) benefits at the expense of the other (host) (+/-) |
| **Primary succession** | Ecological succession on a newly formed surface where no soil exists; begins with pioneer species |
| **Secondary succession** | Ecological succession on a disturbed surface where soil remains; faster than primary succession |
| **Pioneer species** | The first species to colonize a new or disturbed area during ecological succession (e.g., lichens, mosses) |
| **Biodiversity** | The variety of life at all levels: genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity |
| **Species richness** | The total number of different species in a community |
| **Species evenness** | How equally individuals are distributed among species in a community |
| **Invasive species** | A non-native species that causes ecological or economic harm in its new environment |
| **Eutrophication** | Nutrient enrichment (typically nitrogen or phosphorus) of a body of water, leading to algal blooms and oxygen depletion |
| **Biomass** | The total mass of living organisms at a given trophic level or in a given area |
| **Nitrogen fixation** | The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into ammonia (NH3) by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium in legume root nodules) |
| **Denitrification** | The conversion of nitrates (NO3-) back to nitrogen gas (N2) by anaerobic bacteria; returns nitrogen to the atmosphere |
| **Carbon fixation** | The incorporation of inorganic carbon (CO2) into organic molecules during photosynthesis |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Energy is NOT recycled in ecosystems; matter IS**: Energy flows in ONE direction (sun to producers to consumers) and is lost as heat at each trophic level. Matter (carbon, nitrogen, water, phosphorus) is recycled through biogeochemical cycles.

2. **The 10% rule is an APPROXIMATION**: Approximately 10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels, but the actual percentage varies by ecosystem and trophic interaction. Do not state it as an exact value.

3. **Decomposers are NOT a single trophic level**: Decomposers operate across ALL trophic levels by breaking down dead organisms and waste from any level. They are essential for nutrient recycling.

4. **Carrying capacity is NOT fixed**: K can change over time due to changes in resource availability, climate, disease, or other environmental factors. It is a dynamic property of the environment.

5. **r-selected and K-selected are a CONTINUUM, not a strict dichotomy**: Most species fall somewhere between the two extremes. These are general tendencies, not absolute categories.

6. **Commensalism is difficult to prove**: Many relationships initially classified as commensalism may actually involve subtle costs or benefits to the "unaffected" species upon closer investigation.

7. **Keystone species are NOT always top predators**: While some keystone species are predators (e.g., sea stars, wolves), others can be ecosystem engineers (e.g., beavers), mutualists (e.g., fig trees, pollinators), or even pathogens.

8. **Invasive species are NOT just any non-native species**: A species is invasive only if it causes ecological or economic harm. Many non-native species coexist with native species without causing significant disruption.

9. **Climate change and global warming are NOT identical terms**: Climate change encompasses all changes in climate patterns (temperature, precipitation, wind, sea level), not just warming. The current trend is predominantly warming due to greenhouse gas emissions.

10. **Succession does NOT always reach a stable "climax community"**: The concept of a fixed climax community is outdated. Many ecosystems exist in a state of dynamic equilibrium, constantly being disturbed and recovering. Disturbance is a natural part of ecosystem dynamics.

11. **Nitrogen fixation is done by BACTERIA, not by plants**: Legumes do not fix nitrogen themselves; they harbor nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium) in root nodules. The bacteria fix nitrogen; the plant provides carbohydrates in a mutualistic relationship.

12. **Phosphorus has NO significant atmospheric phase**: Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus cycles through rocks, soil, and water, not through the atmosphere. This makes it a slow cycle and a common limiting nutrient.

13. **Individuals do NOT adapt to their environment during their lifetime in an evolutionary sense**: Individuals can acclimate (physiological adjustment), but adaptation is a population-level process occurring over generations through natural selection.
