# Unit 8: Ecology

> AP Exam Weight: 10-15% | ~12-14 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: ENE, EVO, SYI, IST

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 8.1: Responses to the Environment

**Learning Objective ENE-3.D**: Explain how the behavioral and/or physiological response of an organism is related to changes in internal or external environment.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- ENE-3.D.1: Organisms respond to changes in their environment through behavioral and physiological mechanisms.
  - **Taxis**: Directed movement toward (positive) or away from (negative) a stimulus (e.g., phototaxis toward light, chemotaxis toward chemicals).
  - **Kinesis**: Non-directional change in activity rate or turning frequency in response to stimulus intensity (e.g., woodlice increase turning rate in dry areas).
  - **Photoperiodism**: Response to changes in day length that regulates flowering in plants, seasonal breeding in animals, and migration timing.
  - **Phototropism**: Directional growth of plants toward or away from light, mediated by auxin redistribution.
  - **Nocturnal/diurnal activity**: Behavioral patterns linked to light-dark cycles.
  - **Fight-or-flight response**: Physiological response to perceived threats involving epinephrine and the sympathetic nervous system.
- ENE-3.D.2: Organisms exchange information with one another in response to internal changes and external cues, which can change behavior.
  - Predator warning signals (e.g., alarm calls in prairie dogs, warning coloration)
  - Plant responses to herbivory (e.g., release of volatile chemicals that attract predators of herbivores, production of defensive compounds)
  - Pheromone signaling (e.g., ants marking trails, moths attracting mates)

**Learning Objective IST-5.A**: Explain how the behavioral responses of organisms affect their overall fitness and may contribute to the success of the population.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- IST-5.A.1: Individuals can act on information and communicate it to others, affecting overall fitness and population success.
  - Foraging behaviors that maximize energy intake
  - Cooperative behaviors (e.g., alarm calls, group hunting, reciprocal altruism)
  - Communication through visual, auditory, chemical, and tactile signals

### Topic 8.2: Energy Flow Through Ecosystems

**Learning Objective ENE-1.M**: Describe the strategies organisms use to acquire and use energy.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- ENE-1.M.1: **Autotrophs** capture energy from physical or chemical sources in the environment.
  - **Photosynthetic organisms** capture energy present in sunlight (plants, algae, cyanobacteria).
  - **Chemosynthetic organisms** capture energy from small inorganic molecules (e.g., hydrogen sulfide, ammonia) present in their environment; can occur in the absence of oxygen (e.g., deep-sea hydrothermal vents).
- ENE-1.M.2: **Heterotrophs** capture energy present in carbon compounds produced by other organisms.
  - Primary consumers (herbivores) feed on producers.
  - Secondary consumers feed on primary consumers.
  - Tertiary consumers feed on secondary consumers.
  - **Decomposers/detritivores** break down dead organic matter and recycle nutrients.

**Learning Objective ENE-1.N**: Explain how changes in energy availability affect populations and ecosystems.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- ENE-1.N.1: A change in energy resources (such as sunlight) can affect the number and size of the trophic levels.
- ENE-1.N.2: A change in the producer level can affect the number and size of other trophic levels.
- ENE-1.N.3: Changes in energy availability can result in disruptions to an ecosystem (e.g., drought reducing plant productivity cascades through all consumer levels).

**Learning Objective ENE-1.O**: Explain how the activities of autotrophs and heterotrophs enable the flow of energy within an ecosystem.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- ENE-1.O.1: Energy flows through ecosystems in one direction: from the sun to producers to consumers to decomposers. Energy is NOT recycled.
- ENE-1.O.2: **Trophic levels** represent feeding positions in a food chain or food web.
- ENE-1.O.3: **10% rule**: On average, only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next trophic level. The rest is lost as heat through cellular respiration.
- ENE-1.O.4: **Gross primary productivity (GPP)**: The total amount of energy captured by producers through photosynthesis per unit time.
- ENE-1.O.5: **Net primary productivity (NPP)**: GPP minus the energy used by producers for their own cellular respiration. NPP = GPP - R. NPP represents the energy available to consumers.
- ENE-1.O.6: **Food chains** show linear energy transfer; **food webs** show the interconnected feeding relationships in an ecosystem (more realistic representation).
- ENE-1.O.7: **Ecological pyramids**:
  - Pyramid of energy: always upright; each level has less energy.
  - Pyramid of biomass: usually upright (except in some aquatic ecosystems where phytoplankton turn over rapidly).
  - Pyramid of numbers: can be inverted (e.g., one tree supporting many insects).
- ENE-1.O.8: **Biogeochemical cycles**: Matter (unlike energy) IS recycled through ecosystems.
  - **Carbon cycle**: CO2 fixed by photosynthesis into organic compounds; released by respiration, combustion, decomposition.
  - **Nitrogen cycle**: N2 fixed to NH3 (nitrogen fixation by bacteria) then to NO2-/NO3- (nitrification); assimilated by plants; decomposition releases NH3 (ammonification); denitrification returns N2 to atmosphere.
  - **Water cycle**: Evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff.
  - **Phosphorus cycle**: No atmospheric component; phosphorus cycles through rocks, soil, water, and organisms.

### Topic 8.3: Population Ecology

**Learning Objective SYI-1.G**: Describe factors that influence growth dynamics of populations.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- SYI-1.G.1: **Population**: A group of individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time.
- SYI-1.G.2: Population characteristics: size (N), density, dispersion pattern (clumped, uniform, random), age structure.
- SYI-1.G.3: **Exponential growth**: Population grows without resource limitation; J-shaped curve. Equation: **dN/dt = r_max * N** (where r_max = maximum per capita rate of increase, N = population size).
- SYI-1.G.4: Exponential growth occurs when resources are abundant and there are no constraints on growth (e.g., bacteria in fresh medium, invasive species in a new habitat).
- SYI-1.G.5: **r-selected species**: High reproductive rate, short life span, little parental care, thrive in unstable environments (e.g., bacteria, insects, dandelions).
- SYI-1.G.6: **K-selected species**: Low reproductive rate, long life span, extensive parental care, thrive in stable environments at or near carrying capacity (e.g., elephants, whales, humans).

### Topic 8.4: Effect of Density of Populations

**Learning Objective SYI-1.H**: Explain how the density of a population affects and is determined by resource availability in the environment.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- SYI-1.H.1: A population can produce a density of individuals that exceeds the system's resource availability.
- SYI-1.H.2: As limits to growth due to density-dependent and density-independent factors are imposed, a **logistic growth model** generally ensues.
- SYI-1.H.3: **Logistic growth**: Population growth slows as it approaches carrying capacity; S-shaped (sigmoid) curve. Equation: **dN/dt = r_max * N * (K-N)/K** (where K = carrying capacity).
- SYI-1.H.4: **Carrying capacity (K)**: The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely given the available resources. K is NOT fixed and can change over time.
- SYI-1.H.5: **Density-dependent factors**: Factors whose limiting effects intensify as population density increases.
  - Competition (intraspecific and interspecific)
  - Predation
  - Disease and parasitism
  - Accumulation of waste products
- SYI-1.H.6: **Density-independent factors**: Factors that affect population size regardless of density.
  - Natural disasters (floods, fires, hurricanes)
  - Weather and climate events
  - Human activities (habitat destruction, pollution)

### Topic 8.5: Community Ecology

**Learning Objective ENE-4.A**: Describe the structure of a community according to its species composition and diversity.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- ENE-4.A.1: A **community** is all the populations of different species living and interacting in the same area.
- ENE-4.A.2: Community structure is described by **species composition** (which species are present) and **species diversity** (richness + evenness).
- ENE-4.A.3: **Species interactions** shape community structure:
  - **Competition** (-/-): Both species are harmed. Leads to competitive exclusion or resource partitioning.
  - **Predation** (+/-): One species (predator) benefits; the other (prey) is harmed. Drives coevolution of defense mechanisms and hunting strategies.
  - **Herbivory** (+/-): An animal feeds on a plant or alga.
  - **Mutualism** (+/+): Both species benefit (e.g., mycorrhizae, nitrogen-fixing bacteria and legumes, cleaner fish and larger fish).
  - **Commensalism** (+/0): One species benefits; the other is unaffected (e.g., barnacles on whales, epiphytes on trees).
  - **Parasitism** (+/-): One species (parasite) benefits; the other (host) is harmed (e.g., tapeworms, ticks, mistletoe).
- ENE-4.A.4: **Competitive exclusion principle (Gause's principle)**: Two species competing for the same limited resource cannot coexist indefinitely; one will outcompete the other.
- ENE-4.A.5: **Niche**: The total set of biotic and abiotic conditions in which a species lives and reproduces.
  - **Fundamental niche**: The full range of conditions a species could occupy in the absence of competition.
  - **Realized niche**: The actual conditions a species occupies, reduced by competition and other interactions.
- ENE-4.A.6: **Resource partitioning (niche partitioning)**: The differentiation of niches that enables similar species to coexist by reducing competition (e.g., warblers feeding at different heights in spruce trees).
- ENE-4.A.7: **Character displacement**: Traits diverge where two similar species coexist, reducing competition (e.g., Darwin's finches with different beak sizes on shared islands).
- ENE-4.A.8: **Keystone species**: A species whose influence on community structure is disproportionately large relative to its abundance (e.g., sea otters controlling sea urchins in kelp forests, wolves in Yellowstone).
- ENE-4.A.9: **Trophic cascades**: Indirect effects transmitted through food webs when a keystone predator is removed or added (e.g., removing wolves leads to elk overgrazing, which reduces riparian vegetation).
- ENE-4.A.10: **Ecological succession**:
  - **Primary succession**: Colonization of a newly formed surface with no existing soil (e.g., after volcanic eruption, glacial retreat). Pioneer species (lichens, mosses) are first colonizers.
  - **Secondary succession**: Recolonization of an area where soil remains after a disturbance (e.g., after fire, farming, logging). Faster than primary succession.

### Topic 8.6: Biodiversity

**Learning Objective EVO-3.C**: Explain how the amount of biodiversity within an ecosystem is affected by a variety of factors.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- EVO-3.C.1: **Biodiversity** encompasses:
  - **Genetic diversity**: Variation in alleles within and between populations.
  - **Species diversity**: The number and relative abundance of species in an area.
  - **Ecosystem diversity**: The variety of habitats, communities, and ecological processes.
- EVO-3.C.2: **Species richness**: The total number of different species in a community.
- EVO-3.C.3: **Species evenness**: The relative abundance of each species in a community. Higher evenness (more equal distribution) indicates higher diversity.
- EVO-3.C.4: Greater biodiversity tends to increase ecosystem stability and resilience. Ecosystems with more species have greater functional redundancy, so if one species is lost, others can fulfill similar ecological roles.
- EVO-3.C.5: Biodiversity provides critical **ecosystem services**: pollination, water purification, nutrient cycling, food production, medicine, carbon sequestration, and climate regulation.
- EVO-3.C.6: Diverse ecosystems are more resistant to invasive species and better able to recover from disturbances.

### Topic 8.7: Disruptions to Ecosystems

**Learning Objective ENE-4.B**: Describe the effects of disruptions on ecosystems.

**Essential Knowledge:**
- ENE-4.B.1: Ecosystems are dynamic and can be disrupted by both natural and human-caused events.
- ENE-4.B.2: **Invasive species**: Non-native species introduced (intentionally or accidentally) that outcompete native species for resources.
  - Often lack natural predators or competitors in new environment, leading to uncontrolled population growth.
  - Examples: kudzu, cane toads, zebra mussels, Asian carp, European starlings.
- ENE-4.B.3: **Human impacts on ecosystems**:
  - **Habitat destruction and fragmentation**: The leading cause of species extinction today. Reduces population sizes, limits gene flow, increases edge effects.
  - **Overexploitation**: Overhunting, overfishing, and overharvesting of natural resources.
  - **Pollution**: Chemical contamination of air, water, and soil.
  - **Climate change**: Rising global temperatures due to increased greenhouse gases (CO2, methane) from burning fossil fuels and deforestation. Effects include rising sea levels, altered precipitation, shifting species ranges, coral bleaching, and ocean acidification.
- ENE-4.B.4: **Biological magnification (biomagnification)**: Persistent toxins become more concentrated at successively higher trophic levels (e.g., DDT, mercury, PCBs).
- ENE-4.B.5: **Eutrophication**: Excess nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus) from fertilizer runoff cause algal blooms, which lead to oxygen depletion (hypoxia/dead zones) as algae die and decompose.
- ENE-4.B.6: **Geological and meteorological events** affect habitat change and ecosystem distribution. Natural disasters, volcanic eruptions, and climate shifts have shaped Earth's ecosystems throughout history, creating new habitats, destroying existing ones, and driving ecological change.
- ENE-4.B.7: Human activities have accelerated the rate of extinction to 100-1000 times the background rate, constituting a mass extinction event.
- ENE-4.B.8: Ecosystem resilience depends on biodiversity; ecosystems with higher biodiversity recover more quickly from disturbances.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Ecology** | The scientific study of interactions between organisms and their environment, including both biotic and abiotic factors |
| **Biotic factors** | Living components of an ecosystem (organisms, their interactions, and organic matter) |
| **Abiotic factors** | Nonliving components of an ecosystem (temperature, water, sunlight, soil, wind, nutrients) |
| **Population** | A group of individuals of the same species living in the same geographic area at the same time |
| **Community** | All the populations of different species living and interacting in the same area |
| **Ecosystem** | A community of organisms together with their abiotic environment |
| **Biome** | A large-scale region characterized by distinct climate, vegetation, and animal life (e.g., tropical rainforest, tundra, desert) |
| **Biosphere** | The global ecosystem; the sum of all ecosystems on Earth where life exists |
| **Taxis** | Directed movement toward (positive) or away from (negative) a stimulus |
| **Kinesis** | Non-directional change in activity rate or turning frequency in response to stimulus intensity |
| **Photoperiodism** | An organism's physiological response to the relative lengths of day and night (photoperiod) |
| **Carrying capacity (K)** | The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely given available resources |
| **Exponential growth** | Population growth in an unlimited environment; J-shaped curve; dN/dt = r_max * N |
| **Logistic growth** | Population growth that slows as the population approaches carrying capacity; S-shaped curve; dN/dt = r_max * N * (K-N)/K |
| **Density-dependent factor** | A factor whose limiting effect increases as population density increases (e.g., competition, disease, predation) |
| **Density-independent factor** | A factor that affects population size regardless of population density (e.g., weather, natural disasters) |
| **r-selected species** | Species characterized by high reproductive rate, rapid development, and small body size; thrive in unstable environments |
| **K-selected species** | Species characterized by low reproductive rate, slow development, and large body size; thrive in stable environments near carrying capacity |
| **Trophic level** | A feeding position in a food chain or food web (producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, etc.) |
| **Producer (autotroph)** | An organism that produces its own food from inorganic molecules using light energy (photosynthesis) or chemical energy (chemosynthesis) |
| **Consumer (heterotroph)** | An organism that obtains energy by feeding on other organisms |
| **Decomposer** | An organism that breaks down dead organic matter and returns nutrients to the environment (e.g., bacteria, fungi) |
| **Gross primary productivity (GPP)** | The total amount of energy fixed by photosynthesis in an ecosystem per unit time |
| **Net primary productivity (NPP)** | GPP minus the energy consumed by producers in cellular respiration; the energy available to consumers; NPP = GPP - R |
| **Biomagnification** | The progressive increase in concentration of a persistent toxin at successively higher trophic levels |
| **Keystone species** | A species whose influence on community structure is disproportionately large relative to its abundance |
| **Trophic cascade** | Indirect effects on lower trophic levels caused by the addition or removal of a top predator |
| **Competitive exclusion** | The principle that two species competing for identical resources cannot coexist indefinitely; one will be eliminated |
| **Resource partitioning** | The differentiation of niches that enables similar species to coexist by reducing competition |
| **Character displacement** | The divergence of traits in two similar species where they coexist, reducing interspecific competition |
| **Niche** | The sum of all biotic and abiotic conditions required for an organism to survive, grow, and reproduce |
| **Fundamental niche** | The full range of conditions and resources a species could potentially use in the absence of competition |
| **Realized niche** | The actual range of conditions and resources a species uses, limited by competition and other interactions |
| **Mutualism** | A symbiotic interaction in which both species benefit (+/+) |
| **Commensalism** | A symbiotic interaction in which one species benefits and the other is unaffected (+/0) |
| **Parasitism** | A symbiotic interaction in which one species (parasite) benefits at the expense of the other (host) (+/-) |
| **Primary succession** | Ecological succession on a newly formed surface where no soil exists; begins with pioneer species |
| **Secondary succession** | Ecological succession on a disturbed surface where soil remains; faster than primary succession |
| **Pioneer species** | The first species to colonize a new or disturbed area during ecological succession (e.g., lichens, mosses) |
| **Biodiversity** | The variety of life at all levels: genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity |
| **Species richness** | The total number of different species in a community |
| **Species evenness** | How equally individuals are distributed among species in a community |
| **Invasive species** | A non-native species that causes ecological or economic harm in its new environment |
| **Eutrophication** | Nutrient enrichment (typically nitrogen or phosphorus) of a body of water, leading to algal blooms and oxygen depletion |
| **Biomass** | The total mass of living organisms at a given trophic level or in a given area |
| **Nitrogen fixation** | The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into ammonia (NH3) by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium in legume root nodules) |
| **Denitrification** | The conversion of nitrates (NO3-) back to nitrogen gas (N2) by anaerobic bacteria; returns nitrogen to the atmosphere |
| **Carbon fixation** | The incorporation of inorganic carbon (CO2) into organic molecules during photosynthesis |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Energy is NOT recycled in ecosystems; matter IS**: Energy flows in ONE direction (sun to producers to consumers) and is lost as heat at each trophic level. Matter (carbon, nitrogen, water, phosphorus) is recycled through biogeochemical cycles.

2. **The 10% rule is an APPROXIMATION**: Approximately 10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels, but the actual percentage varies by ecosystem and trophic interaction. Do not state it as an exact value.

3. **Decomposers are NOT a single trophic level**: Decomposers operate across ALL trophic levels by breaking down dead organisms and waste from any level. They are essential for nutrient recycling.

4. **Carrying capacity is NOT fixed**: K can change over time due to changes in resource availability, climate, disease, or other environmental factors. It is a dynamic property of the environment.

5. **r-selected and K-selected are a CONTINUUM, not a strict dichotomy**: Most species fall somewhere between the two extremes. These are general tendencies, not absolute categories.

6. **Commensalism is difficult to prove**: Many relationships initially classified as commensalism may actually involve subtle costs or benefits to the "unaffected" species upon closer investigation.

7. **Keystone species are NOT always top predators**: While some keystone species are predators (e.g., sea stars, wolves), others can be ecosystem engineers (e.g., beavers), mutualists (e.g., fig trees, pollinators), or even pathogens.

8. **Invasive species are NOT just any non-native species**: A species is invasive only if it causes ecological or economic harm. Many non-native species coexist with native species without causing significant disruption.

9. **Climate change and global warming are NOT identical terms**: Climate change encompasses all changes in climate patterns (temperature, precipitation, wind, sea level), not just warming. The current trend is predominantly warming due to greenhouse gas emissions.

10. **Succession does NOT always reach a stable "climax community"**: The concept of a fixed climax community is outdated. Many ecosystems exist in a state of dynamic equilibrium, constantly being disturbed and recovering. Disturbance is a natural part of ecosystem dynamics.

11. **Nitrogen fixation is done by BACTERIA, not by plants**: Legumes do not fix nitrogen themselves; they harbor nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium) in root nodules. The bacteria fix nitrogen; the plant provides carbohydrates in a mutualistic relationship.

12. **Phosphorus has NO significant atmospheric phase**: Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus cycles through rocks, soil, and water, not through the atmosphere. This makes it a slow cycle and a common limiting nutrient.

13. **Individuals do NOT adapt to their environment during their lifetime in an evolutionary sense**: Individuals can acclimate (physiological adjustment), but adaptation is a population-level process occurring over generations through natural selection.
