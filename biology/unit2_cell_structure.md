# Unit 2: Cells

> AP Exam Weight: 10-13% | ~14-16 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: 1 Evolution · 2 Energetics · 3 Information Storage & Transmission · 4 Systems Interactions

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 2.1: Cell Structure and Function

**Key Learning Goals**: Identify the structural features and roles of subcellular components and organelles found in prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.

**Core Concepts:**
- All living organisms are composed of cells, which are the basic units of life.
- Ribosomes are found in all living organisms and serve as the site of protein synthesis.
- Eukaryotic cells contain membrane-bound organelles, including but not limited to: endoplasmic reticulum (rough and smooth), Golgi apparatus, mitochondria, lysosomes, vacuoles, and in plant cells, chloroplasts and a central vacuole.

#### Subcellular Components and Cell Function (Fall 2025: folded into Topic 2.1)

**Key Learning Goals**: Analyze how the various subcellular components and organelles act in concert to carry out the essential activities of a cell.

**Core Concepts:**
- Organelles and subcellular structures work together to carry out cell functions such as protein synthesis, energy transformation, and transport.
- Endomembrane system: nuclear envelope, rough ER, smooth ER, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, vesicles, and plasma membrane are interconnected in function.

### Topic 2.2: Cell Size

**Key Learning Goals**: Account for the constraints that surface area-to-volume ratio places on cell size and identify adaptations that help cells overcome these limitations.

**Core Concepts:**
- As a cell increases in size, its volume increases faster than its surface area, decreasing the surface area-to-volume ratio.
- A high surface area-to-volume ratio is necessary for efficient exchange of materials (nutrients, wastes, gases) with the environment.
- This constraint limits cell size and explains why cells are small or have adaptations that increase surface area (e.g., microvilli, folded membranes).

### Topic 2.3: Plasma Membrane

**Key Learning Goals**: Outline the components and organization of the plasma membrane as described by the Fluid Mosaic Model.

**Core Concepts:**
- Cell membranes are composed of a phospholipid bilayer with embedded and peripheral proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates (glycoproteins and glycolipids).
- The phospholipid bilayer creates a selectively permeable barrier: hydrophilic heads face outward, hydrophobic tails face inward.
- Membrane proteins have diverse functions: transport (channel and carrier proteins), enzymatic activity, signal transduction (receptors), cell-cell recognition, intercellular joining, and attachment to the cytoskeleton.

### Topic 2.4: Membrane Permeability

**Key Learning Goals**: Connect the molecular architecture of the plasma membrane to its role as a selective barrier that regulates what enters and exits the cell.

**Core Concepts:**
- Small, nonpolar molecules (O2, CO2, N2) can pass directly through the phospholipid bilayer.
- Large, polar molecules and ions cannot pass through the hydrophobic core without assistance from membrane transport proteins.
- Selective permeability allows cells to maintain distinct internal environments.

### Topic 2.5: Membrane Transport

**Key Learning Goals**: Explain how concentration gradients and membrane permeability together determine the direction and rate of molecular movement across a membrane.

**Core Concepts:**
- Passive transport moves molecules down their concentration gradient without energy input.
- Diffusion is the net movement of molecules from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration.
- Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration.

### Topic 2.6: Facilitated Diffusion

**Key Learning Goals**: Distinguish the roles of channel proteins and carrier proteins in enabling the passage of specific molecules across the membrane without an energy cost.

**Core Concepts:**
- Channel proteins provide hydrophilic passageways for specific molecules and ions.
- Carrier proteins change shape to transport specific molecules across the membrane.
- Aquaporins are channel proteins that facilitate the rapid transport of water.
- Facilitated diffusion is still passive transport (no ATP required); it just requires a protein to assist.

### Topic 2.7: Tonicity and Osmoregulation

**Key Learning Goals**: Predict the direction of water movement across a membrane given the relative solute concentrations inside and outside a cell, and describe the resulting effects on cell shape.

**Core Concepts:**
- Tonicity describes the relative solute concentration of the external solution compared to the cell:
  - **Hypertonic**: higher solute outside; water flows out; cell shrivels (crenation in animal cells, plasmolysis in plant cells)
  - **Hypotonic**: lower solute outside; water flows in; cell swells (lysis in animal cells, turgid in plant cells)
  - **Isotonic**: equal solute; no net water movement; cell maintains its shape
- Organisms have mechanisms for osmoregulation (e.g., contractile vacuoles in protists, kidneys in animals).

### Topic 2.8: Mechanisms of Transport

**Key Learning Goals**: Compare passive and active transport mechanisms, and describe how cells move large particles across the plasma membrane via vesicle-mediated processes.

**Core Concepts:**
- Active transport uses ATP to move molecules against their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration).
- The sodium-potassium pump (Na+/K+ ATPase) is an example of primary active transport.
- Endocytosis brings large molecules into the cell via membrane-bound vesicles (phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated endocytosis).
- Exocytosis expels large molecules from the cell by fusing vesicles with the plasma membrane.
- Bulk transport (endocytosis and exocytosis) requires energy.

### Topic 2.9: Cell Compartmentalization

**Key Learning Goals**: Explain how the system of internal membranes in eukaryotic cells enables distinct biochemical environments and parallel metabolic processes within a single cell.

**Core Concepts:**
- Internal membranes allow eukaryotic cells to carry out different metabolic reactions simultaneously in separate compartments.
- Compartmentalization allows for the maintenance of different chemical environments (pH, ion concentrations) within the same cell.

### Topic 2.10: Origins of Cell Compartmentalization

**Key Learning Goals**: Evaluate the evidence supporting the endosymbiotic origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts by comparing these organelles to their proposed free-living prokaryotic ancestors.

**Core Concepts:**
- The endosymbiotic theory proposes that mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living prokaryotes that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells.
- Evidence for endosymbiosis:
  - Mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own circular DNA (similar to bacterial DNA)
  - They have double membranes (inner membrane from the original prokaryote, outer from the host's vesicle)
  - They have their own ribosomes (70S, similar to bacterial ribosomes, not 80S like eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes)
  - They replicate independently by binary fission
  - They are similar in size to bacteria

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Prokaryotic cell** | A cell lacking a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; includes bacteria and archaea |
| **Eukaryotic cell** | A cell containing a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; includes protists, fungi, plants, and animals |
| **Nucleus** | Membrane-bound organelle containing the cell's DNA; bounded by a double membrane (nuclear envelope) with nuclear pores |
| **Ribosome** | Non-membrane-bound structure (composed of rRNA and protein) that is the site of protein synthesis; 80S in eukaryotic cytoplasm, 70S in prokaryotes, mitochondria, and chloroplasts |
| **Rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER)** | ER studded with ribosomes; synthesizes and processes secretory proteins and membrane proteins |
| **Smooth endoplasmic reticulum (SER)** | ER lacking ribosomes; functions in lipid synthesis, detoxification, and calcium storage |
| **Golgi apparatus** | Stack of flattened membrane sacs (cisternae) that modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for transport |
| **Lysosome** | Membrane-bound organelle containing hydrolytic (digestive) enzymes at acidic pH (~5); found in animal cells |
| **Mitochondrion** | Double-membrane organelle that is the site of aerobic cellular respiration (ATP production); has its own DNA and ribosomes |
| **Chloroplast** | Double-membrane organelle in plants and algae that is the site of photosynthesis; contains thylakoids and stroma; has its own DNA and ribosomes |
| **Central vacuole** | Large membrane-bound sac in plant cells; stores water, ions, and metabolites; maintains turgor pressure |
| **Cell wall** | Rigid structure external to the plasma membrane in plants (cellulose), fungi (chitin), and bacteria (peptidoglycan); provides structural support |
| **Plasma membrane** | The phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins that forms the boundary of all cells |
| **Fluid Mosaic Model** | Model describing the structure of the plasma membrane as a fluid phospholipid bilayer with a mosaic of embedded proteins that can move laterally |
| **Selective permeability** | The property of a membrane that allows some substances to cross more easily than others |
| **Diffusion** | Net movement of molecules from higher concentration to lower concentration, driven by random thermal motion |
| **Osmosis** | The diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration |
| **Active transport** | Movement of molecules across a membrane against their concentration gradient, requiring energy (ATP) |
| **Passive transport** | Movement of molecules across a membrane down their concentration gradient, requiring no energy input |
| **Endocytosis** | The process of taking in material by forming a vesicle from the plasma membrane |
| **Exocytosis** | The process of expelling material from the cell by fusing a vesicle with the plasma membrane |
| **Turgor pressure** | The pressure exerted by water inside a plant cell against the cell wall; maintains cell rigidity |
| **Plasmolysis** | The shrinking of a plant cell's cytoplasm away from the cell wall due to water loss in a hypertonic environment |
| **Crenation** | The shrinking/shriveling of an animal cell due to water loss in a hypertonic environment |
| **Lysis (cytolysis)** | The bursting of an animal cell due to excessive water uptake in a hypotonic environment |
| **Endosymbiotic theory** | The theory that mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as free-living prokaryotes that were engulfed by and became endosymbionts within ancestral eukaryotic cells |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Prokaryotes DO have DNA**: Prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus, but they still contain DNA. Their DNA is typically a single circular chromosome in the nucleoid region, plus plasmids.

2. **Not all cells have the same organelles**: Animal cells lack chloroplasts, cell walls, and large central vacuoles. Plant cells lack centrioles (in most species) and lysosomes. Be specific about which cell type you are describing.

3. **Osmosis is about WATER movement, not solute movement**: Water moves from low solute (high water) concentration to high solute (low water) concentration. Do NOT say water moves from "high concentration to low concentration" without specifying concentration OF WHAT.

4. **Facilitated diffusion is PASSIVE, not active**: Even though facilitated diffusion uses transport proteins, it does NOT require ATP because molecules still move down their concentration gradient.

5. **70S vs. 80S ribosomes**: Prokaryotes have 70S ribosomes. Eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes are 80S. Mitochondrial and chloroplast ribosomes are 70S (evidence for endosymbiosis). The "S" stands for Svedberg units (a measure of sedimentation rate, NOT a simple additive of subunit sizes).

6. **The cell membrane is NOT a static structure**: The Fluid Mosaic Model emphasizes that phospholipids and proteins can move laterally within the membrane. It is dynamic, not rigid.

7. **Hypertonic/Hypotonic confusion**: These terms describe the SOLUTION, not the cell. A hypertonic solution has MORE solute (less water) than the cell, causing the cell to lose water.

8. **Surface area-to-volume ratio**: As a cell grows, volume increases as the cube of the radius, but surface area only increases as the square. This is why large cells are inefficient, not because they "use more energy."
