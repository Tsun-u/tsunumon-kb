# Unit 3: Cellular Energetics

> AP Exam Weight: 12-16% | ~12-14 Class Periods
> Big Ideas: 2 Energetics · 4 Systems Interactions

## Topics and Learning Objectives

### Topic 3.1: Enzymes

**Key Learning Goals**: Analyze the structural and functional properties that define enzymes as biological catalysts.

**Core Concepts:**
- Enzymes are biological catalysts that accelerate chemical reactions without being used up in the process.
- Enzymes are predominantly proteins, though certain RNA molecules known as ribozymes can also function as catalysts.
- Each enzyme has an active site — a structurally specific region that binds the substrate molecule.
- The induced fit model accounts for enzyme-substrate specificity: the enzyme's active site undergoes a slight conformational change upon substrate binding to optimize the interaction, making the older lock-and-key model an oversimplification.

#### Enzyme Catalysis (Fall 2025: folded into Topic 3.1)

**Key Learning Goals**: Examine how enzymes influence the rate of biochemical reactions and identify factors that alter their effectiveness.

**Core Concepts:**
- Enzymes reduce the activation energy (Ea) required for a reaction, thereby increasing how quickly the reaction proceeds.
- Enzymes do NOT alter the free energy (delta G) of the reaction or shift the equilibrium position — they only accelerate the rate at which equilibrium is reached.
- Cofactors (inorganic helpers, e.g., metal ions) and coenzymes (organic helpers, e.g., vitamins, NAD+, FAD) are required by many enzymes for full catalytic function.
- Competitive inhibitors occupy the active site, preventing substrate access. Noncompetitive (allosteric) inhibitors attach to a separate regulatory site, altering the enzyme's three-dimensional shape and reducing catalytic efficiency.

### Topic 3.2: Environmental Impacts on Enzyme Function

**Key Learning Goals**: Outline how shifts in environmental conditions affect enzyme activity and stability.

**Core Concepts:**
- Every enzyme operates most efficiently within a specific range of temperature and pH, called its optimal conditions.
- Exposure to extreme temperatures or pH values can denature enzymes, unfolding their three-dimensional structure and permanently disabling catalytic function.
- Reaction rate increases with substrate concentration until all active sites are occupied and the maximum rate (Vmax) is reached — a state called enzyme saturation.
- Allosteric regulation involves activators or inhibitors binding to regulatory sites distinct from the active site, producing conformational changes that either enhance or reduce enzyme activity.
- Feedback inhibition occurs when the final product of a metabolic pathway inhibits an earlier enzyme in that pathway, allowing the cell to self-regulate output levels.

### Topic 3.3: Cellular Energy

**Key Learning Goals**: Outline the structure of ATP and analyze how energy coupling enables endergonic reactions to proceed within cells.

**Core Concepts:**
- ATP (adenosine triphosphate) serves as the primary energy currency that powers cellular work.
- ATP is composed of an adenine base, a ribose sugar, and three phosphate groups.
- Hydrolysis of ATP to ADP (adenosine diphosphate) and inorganic phosphate (Pi) releases free energy that can drive cellular processes.
- Energy coupling links exergonic reactions (energy-releasing, negative delta G) to endergonic reactions (energy-requiring, positive delta G) through the transfer of phosphate from ATP.
- The laws of thermodynamics govern biological energy transformations:
  - First law: Energy is neither created nor destroyed — it changes from one form to another.
  - Second law: Each energy transformation increases the total entropy of the universe.

### Topic 3.4: Photosynthesis

**Key Learning Goals**: Examine the two-stage mechanism of photosynthesis and trace the flow of energy and matter through each stage.

**Core Concepts:**
- Overall equation: 6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2
- Photosynthesis takes place in chloroplasts and is divided into two interconnected stages:

**Light-Dependent Reactions (thylakoid membranes):**
- Photosystems II and I (PS II and PS I) absorb light energy through chlorophyll and accessory pigments embedded in thylakoid membranes.
- Water molecules are split by photolysis: 2H2O → 4H+ + 4e- + O2. The released oxygen originates from water, NOT from CO2.
- Electrons released from water travel through the electron transport chain (ETC) from PS II to PS I; the energy released pumps H+ ions across the thylakoid membrane, generating a proton gradient used to synthesize ATP via chemiosmosis.
- NADP+ is reduced to NADPH at PS I.
- Outputs of the light reactions: ATP, NADPH, O2

**Light-Independent Reactions / Calvin Cycle (stroma):**
- The enzyme RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) catalyzes CO2 fixation, incorporating carbon into a 3-carbon compound, 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA).
- ATP and NADPH generated in the light reactions power the reduction of fixed carbon into G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate).
- G3P is the building block used to synthesize glucose and other organic compounds.
- Inputs: CO2, ATP, NADPH; Outputs: G3P, ADP, NADP+

### Topic 3.5: Cellular Respiration

**Key Learning Goals**: Trace the stepwise breakdown of glucose through cellular respiration and evaluate the ATP yield of each stage.

**Core Concepts:**
- Overall equation: C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + ATP (+ heat)
- Cellular respiration proceeds through three main stages:

**Glycolysis (cytoplasm):**
- Glucose (6C) is cleaved into two pyruvate molecules (3C each).
- Net yield: 2 ATP (via substrate-level phosphorylation) + 2 NADH
- Oxygen is not required — this stage is anaerobic.

**Krebs Cycle / Citric Acid Cycle (mitochondrial matrix):**
- Pyruvate is first converted to acetyl-CoA during pyruvate oxidation, releasing CO2 and producing NADH.
- Each turn of the cycle yields: 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 1 ATP (or GTP), and 2 CO2.
- Two turns of the cycle occur per glucose molecule.

**Oxidative Phosphorylation / Electron Transport Chain (inner mitochondrial membrane):**
- NADH and FADH2 transfer electrons to the ETC, where electrons move through protein complexes (I, II, III, IV).
- The energy released pumps H+ ions from the mitochondrial matrix to the intermembrane space, establishing a proton gradient that drives chemiosmosis.
- H+ ions flow back into the matrix through ATP synthase, powering ATP synthesis.
- Molecular oxygen serves as the terminal electron acceptor, combining with electrons and H+ to form water — which is why aerobic respiration requires oxygen.
- Yields approximately ~30-32 ATP per glucose molecule (the dominant stage of ATP production).

**Fermentation (cytoplasm):**
- When oxygen is absent, fermentation regenerates NAD+ from NADH so that glycolysis can continue producing ATP.
- Lactic acid fermentation: pyruvate → lactate (occurs in muscle cells and certain bacteria)
- Alcoholic fermentation: pyruvate → ethanol + CO2 (occurs in yeast and some bacteria)
- Fermentation generates only 2 ATP per glucose (from glycolysis alone).

## Key Terminology

| Term | Precise Definition |
|------|-------------------|
| **Enzyme** | A biological catalyst, usually a protein, that speeds up a chemical reaction by lowering the activation energy without being consumed |
| **Substrate** | The specific reactant molecule that an enzyme acts upon |
| **Active site** | The specific region of an enzyme where the substrate binds and the catalytic reaction occurs |
| **Activation energy (Ea)** | The minimum amount of energy required to initiate a chemical reaction |
| **Induced fit** | The model in which the enzyme's active site changes shape slightly upon substrate binding to achieve optimal fit |
| **Competitive inhibitor** | A molecule that competes with the substrate for binding to the enzyme's active site |
| **Noncompetitive inhibitor** | A molecule that binds to an allosteric site (not the active site), changing the enzyme's shape and reducing its activity |
| **Allosteric site** | A site on an enzyme other than the active site where regulatory molecules bind |
| **Cofactor** | An inorganic ion or molecule (e.g., Zn2+, Fe2+, Mg2+) required for enzyme activity |
| **Coenzyme** | An organic molecule (often derived from vitamins) that assists enzyme function (e.g., NAD+, FAD, coenzyme A) |
| **ATP (adenosine triphosphate)** | The primary energy currency molecule of cells; energy is released when the terminal phosphate bond is hydrolyzed |
| **ADP (adenosine diphosphate)** | The product of ATP hydrolysis; has two phosphate groups; recycled back to ATP |
| **NAD+ / NADH** | Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide; an electron carrier. NAD+ is the oxidized form; NADH is the reduced form (carries electrons) |
| **FADH2** | Flavin adenine dinucleotide (reduced form); an electron carrier that donates electrons to Complex II of the ETC |
| **NADP+ / NADPH** | Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate; an electron carrier used primarily in photosynthesis (anabolic reactions) |
| **Chemiosmosis** | The process by which ATP is synthesized using the energy of a hydrogen ion (H+) gradient across a membrane, via ATP synthase |
| **ATP synthase** | The enzyme complex embedded in membranes (thylakoid or inner mitochondrial) that synthesizes ATP as H+ ions flow through it down their concentration gradient |
| **Photosystem** | A cluster of chlorophyll and accessory pigment molecules in the thylakoid membrane that absorbs light energy; PS II (P680) and PS I (P700) |
| **Chlorophyll** | The primary photosynthetic pigment that absorbs red and blue light, reflecting green; chlorophyll a is the main pigment, chlorophyll b is an accessory pigment |
| **Calvin cycle** | The light-independent reactions of photosynthesis in the stroma; fixes CO2 into organic molecules using ATP and NADPH |
| **RuBisCO** | Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase; the enzyme that catalyzes the first step of carbon fixation in the Calvin cycle |
| **Glycolysis** | The metabolic pathway that splits glucose (6C) into two pyruvate (3C) molecules in the cytoplasm; produces 2 ATP and 2 NADH net |
| **Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle)** | The cyclic metabolic pathway in the mitochondrial matrix that oxidizes acetyl-CoA, producing CO2, NADH, FADH2, and ATP/GTP |
| **Electron transport chain (ETC)** | A series of protein complexes in the inner mitochondrial membrane (or thylakoid membrane) that transfer electrons and pump H+ to generate a proton gradient |
| **Oxidative phosphorylation** | The production of ATP using energy derived from the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis |
| **Substrate-level phosphorylation** | The direct transfer of a phosphate group from a substrate molecule to ADP, forming ATP (occurs in glycolysis and the Krebs cycle) |
| **Fermentation** | An anaerobic metabolic pathway that regenerates NAD+ from NADH, allowing glycolysis to continue without oxygen |
| **Aerobic** | Requiring oxygen |
| **Anaerobic** | Not requiring oxygen |
| **Exergonic reaction** | A reaction that releases free energy (negative delta G); spontaneous |
| **Endergonic reaction** | A reaction that absorbs free energy (positive delta G); nonspontaneous; requires energy input |
| **Entropy** | A measure of disorder or randomness in a system; increases in every energy transformation |

## Common Misconceptions to Avoid

1. **Enzymes do NOT provide energy**: Enzymes lower activation energy, making reactions proceed faster. They do NOT change the delta G (free energy change) or provide energy to the reaction.

2. **Enzymes are NOT consumed**: Enzymes are catalysts that are recycled after each reaction. They are not "used up."

3. **The oxygen in photosynthesis comes from WATER, not CO2**: The O2 released during photosynthesis is derived from the photolysis (splitting) of water molecules, not from carbon dioxide.

4. **The Calvin cycle is NOT the "dark reactions"**: The term "dark reactions" is misleading because the Calvin cycle does not require darkness — it simply does not directly use light. It occurs simultaneously with the light reactions during the day. Use "light-independent reactions" instead.

5. **Plants perform BOTH photosynthesis AND cellular respiration**: Plants are not only photosynthetic. They also perform cellular respiration 24 hours a day to produce ATP. Photosynthesis occurs only in the presence of light.

6. **Glycolysis occurs in ALL living cells**: Glycolysis is universal and occurs in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, in the cytoplasm. It does not require mitochondria.

7. **Fermentation does NOT produce ATP directly**: Fermentation itself produces no additional ATP. Its purpose is to regenerate NAD+ so glycolysis can continue producing ATP.

8. **ATP yield from cellular respiration is approximately 30-32, not exactly 36-38**: Older textbooks state 36-38 ATP per glucose, but current estimates are 30-32 due to the cost of transporting molecules across membranes.

9. **Competitive inhibition CAN be overcome by increasing substrate concentration**: Adding more substrate can outcompete the inhibitor for the active site. Noncompetitive inhibition CANNOT be overcome this way because the inhibitor binds elsewhere.

10. **Chemiosmosis occurs in BOTH photosynthesis and cellular respiration**: The mechanism of using a proton gradient to drive ATP synthase is shared between the two processes, just in different organelles and membranes (thylakoid membrane vs. inner mitochondrial membrane).
